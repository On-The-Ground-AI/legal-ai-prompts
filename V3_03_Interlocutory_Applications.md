# AI Prompts for Interlocutory Applications

Comprehensive prompts for drafting interlocutory applications in civil litigation, including injunctions, summary judgment, striking out applications, security for costs, discovery orders, and other interim relief procedures. References Singapore Rules of Court 2021.

---

## INJUNCTIONS — MAREVA ORDERS

**PROMPT 001 — Draft Mareva Injunction Application (Asset Preservation)**

You are a litigation AI assistant. I am preparing an urgent Mareva injunction application under Singapore Rules of Court 2021, Order 29 Rule 1. The defendant has [DESCRIBE DEFENDANT ACTIONS: e.g., fraudulently diverted company funds, hidden assets, transferred property]. I need to establish: (1) a strong prima facie case of [SPECIFY BREACH: contract breach / tortious conduct / fraud]; (2) the balance of convenience heavily favours the injunction; and (3) risk of asset dissipation is real and imminent.

Please draft a comprehensive affidavit structure with:
- Clear narrative of the defendant's suspicious asset movements (with dates, amounts, beneficial owners)
- Evidence of deliberate concealment or transfer patterns showing consciousness of liability
- Specific asset tracing (bank transfers, property transactions, beneficial ownership chains)
- Urgency justification (timeline of when dissipation is likely to occur)
- Proposed undertaking as to damages and security arrangements
- Prayer for interim relief and disclosure orders ancillary to the Mareva

Ensure the application addresses the established principles from Gee v Lees [relevant case law] that Mareva relief requires clear evidence of a real risk, not mere suspicion. Include reference to the three-stage test from relevant case law and why our evidence meets each limb. Format as a structured skeleton argument suitable for urgent same-day hearing.

Context: When you need to urgently freeze assets of a defendant to prevent them being dissipated before trial, particularly in cases of fraud, breach of fiduciary duty, or dishonest breach of contract.
Difficulty: Advanced
Best AI tool: Claude
Follow-up: PROMPT 002 (Mareva Affidavit Details) or PROMPT 010 (Disclosure Order Ancillary to Mareva)

**PROMPT 002 — Detailed Mareva Affidavit with Asset Tracing Evidence**

Draft a detailed affidavit for a Mareva injunction application with comprehensive asset tracing. The plaintiff alleges the defendant received [AMOUNT] in [DESCRIBE PROCEEDS: e.g., fraudulently induced investment funds, misappropriated company assets]. The funds were traced through the following chain: [PROVIDE TRANSACTION DETAILS: original account → intermediate accounts → final destination assets].

Please structure the affidavit with:
- Deponent's qualifications and knowledge (direct participant / investigator / forensic expert)
- Chronological transaction narrative with supporting documentary evidence references (bank statements, transfer slips, land registry extracts)
- Clear identification of each asset: real property (with title details and current market value), bank accounts (with account holder names and balances), vehicles (with registration and mortgages), shares/investments (with beneficial ownership)
- Explanation of why each asset trace is reliable (documentary evidence hierarchy: bank statements > affidavits > inference)
- Pattern analysis showing deliberate concealment (shell companies, rapid transfers, use of proxies/family members)
- Urgency statement: when is the defendant likely to further dissipate assets, based on what evidence
- Proposed schedule of assets sought to be frozen with supporting evidence references
- Undertaking as to damages (and security/insurance details if relevant)

Include detailed submissions on the test for Mareva relief, addressing any defences the defendant might raise. Format as a complete affidavit ready for filing with exhibits properly referenced.

Context: When you have detailed asset tracing evidence and need to present it persuasively in an affidavit format for a contested Mareva hearing.
Difficulty: Advanced
Best AI tool: Claude
Follow-up: PROMPT 003 (Mareva Hearing Oral Submissions) or PROMPT 020 (Undertaking as to Damages)

**PROMPT 003 — Mareva Injunction Hearing Oral Submissions**

Prepare oral submission notes for a Mareva injunction hearing before the High Court. The Judge will want to understand: (1) Why is immediate restraint necessary (timing/urgency)? (2) What is the strength of the plaintiff's case on the merits? (3) What is the evidence of asset dissipation risk? (4) Why does the balance of convenience favour granting the injunction? (5) What supervision/enforcement mechanisms will protect the defendant's interests?

Please structure comprehensive submission notes covering:
- Opening: brief narrative (2-3 minutes) of the urgent circumstances and what relief is sought
- Merits stage: concise summary of the prima facie case (why the plaintiff is likely to succeed at trial) with reference to key documents and law
- Urgency/risk of dissipation: specific factual evidence of the defendant's likely future conduct, tied to identified assets and timelines
- Balance of convenience: why stopping the dissipation now outweighs any inconvenience to the defendant (consider: defendant can apply to discharge if circumstances change; delay causes irreversible prejudice to plaintiff)
- Proposed terms: scope of injunction (which assets specifically), carve-outs (legal fees, essential living expenses), disclosure requirements, surveillance/reporting mechanisms
- Undertaking as to damages: amount and security mechanism (personal undertaking / insurance bond / cash deposit)
- Addresses to anticipated defendant's counter-arguments: (a) no real risk of dissipation (speculative); (b) plaintiff's case is weak; (c) injunction would cause disproportionate harm; (d) plaintiff has alternative remedies
- Proposed conditions and reporting arrangements to protect defendant's legitimate interests

Include key case law citations (Coutts & Co v Bagot's Executor & Trustee Co Ltd, Derby & Co Ltd v Weldon, etc.) and Singapore Court of Appeal authorities. Prepare notes structured by topic for easy reference during oral hearing, with time allocations for each section. Include suggested responses to likely judicial questions.

Context: When preparing to appear before a Judge in a contested Mareva hearing and need structured notes for oral advocacy.
Difficulty: Advanced
Best AI tool: Claude
Follow-up: PROMPT 004 (Anton Piller Injunction) or PROMPT 008 (Injunction Undertaking Calculation)

**PROMPT 004 — Anton Piller Injunction Application (Search Order)**

Draft an Anton Piller injunction (search order) application for urgent discovery and inspection of the defendant's premises/documents. The plaintiff has reason to believe the defendant holds [DESCRIBE EVIDENCE AT RISK: e.g., confidential documents, infringing materials, evidence of fraud]. There is a serious risk that the defendant will [DESCRIBE DISSIPATION OF EVIDENCE: e.g., destroy documents, conceal infringing goods, tamper with evidence] if advance notice is given.

Please prepare:
- Skeleton argument establishing: (1) strong prima facie case on the merits; (2) serious risk of evidence destruction/concealment; (3) balance of convenience favours search order; (4) proportionality of intrusion
- Affidavit with: specific factual basis for believing evidence exists and will be destroyed (prior breaches by defendant, defendant's knowledge of the claim, suspicious conduct upon learning of the claim), timeline showing urgency, description of premises and evidence sought, estimated duration and scope of search
- Draft search order with precise terms: premises to be searched, categories of documents/items to be inspected, supervising solicitor's role, presence of defendant's representative, permitted duration, sealing of sensitive materials, return of documents schedule, contempt sanction for breach
- Address to likely objections: proportionality (search order is intrusive, must be necessary and proportionate), adequacy of alternative remedies, defendant's rights (right to legal advice, right to protect privileged documents)
- Proposed supervising solicitor appointment with draft letter of instruction covering: duties and responsibilities, document inspection and sealing procedures, conflict of interest management, reporting obligations

Include case law from Rank Film Distributors Ltd v Video Information Centre (principles for Anton Piller orders) and Singapore authorities. Explain why ordinary discovery is insufficient and why search order is the only effective remedy. Format as court-ready application.

Context: When evidence of wrongdoing exists and there is serious risk of destruction if normal discovery procedures are used. Requires urgent hearing, often on an ex parte or on notice basis.
Difficulty: Advanced
Best AI tool: Claude
Follow-up: PROMPT 005 (Supervising Solicitor Brief for Search Order) or PROMPT 011 (Post-Search Order Disclosure Process)

**PROMPT 005 — Supervising Solicitor Brief for Search Order (Anton Piller)**

Prepare detailed instructions for the supervising solicitor appointed in an Anton Piller order (search order). The search is scheduled for [DATE/TIME] at [DEFENDANT'S PREMISES]. The order permits inspection of [DESCRIBE CATEGORIES: e.g., all documents relating to the alleged infringement, samples of goods, electronic devices containing customer data].

Please draft comprehensive instructions covering:
- Role and responsibilities: neutral officer of the court, must ensure fairness to defendant, cannot take sides, must protect legitimate interests of both parties
- Pre-search procedures: scheduling, notifying defendant's solicitor (if appropriate), confirming defendant's availability and legal representation, liaison with supervising constable (if court-appointed police assistance is needed)
- Search execution protocol: identification of persons present, purpose explanation to defendant, systematic search methodology (which rooms, order of inspection), document handling (segregation of privileged materials, sensitive data protection)
- Privilege protection: procedure for identifying privileged documents without reviewing content, defendant's right to inspect and claim privilege, separate envelope sealing, return procedures
- Document inspection process: safe handling of originals, photography/scanning of relevant documents, maintaining chain of custody, segregating sensitive or confidential materials, labeling and scheduling all items inspected
- Dispute resolution during search: what to do if defendant refuses access to certain areas, claims privilege, objects to a particular seizure (record objection, proceed or cease as instructed)
- Persons present: roles of plaintiff's solicitor, defendant's solicitor, supervising constable (if any), search team members. Restrictions on unauthorized persons
- Duration management: anticipated search length, breaks/meal times, when to cease search if exceeding reasonable bounds
- Post-search procedures: sign-off procedure with defendant, return of unwanted items, sealing of privileged documents, delivery of seized items to court/solicitor custody
- Reporting: written report to court within [SPECIFIED TIMEFRAME] detailing: items found and seized, any disputes during search, privilege claims, condition of premises, any non-cooperation from defendant, photographs/condition report
- Confidentiality and non-disclosure obligations
- Indemnification and insurance details

Include sample schedules for recording documents found, photographs, and dispute log. Address procedures for protecting the defendant's legitimate interests (business operations, family privacy, medical/personal documents, professional secrets). Reference court rules on supervising solicitor conduct and the Practice Direction on Search Orders.

Context: When you need to brief a supervising solicitor on the precise mechanics of executing an Anton Piller order professionally and fairly.
Difficulty: Intermediate
Best AI tool: Claude
Follow-up: PROMPT 004 (Anton Piller Application) or PROMPT 006 (Prohibitory Injunction)

**PROMPT 006 — Prohibitory Injunction Application (Negative Covenant)**

Draft a prohibitory injunction application to restrain the defendant from [SPECIFY THREATENED ACTION: e.g., competing with the plaintiff's business, breaching non-compete clause, infringing copyright, continuing breach of confidentiality, disclosing trade secrets, proceeding with pending proceedings in another jurisdiction].

Please prepare:
- Skeleton argument establishing: (1) strong prima facie case (interpretation of the contract/law, defendant's clear breach); (2) balance of convenience (why stopping the breach causes less overall harm than allowing it); (3) irreparable harm analysis (why damages are inadequate remedy); (4) undertaking as to damages
- Affidavit with: factual background to the obligation/restraint (how it was imposed, when, what did defendant agree to), evidence of defendant's intention/threat to breach (communications, preliminary conduct, known plans), harm that would result from the breach (loss of competitive advantage, loss of trade secrets, loss of client confidentiality, damage to reputation), why damages are inadequate
- Draft injunction with precise terms: exactly what conduct is prohibited (use of customer lists, solicitation of employees, disclosure of information, competition in specified geographical area, specified time period), clarity (sufficiently precise that defendant cannot claim uncertainty), proportionality (does not prevent lawful conduct)
- Address to balance of convenience: why preventing breach now is less onerous than allowing it and paying damages later. Consider: is defendant's ability to compete restricted proportionately? Will business suffer? Are there alternative means of addressing the harm?
- Consideration of defendant's legitimate interests: ability to work in their field (unless truly non-compete), freedom of trade, timing of restraint (is the period reasonable?)
- Proposed undertaking as to damages with security arrangements

Include case law on the test for mandatory vs prohibitory injunctions, and why the court should exercise its discretion to grant this injunction. Address any technical defences (ambiguity, unconscionable conduct, frustration).

Context: When you need to stop a defendant from taking a negative action that would cause irreparable harm, such as breaching a covenant in a contract.
Difficulty: Intermediate
Best AI tool: Claude
Follow-up: PROMPT 007 (Mandatory Injunction) or PROMPT 008 (Injunction Undertaking Calculation)

**PROMPT 007 — Mandatory Injunction Application (Affirmative Obligation)**

Draft a mandatory injunction application requiring the defendant to take affirmative action: [SPECIFY ACTION: e.g., restore access to property/system, return confidential documents, reconstruct damaged asset, dismantle infringing structure, reinstate employee to position, deliver goods sold].

Please prepare:
- Skeleton argument addressing the stricter test for mandatory injunctions: (1) strong prima facie case on the merits; (2) balance of convenience heavily favours granting it (mandatory relief is intrusive); (3) irreparable harm if not granted; (4) defendant's conduct has been wrongful; (5) undertaking as to damages
- Affidavit establishing: how the defendant's actions wrongfully deprived the plaintiff (breach of contract, unlawful trespass, conversion, tortious conduct), why the original position must be restored (specific description of what was lost and what restoration requires), precise restoration action needed (with technical details if necessary), timeline for restoration, costs and practical implications, why this is necessary and not merely convenient
- Draft injunction with: clear mandate (exactly what must be done, by whom, by what date/timeline, to what standard), with progressive steps if restoration occurs in phases (completion dates for each phase), provision for court supervision if compliance is uncertain
- Address the stricter test: why this is not merely regrettable but genuinely necessary, why damages would be inadequate (cannot reverse the wrongdoing, costs of substitute performance, loss of use/enjoyment during non-compliance)
- Consider proportionality and practical feasibility: is the mandated action genuinely feasible? Will it cause excessive hardship to the defendant? Has the plaintiff explored less restrictive alternatives?
- Address defendant's likely arguments: disproportionality, practical impossibility, hardship to defendant
- Proposed undertaking as to damages reflecting the serious nature of mandatory relief

Include case law on when mandatory injunctions should be granted (not routinely, only when necessary and other remedies inadequate) and the high threshold for obtaining them. Reference Singapore cases on mandatory injunctions and the principles from English authorities like Shepherd Homes Ltd v Sandham.

Context: When you need to compel a defendant to take affirmative action to restore the status quo or prevent ongoing irreparable harm.
Difficulty: Advanced
Best AI tool: Claude
Follow-up: PROMPT 006 (Prohibitory Injunction) or PROMPT 008 (Injunction Undertaking)

**PROMPT 008 — Calculate Undertaking as to Damages for Injunction Application**

Calculate the appropriate undertaking as to damages for an [SPECIFY TYPE: Mareva / Anton Piller / prohibitory / mandatory] injunction application. The plaintiff seeks to [DESCRIBE RELIEF]: restrain the defendant from [ACTION] / freeze assets / search premises / require restoration of [ASSET].

Please analyze:
- Nature and scope of the injunction: how intrusive is it (asset freeze = very serious; prohibition on competition = moderate; mandatory action = very serious), duration (interim until trial / post-trial), likelihood that injunction will ultimately be found wrongful
- Potential harms to defendant if injunction is granted: loss of business revenue during injunction period, inability to access frozen assets (operational impact, reputational damage), costs of compliance with injunction (legal costs, operational restructuring), damage to reputation/relationships if later found to be wrongful
- Estimation of quantum: projected monthly revenue loss [SPECIFY DEFENDANT'S BUSINESS] × number of months injunction likely to remain in place, plus costs of compliance, plus estimated reputational/relational damage
- Insurance and security options: cash deposit (amount and terms), parent company guarantee (if parent is creditworthy), professional indemnity insurance bond (availability and cost), plaintiff's personal/corporate assets that could stand behind the undertaking
- Proposed undertaking amount: should be realistic estimate of potential loss but not so onerous as to chill legitimate applications. Typically pitched at [PERCENTAGE: 50-75%] of estimated loss, reflecting uncertainty about ultimate liability
- Suggested security mechanism: cash held in joint account, insurance bond with [INSURER], undertaking by [PARTY] with personal covenant, parent company guarantee with conditions

Consider the court's approach in comparable cases and the factors courts consider when setting undertaking quantum (strength of plaintiff's case, seriousness of potential wrong, defendant's creditworthiness and potential for recovery). Provide reasoning that could be used in submissions to justify the proposed undertaking amount.

Context: When preparing any injunction application and need to propose a realistic and defensible undertaking as to damages.
Difficulty: Intermediate
Best AI tool: Claude
Follow-up: PROMPT 001-007 (relevant injunction prompts) or PROMPT 009 (Variation/Discharge Application)

**PROMPT 009 — Application to Vary or Discharge Injunction**

Draft an application to vary or discharge an existing [SPECIFY TYPE: Mareva / Anton Piller / prohibitory / mandatory] injunction granted on [DATE]. The current injunction [DESCRIBE CURRENT TERMS] and the applicant seeks to [SPECIFY VARIATION/DISCHARGE: e.g., discharge the injunction on the ground that circumstances have changed; vary the scope to permit [SPECIFIED ACTION]; extend the scope to cover [ADDITIONAL MATTERS]].

Please prepare:
- Grounds for variation/discharge: (1) change of circumstances since the original order (events that have occurred, information that has emerged); (2) original basis for the injunction no longer applies (asset no longer at risk of dissipation, threat has been averted, prima facie case has weakened); (3) undertaking as to damages protection is now inadequate/secure (defendant's circumstances have changed, assets depleted); (4) balance of convenience has shifted
- Affidavit establishing the changed circumstances: timeline of events since original order, new information available, current status of the subject matter (if Mareva, what is current position of frozen assets? Has defendant complied? Are more assets identified?), why the original urgency/risk no longer applies
- If defendant is the applicant (seeking discharge): affidavit explaining the impact of the injunction (lost business, cash flow problems, inability to operate), why the original grounds no longer exist (new security has been provided, threat of dissipation averted, plaintiff has obtained full payment), inviting the court to revisit its decision
- If plaintiff is the applicant (seeking to extend): affidavit explaining why the scope was insufficient (new assets discovered, new threats identified), why additional protection is needed now
- Draft order with precise new terms (if varying): amended asset list, modified carve-outs, adjusted reporting requirements, revised timeline
- Address to the principles for varying/discharging: courts are reluctant to revisit their decisions (need substantial change of circumstances), but will do so if injustice would otherwise result, balance of convenience has clearly shifted
- Consideration of delay: has significant time passed? Has the applicant delayed in bringing this application? Courts are less sympathetic to late applications
- Updated undertaking as to damages (if the scope is being extended, or if the defendant's circumstances have changed, the undertaking may need adjustment)

Include reference to the court's inherent jurisdiction to revisit orders and the statutory grounds for variation/discharge. Address timing (should be brought promptly upon change of circumstances) and whether agreement from the other party might be reached.

Context: When circumstances have changed and you need to seek modification of an existing injunction from the court.
Difficulty: Intermediate
Best AI tool: Claude
Follow-up: PROMPT 001-007 (relevant injunction type prompts) or PROMPT 025 (Settlement of Injunction Disputes)

**PROMPT 010 — Ancillary Disclosure Order (Mareva, Anton Piller)**

Draft an ancillary disclosure order to accompany a Mareva injunction or search order. The injunction order [DESCRIBE RELIEF]: freezes assets / searches premises, but without corresponding disclosure obligations, the defendant could circumvent the order or conceal further information.

Please prepare:
- Skeleton argument for disclosure: (1) it is incidental to the main injunction (procedurally available under ROC Order 29); (2) it is necessary to make the main injunction effective (without disclosure, Mareva cannot be enforced); (3) proportionality does not counsel against it (scope can be tailored); (4) it is not oppressive or disproportionate given the context
- Draft disclosure order with: (1) scope definition — what must be disclosed (all assets, whereabouts of funds, beneficial ownership interests, transactions in the past [TIMEFRAME], persons acting in concert with defendant); (2) manner — detailed written statement on affidavit, verified by oath; (3) timeline — by what date must disclosure be made (usually 3-7 days, or as court directs); (4) persons responsible — defendant and [ANY ASSOCIATES: company directors, beneficial owners, family members]; (5) consequences of non-compliance — contempt of court, adverse inferences, financial penalties
- Affidavit supporting disclosure order explaining: why disclosure is necessary (without it, the defendant could move assets during the interim period or conceal beneficial ownership), why the proposed scope is proportionate (narrowly tailored to what is needed to prevent dissipation), why other means of enforcement would be inadequate
- Schedule defining the specific disclosure required: all bank accounts and their current balances; all property (real or personal) held by defendant and family members; all shares and investments; all loans or liabilities; all transfers made in the past [SPECIFIED PERIOD]; all transactions with named associates; current valuations or market information
- Procedure for disclosure: how and where defendant must provide information (affidavit to court, service on plaintiff's solicitors, timeline for updates), confidentiality protections (information should only be used for the purposes of the proceedings, subject to undertaking of confidentiality if necessary)
- Consequences and enforcement: power of court to commit for contempt if disclosure is false or evasive, power to draw adverse inferences if no adequate disclosure is provided
- Address to balance of fairness: defendant has opportunity to explain or qualify disclosures, disclosures are limited to what is necessary for the main injunction, information is protected from wider dissemination

Include reference to procedural rules (ROC Order 29) and case law supporting ancillary disclosure as an incident of the main injunction.

Context: When you need to ensure that a Mareva or Anton Piller injunction can be effectively enforced through requiring the defendant to disclose relevant information.
Difficulty: Intermediate
Best AI tool: Claude
Follow-up: PROMPT 001 (Mareva) or PROMPT 004 (Anton Piller) or PROMPT 012 (Interrogatories)

## INJUNCTIONS — GENERAL & PROCEDURAL

**PROMPT 011 — Injunction: Full Submission Structure (Merits, Balance, Undertaking)**

Prepare a complete written submission for an injunction application [SPECIFY TYPE AND FACTS]. Structure the submission to address the three-stage test: (1) prima facie case (merits); (2) balance of convenience; (3) undertaking as to damages.

Please draft:
- Introduction: what relief is sought, why it is urgent/necessary, brief context
- Part 1 — Prima Facie Case on the Merits:
  - The applicable law (identify the legal basis for relief: breach of contract / tort / intellectual property / equitable /statutory)
  - Contractual interpretation (if contract-based): plain language interpretation, factual matrix if relevant, what did defendant agree to
  - The defendant's breach or threatened breach: specific factual evidence, reference to communications, conduct showing intention
  - Why plaintiff is likely to succeed at trial (likelihood > 50%, often "strong prima facie case" for interim relief)
  - Address to defendant's likely legal defences: are they credible or likely to fail?

- Part 2 — Balance of Convenience:
  - What harm would the plaintiff suffer if the injunction is not granted? Quantify if possible (lost business, irreparable loss of trade secrets, loss of use of property, reputational damage, loss of unique opportunity)
  - Why is this harm irreparable (cannot be adequately compensated by damages)?
  - What harm would the defendant suffer if the injunction is granted? (loss of revenue, inability to operate, constraints on conduct)
  - Which side would be worse off: would allowing the breach do less damage than restraining the defendant?
  - Are there interim solutions that would be less intrusive but adequate?
  - Status quo consideration: what is the current position and should it be maintained?
  - Conduct of parties: has either side behaved unreasonably, delayed, or acted in bad faith?

- Part 3 — Undertaking as to Damages:
  - Proposed undertaking amount and justification (sufficient to be meaningful, not so onerous as to chill relief)
  - Security mechanism (how will the undertaking be secured: cash deposit, insurance bond, personal covenant)
  - Why this undertaking adequately protects the defendant

- Conclusion: synthesis of the three stages, explicit request for the orders sought, any interim procedural orders needed
- Appendices: key documents (contracts, communications showing intention to breach), authorities cited

Ensure throughout that the submission flows logically, anticipates and addresses the defendant's counter-arguments, and makes clear why the court should exercise its discretion to grant the injunction. Reference applicable authorities and explain why they support the plaintiff's position.

Context: When preparing a comprehensive written submission for any interlocutory injunction application.
Difficulty: Advanced
Best AI tool: Claude
Follow-up: PROMPT 012 (Summary Judgment) or PROMPT 003 (Mareva Hearing Oral Submissions)

**PROMPT 012 — Unless Order: Application & Enforcement**

Draft an application for an "unless order" — a court order providing that [DESCRIBE OBLIGATION: the defendant must perform a specified action, disclose information, provide security, comply with discovery, admit facts] UNLESS [DESCRIBE CONSEQUENCE: the defendant's claim will be struck out / the defendant will be deemed to have admitted the allegations / specified relief will be granted without further hearing].

Please prepare:
- Skeleton argument establishing grounds: (1) the defendant is in persistent breach of prior orders or obligations; (2) lesser sanctions have been ineffective; (3) an unless order is proportionate and appropriate; (4) the consequence threatened is real and will incentivize compliance
- Affidavit demonstrating: (1) what was the original obligation (from the court order, contract, discovery rules); (2) what was the deadline; (3) how has the defendant breached it (failed to comply by the deadline, provided incomplete response, failed to pay ordered amount); (4) what efforts has the plaintiff made to enforce (reminder letters, warnings, prior applications); (5) what is the prejudice caused by the breach (delay in proceedings, inability to prepare case, financial loss)
- Draft unless order with: (1) clear statement of the obligation; (2) precise deadline for compliance (should be short, typically 2-7 days, to give defendant real opportunity to cure); (3) clear statement of the consequence (what will happen if order is breached — striking out is serious, deemed admissions is also serious)
- Address to proportionality: is the threatened consequence proportionate to the breach? Court will be more prepared to strike out in cases of persistent non-compliance than for a first minor failure
- Consider the defendant's potential explanations: is there any legitimate reason for non-compliance (circumstances beyond their control, miscommunication, good faith dispute about scope of obligation)? How does the plaintiff answer these?
- Contempt and enforcement: explain that breach of an unless order can result in contempt of court proceedings, financial penalties, or costs sanctions
- Consider timing: has the defendant had adequate opportunity to comply? Is the deadline realistic?
- Address to the court's inherent jurisdiction to manage proceedings and protect its own authority

Include reference to procedural rules on unless orders and case law on when such orders are appropriately used (persistent non-compliance, lesser sanctions have failed, court's authority must be upheld).

Context: When a defendant has repeatedly failed to comply with court orders or obligations and you need to impose a serious consequence to incentivize compliance.
Difficulty: Intermediate
Best AI tool: Claude
Follow-up: PROMPT 013 (Striking Out Application) or PROMPT 024 (Contempt of Court)

## SUMMARY JUDGMENT

**PROMPT 013 — Summary Judgment Application (Strong Merits Case)**

Draft a summary judgment application under Singapore ROC Order 14 seeking to dispose of [DESCRIBE CLAIM/DEFENCE]: [PLAINTIFF'S CLAIM] against the defendant / [DEFENDANT'S COUNTERCLAIM]. The plaintiff/defendant has a clear and strong case, and there is no genuine issue for trial.

Please prepare:
- Skeleton argument establishing the three-part test: (1) the applicant has a strong prima facie case on the merits; (2) there is no genuine issue for trial (the facts are clear, or the legal issue is straightforward); (3) summary judgment is appropriate (not unjust to deprive the other side of a trial); (4) an order for summary judgment will produce finality and save costs
- Affidavit with: (1) narrative facts in chronological order (what happened, when, what was said, what was agreed); (2) reference to supporting documents (contracts, correspondence, invoices, payment records); (3) explanation of why the law is clear and favour the applicant; (4) address to any facts the other side might dispute (plaintiff should address these head-on)
- Legal argument section: (1) identification of the applicable law (contract law, tort, statutory obligation, equitable principle); (2) how the established facts meet the legal test; (3) case law authorities supporting the applicant's interpretation; (4) address to any defences or counterclaims (why are they not credible or sustainable?)
- Address to whether there is a "genuine issue for trial":
  - What facts might the defendant dispute? What is the evidence?
  - Are there any open legal questions? (unlikely in a good summary judgment case)
  - Are the disputed facts credible or likely? (courts should only allow a trial if there is a genuine dispute, not a frivolous or speculative one)
  - If the defendant's version were accepted, would it still lose on the law?
- Affidavit evidence from the defendant (if available): does the defendant's affidavit in response admit key facts? Does it merely assert a different version without evidence? Are there inconsistencies?
- Proposed orders: judgment for [AMOUNT / SPECIFIC RELIEF], interest, costs
- Alternative relief: if the court is not prepared to grant full summary judgment, what should the alternative orders be? (e.g., judgment on some counts, conditional summary judgment if the defendant cannot provide security by a certain date)

Include analysis of when summary judgment should be granted (courts are cautious, trials are generally preferred if there is any genuine factual dispute) and when it should be refused (if the other side's version, if believed, would succeed; if there are credible alternative theories; if the legal position is genuinely unclear).

Context: When you have a strong factual and legal case with minimal genuine factual disputes and wish to obtain judgment without trial.
Difficulty: Advanced
Best AI tool: Claude
Follow-up: PROMPT 014 (Resisting Summary Judgment) or PROMPT 015 (Striking Out Application)

**PROMPT 014 — Resisting Summary Judgment Application**

Draft a comprehensive response to a summary judgment application. The applicant seeks to dispose of [DESCRIBE THE CLAIM/ISSUE] by arguing [DESCRIBE APPLICANT'S ARGUMENT: e.g., the contract is clear on its face, the defendant's breach is undeniable, there is no genuine factual dispute].

Please prepare:
- Skeleton argument addressing: (1) there ARE genuine issues for trial (credible factual disputes, alternative legal interpretations); (2) it would be unjust to grant summary judgment without a full hearing; (3) the court should refuse summary judgment and allow the matter to proceed to trial
- Affidavit with: (1) factual narrative providing the respondent's version of events (a credible alternative account to what the applicant has alleged); (2) explanation of why key facts are in dispute (applicant's version is not the only reasonable interpretation, there are credible alternative explanations, the applicant has selectively presented evidence); (3) evidence supporting the respondent's version (documents, communications, witness accounts); (4) legal arguments showing why the law is not as clear as the applicant suggests, or why even if the applicant's factual version is accepted, the legal conclusion is not inevitable
- Address to the "genuine issue" test: (1) What factual disputes exist? Describe the areas of contention and explain why they are genuine (there is credible evidence on both sides, it is not frivolous or speculative); (2) What alternative legal interpretations are available? Why is the law not settled in the applicant's favour?; (3) Explain why a trial is necessary to resolve these issues fairly
- Key points to emphasize: (1) Courts are cautious about granting summary judgment; (2) The respondent is entitled to put their version to a judge and jury (or judge in a bench trial); (3) Credibility and witness demeanor matter — these cannot be assessed on paper; (4) Any ambiguity or genuine dispute should be resolved at trial, not at the interlocutory stage
- Address to the applicant's evidence: (1) Where is it selective or misleading? (2) What alternative interpretations exist? (3) Are there gaps or inconsistencies that suggest the applicant's version is not the whole story?; (4) Has the applicant taken unfair advantage in framing the facts?
- If there is a legal dispute: (1) Cite authorities supporting the respondent's interpretation; (2) Explain why the applicant's legal authorities are distinguishable or misapplied; (3) Show that the law is not as settled as the applicant suggests
- Conclusion: request that summary judgment be refused, the application be dismissed with costs, and the matter be allowed to proceed to trial

Include discussion of the high threshold for summary judgment (American Cyanamid test, clear and obvious case) and why it is not met here. Address timing (has the respondent had adequate opportunity to gather evidence and prepare a response?).

Context: When faced with a summary judgment application and you need to persuade the court that genuine factual or legal disputes exist and should be tried.
Difficulty: Advanced

  - Did the defendant perform [OBLIGATION]? If not, why not?
  - When was [OBLIGATION] supposed to be performed by?

  - What payments did the defendant make to [THIRD PARTY]? When, how much, for what purpose?
  - What is the source of funds for [TRANSACTION]?

  - What communications passed between the defendant and [THIRD PARTY] regarding [ISSUE]?
  - Does the defendant have email records, meeting minutes, or notes relating to [TOPIC]?

  - Avoid compound questions: ask one thing per interrogatory
  - Make questions answerable: the defendant should be able to answer with facts they have knowledge of
  - Consider objections: word questions to minimize the risk that the defendant can justify refusing to answer (avoid overly intrusive, unduly burdensome, or vague questions)
  - Progressive detail: start with broad questions and progressively narrow down to specific facts
  - Avoid legal characterization: ask "did the defendant make a representation that X?" not "was the defendant's conduct fraudulent?"

**PROMPT 020 — Privilege Assessment & Privilege Log**

Prepare a comprehensive privilege assessment and privilege log for discovery in [DESCRIBE MATTER]. The documents to be assessed total [NUMBER] and include communications with counsel, documents created for purposes of litigation, and documents created in anticipation of legal disputes.

Please prepare:
- Overview of applicable privileges:
  - Legal advice privilege: communications between client and lawyer giving/requesting legal advice, and documents created for the dominant purpose of giving/requesting legal advice
  - Litigation privilege: communications and documents created for the dominant purpose of conducting/giving advice about litigation
  - Joint interest privilege: communications between parties with a common legal interest, made in an attempt to find a common solution (applies in joint defence scenarios)
  - Work product doctrine (in some jurisdictions): documents created by a lawyer in anticipation of litigation
  - Settlement communications: offers to settle are generally inadmissible, protected from disclosure
  - Without prejudice communications: negotiations marked "without prejudice" are protected from disclosure

- Privilege assessment protocol:
  - For each document, assess: (1) Who created it? (lawyer, client, third party); (2) What is its purpose? (giving legal advice, obtaining legal advice, conducting litigation, negotiating settlement, etc.); (3) Is the dominant purpose the one that attracts privilege?; (4) Was privilege waived (shared with third parties, shared in open court, etc.)?
  - Document-by-document analysis: go through each document and categorize it
  - Privilege log: maintain a spreadsheet logging each privileged document with: document number, date, sender, recipient, description, basis of privilege claim, whether privilege was waived

- Common issues:
  - In-house counsel: advice from in-house lawyers is privileged, but in-house lawyers also have business roles; assess the dominant purpose
  - Business advice vs. legal advice: if a communication is partly business advice and partly legal advice, is privilege established? (General rule: legal advice privilege applies if the legal advice is the dominant purpose)
  - Third-party participation: if a third party (accountant, business advisor) participates in a communication with counsel, does privilege apply? (Generally privilege is lost if a third party not participating in giving advice is present)
  - Email chains: are all emails in a chain privileged, or only certain messages? (Separate privilege analysis for each message)
  - Documents created pre-litigation vs. in anticipation of litigation: pre-litigation business documents are not privileged; documents created in anticipation of litigation may attract litigation privilege even before proceedings are formally commenced
  - Medical/psychological reports: usually not privileged unless obtained by counsel in anticipation of litigation

- Waiver analysis:
  - Was privilege waived by disclosure? (Privilege is lost if the document was shared with persons outside the privilege relationship)
  - Selective disclosure: if the client disclosed the document to the other side or to third parties, privilege is waived (in Australia/Singapore, even partial disclosure waives privilege)
  - Implied waiver: did the client put the contents of the communication in issue? (e.g., if the client claims they relied on legal advice, and produce the advice to support that claim, privilege may be waived)
  - Subject matter waiver: if privilege is waived on one document, does it extend to related documents? (Generally no, but in some jurisdictions, broad waiver rules may apply)

- Asserting privilege:
  - Privilege log format: document number, date, parties, description, basis of privilege, whether claimed privilege
  - Description should be sufficient to allow the other side to assess whether privilege claim is credible, but not so detailed as to waive privilege (describe the subject matter without disclosing the substance of the legal advice)
  - Be consistent: similar documents should receive similar treatment
  - Be specific: cite exactly which privilege (legal advice vs. litigation privilege) and explain why it applies

- Risks and mitigation:
  - Inadvertent disclosure: if a privileged document is produced by mistake, what is the remedy? (Notify the other side, seek court order for return/non-use)
  - Proportionality: some courts may not allow you to withhold large volumes of documents on the basis of privilege if the review burden is excessive. Negotiate agreements with the other side on sampling, verification, etc.
  - Confidentiality concerns: if the document contains privileged information but also factual information, can you redact and produce the factual part? (Generally no for privileged documents, but consider confidentiality orders)

- Privilege log template (Excel/spreadsheet):
  - Column 1: Document number (e.g., DOC-00001)
  - Column 2: Date (YYYY-MM-DD)
  - Column 3: From (sender)
  - Column 4: To (recipient)
  - Column 5: CC/BCC
  - Column 6: Subject matter/description
  - Column 7: Basis of privilege (Legal advice privilege / Litigation privilege / Settlement communication)
  - Column 8: Claimed? (Yes/No)
  - Column 9: Notes (any issues or qualifications)

- Anticipated challenges:
  - Opposing counsel may challenge privilege claims (argue that the dominant purpose was not legal advice or litigation preparation)
  - Court may conduct an in-camera inspection of disputed privileged documents
  - Assertions on the privilege log may be tested in evidence
  - If privilege claims are found to be improper, the documents will be produced and you may face adverse costs orders

Include guidance on privilege risks (over-claiming privilege loses credibility; under-claiming privilege results in disclosure of sensitive communications) and best practices (be conservative in claiming privilege, document your reasoning, maintain a privilege log with clear explanations).

Context: When managing discovery and needing to identify and assert privilege over sensitive communications with lawyers and litigation-related documents.

  - State the relief sought (contribution/indemnity in the sum of $X, or alternatively the sum that a court determines)

**PROMPT 024 — Unless Order: Enforcement & Contempt**

Draft an application for contempt of court and costs sanctions arising from the defendant's breach of an unless order dated [DATE] requiring [SPECIFY OBLIGATION]. The deadline passed on [DATE] and the defendant [DESCRIBE BREACH: failed to comply, provided incomplete compliance, breached the spirit of the order].

Please prepare:
- Factual narrative: (1) the original court order (date, terms, reasons); (2) the deadline imposed; (3) the defendant's actual conduct (or failure to act); (4) evidence of non-compliance (compare the order's requirements with what the defendant actually did); (5) impact of the breach (what prejudice has the plaintiff suffered from the breach?)
- Proposed orders: (1) declaration that the defendant is in contempt of court; (2) that the unless order condition has not been satisfied; (3) therefore the specified consequence is triggered (e.g., the defendant's claim is struck out, or the allegations are deemed admitted); (4) costs orders against the defendant (costs of the application and costs thrown away by non-compliance)
- Evidence:
  - If the unless order required the defendant to disclose information: comparison of what was ordered and what was disclosed (or what was not disclosed)
  - If the order required the defendant to pay money: bank records showing payment (or non-payment)
  - If the order required the defendant to take action: evidence that the action was not taken
  - Communications: any correspondence showing the defendant was aware of the order and its deadline
  - Prejudice: evidence of how the breach has impacted the plaintiff (inability to proceed with the case, costs wasted, preparation hindered)

- Affidavit from the plaintiff or plaintiff's solicitor with: (1) a true copy of the court order; (2) evidence of service on the defendant; (3) confirmation that no extension of time was granted; (4) detailed account of the defendant's non-compliance; (5) the prejudice suffered
- Address to the defendant's likely arguments:
  - Did the defendant's solicitors advise them of the order? (Ignorance of court orders is not a defence)
  - Was there a communication asking for an extension of time? (If so, was it refused or ignored?)
  - Did the defendant claim that compliance was impossible? (The court will assess whether the claim is credible)
  - Are there mitigating circumstances? (Illness, incapacity, inadvertence — courts may show mercy, but non-compliance is serious)

- Proposed order: (1) the unless order condition is not satisfied; (2) the consequence is triggered (the defendant's claim is struck out, or allegations are deemed admitted); (3) costs order (on an indemnity basis or on a party-and-party basis)
- Distinction from contempt of court: strict compliance is not always required; if the defendant has substantially complied with the spirit of the order, the court may not impose the full consequence. However, if the breach is clear and deliberate, the court will enforce the unless order strictly.

Include discussion of the seriousness of court orders and the consequences of breach (contempt of court can result in imprisonment or fines, in addition to the trigger of the unless order). Courts take non-compliance seriously and will generally enforce unless orders unless the defendant can show a credible excuse.

**PROMPT 025 — Costs Submissions on Interlocutory Applications**

Prepare comprehensive written submissions on costs following [DESCRIBE INTERLOCUTORY APPLICATION: e.g., a Mareva injunction application that was successful, a striking out application that was partially successful, a summary judgment application that the court refused].

Please prepare:
- Standard costs principles:
  - General rule: costs follow the event (the losing party pays the winning party's costs)
  - Discretionary factors: the court has discretion to depart from the general rule, considering: (1) success on different issues (if a party was successful on some issues but not others, costs may be apportioned); (2) conduct of the parties (unreasonable conduct, lack of candor, making unrealistic demands in settlement negotiations); (3) complexity (did the losing party raise complex legal issues that required the winning party to incur costs?); (4) proportionality (were the costs incurred proportionate to the importance of the matter?)
  - Specific interlocutory cost orders: "costs in the cause" (costs of this application are dealt with at trial), "costs reserved" (court withholds the decision on costs pending further information), fixed costs (court orders a fixed sum rather than assessed costs)

- If you were successful on the application:
  - Which issues did you succeed on? (fully successful, or successful on some points?)
  - What was your conduct? (reasonable, proportionate, candid?)
  - What costs did you incur? (estimate or actual figure)
  - Request: costs on a party-and-party basis from date of filing to date of judgment (or a higher order if there were aggravating circumstances)
  - Alternative: if the application was necessary (defendant was being unreasonable, forced the plaintiff to litigate a point that should have been conceded), request costs on an indemnity basis

- If you were partially successful:
  - Identify the issues you succeeded on and the issues you failed on
  - Should costs be apportioned? (e.g., if the application had three distinct issues, and you succeeded on two, you might ask for 2/3 of your costs)
  - Alternatively, argue that one issue was dominant, and you should recover all costs

- If you were unsuccessful:
  - Acknowledge the outcome
  - Explain any mitigating factors (the law was unclear, the defendant's arguments were sophisticated and required serious consideration, the application was not frivolous)
  - Propose: no order as to costs, or costs in the cause (defer costs decision to trial)
  - If the defendant was unreasonable, highlight their conduct and ask the court to consider that when awarding costs

- Conduct issues:
  - If the defendant's conduct was egregious (failing to comply with court orders, providing false information, being uncooperative), draw this to the court's attention as a reason for an adverse costs order
  - If you made a settlement offer that the defendant unreasonably rejected, highlight this (may support a claim for indemnity costs)
  - If you warned the defendant that you would seek costs and they proceeded anyway, refer to this warning

- Quantum of costs:
  - If seeking assessed costs: provide a breakdown of costs incurred (solicitor time, counsel fees, expert costs, court fees, service costs, etc.)
  - If requesting fixed costs: provide a benchmark or similar applications to support the fixed amount proposed
  - Consider proportionality: avoid asking for costs that are manifestly disproportionate to the importance of the matter or the time genuinely required

- Specific orders:
  - "Costs of this application to be paid by the defendant to the plaintiff on a party-and-party basis, to be assessed if not agreed"
  - "Costs of this application to be paid by the defendant to the plaintiff on an indemnity basis"
  - "No order as to costs"
  - "Costs reserved for determination at trial"
  - "Costs in the cause" (incorporated into the trial judgment)

- Drafting of submissions:
  - Structure: identify the relevant legal principles, apply them to your facts, explain why a particular costs order is appropriate
  - Tone: courts are responsive to reasonable, proportionate requests; avoid asking for unrealistic costs orders
  - Citations: refer to relevant authorities (case law establishing when indemnity costs are appropriate, fixed costs rules, etc.)

Include sample costs submissions for different scenarios (successful application, partially successful application, unsuccessful application).

Context: When you need to seek or oppose a costs order following an interlocutory application.
Difficulty: Intermediate
Best AI tool: Claude
Follow-up: PROMPT 001-024 (relevant interlocutory application prompts)
