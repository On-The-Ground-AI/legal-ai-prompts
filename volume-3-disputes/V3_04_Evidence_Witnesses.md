# AI Prompts for Evidence & Witness Preparation

Comprehensive prompts for preparing evidence, witness statements, expert reports, and supporting documentation for civil litigation. Covers AEICs, witness statements, privilege assessment, chronologies, and evidence management.

## AFFIDAVITS OF EVIDENCE-IN-CHIEF (AEIC)

**PROMPT 026 — Draft AEIC: Factual Narrative (Chronological)**

Prepare an Affidavit of Evidence-in-Chief (AEIC) for [WITNESS NAME], providing comprehensive factual evidence on [DESCRIBE ISSUE: e.g., the plaintiff's loss and damage, communications with the defendant, transactions and payments, knowledge of wrongdoing]. The witness has direct knowledge of [SPECIFY FACTS].

Please draft:
- Heading and formal parts:
  - Title: "Affidavit of Evidence-in-Chief of [WITNESS NAME]"
  - Form: "I, [WITNESS NAME], of [ADDRESS], [OCCUPATION], make oath and say as follows:"
  - Statement of truth/oath language

- Main narrative organized chronologically:
  - Introduction: identify the witness (name, occupation, how they became involved in the matter), state their role (direct participant, observer, decision-maker, advisor, investigator)
  - Background context: provide sufficient context so the judge understands the setting (business relationship, contractual relationship, prior dealings between the parties)
  - Chronological narrative: march through the events in order, with dates and times where known
    - Each paragraph should cover one event or conversation
    - Be specific: who said what, when, where, to whom, what was said (use direct quotes where appropriate, or paraphrase accurately)
    - Reference supporting documents: "as evidenced by the email dated [DATE] marked [EXHIBIT]"
    - Include the witness's contemporaneous state of mind: what did the witness understand, what were they concerned about, what was their reaction
    - Avoid legal argument: state facts, not conclusions about liability ("the defendant said X" not "the defendant lied")

- Key factual areas to cover:
  - Contractual formation: how the contract came about, who negotiated, what were the key terms, what did each party understand the terms to mean
  - Performance: what each party was supposed to do, when, what did each party actually do, any deviations from the contract
  - Communications: key emails, phone calls, meetings; what was said, who was present, what was the context
  - Knowledge: what did the witness know about the defendant's conduct, when did they find out, how did they find out, what was their reaction
  - Loss and damage: how has the witness (or the plaintiff) been harmed, what is the basis for quantifying the loss, what documentation supports the loss claim
  - Causation: what caused the harm (the defendant's breach, the defendant's conduct), what steps did the witness take to mitigate

- Evidence structure:
  - Factual account: "On 15 March 2024, I received an email from the defendant [EMAIL REFERENCE] stating..."
  - Supporting documents: reference exhibits ("as shown in Exhibit A"), with exhibits appended and marked
  - Knowledge and belief: "I believe that the defendant knew of this requirement because [FACTS]"
  - Credibility aids: explain sources of knowledge ("I was present at the meeting", "this is recorded in the meeting minutes", "the defendant told me")

- Length and detail:
  - AEICs can be lengthy but should be focused and relevant
  - Avoid repetition; if a fact is stated once, it need not be restated
  - Include sufficient detail for the judge to understand the significance (not too much "he said/she said" without context, not too little that the judge is left confused)
  - Aim for a coherent, flowing narrative that is easy for the judge to follow

- Exhibits:
  - Identify and number all exhibits (Exhibit A, Exhibit B, etc.)
  - Each exhibit should be referenced in the AEIC narrative
  - Exhibits can include: emails, letters, contracts, invoices, payment records, meeting minutes, photographs, property valuations, expert reports
  - Exhibits should be authentic originals or certified copies (not summaries or paraphrases)

- Verification:
  - Final paragraph: "The facts stated in this affidavit are true and correct to the best of my knowledge, information and belief"
  - Date and sign the affidavit
  - Have the affidavit sworn before a commissioner for oaths or notary public

- Tone and presentation:
  - Be objective and factual, not argumentative
  - Avoid emotional language or expressions of opinion
  - Use clear, simple language (the judge will not be familiar with technical or industry jargon unless explained)
  - Maintain consistency with other evidence (don't contradict your earlier positions or other witness statements)
  - Be honest about gaps or uncertainties ("I don't recall the exact date", "I may be mistaken about the details", "I was not present at this meeting")

- Common pitfalls to avoid:
  - Leading the reader: "It was clear that the defendant was trying to deceive me" (state facts; let the judge draw conclusions)
  - Argumentative tone: resist the urge to persuade, just recount
  - Over-length: don't include irrelevant background or repetitive material
  - Inconsistency: ensure the AEIC is consistent with other statements, prior testimony, and documents
  - Vagueness: be specific about dates, amounts, and who said what

- Cross-examination preparation:
  - Anticipate questions the opposing counsel will ask
  - Be prepared to explain the basis for your evidence (how do you know this, who told you, what documents support this)
  - Be prepared to acknowledge areas of uncertainty or gaps in your knowledge
  - Understand that the opposing counsel may test your credibility and reliability

Include guidance on best practices for AEIC preparation, the importance of accuracy and completeness, and how AEICs are used in court (read as the witness's main evidence, tested in cross-examination, and used to support final submissions).

Context: When you need to prepare a comprehensive factual account from a witness for use as their main evidence at trial.
Difficulty: Advanced
Best AI tool: Claude
Follow-up: PROMPT 027 (AEIC with Expert Evidence) or PROMPT 029 (Cross-Examination Preparation)

**PROMPT 027 — AEIC Incorporating Expert Evidence & Technical Facts**

Draft an Affidavit of Evidence-in-Chief for [WITNESS NAME], a [PROFESSION: engineer, accountant, IT specialist, architect, surveyor, etc.], addressing [DESCRIBE TECHNICAL ISSUE: e.g., defects in a building, financial irregularities, software vulnerabilities, property damage, technical compliance].

Please draft:
- Introduction:
  - Identify the witness and their qualifications (professional credentials, experience, relevant background)
  - State their role in the matter (was retained by whom, when, what were they asked to do)
  - Confirm they are providing evidence based on their professional knowledge and experience

- Expert opinion vs. factual observations:
  - Factual observations: "I inspected the building on [DATE] and observed [SPECIFIC DEFECTS: cracks in the foundation, water seepage, etc.]"
  - Professional opinion: "Based on my professional experience and the observations I made, it is my opinion that [CAUSES, IMPLICATIONS, REMEDIATION REQUIRED]"
  - Distinguish clearly between facts you observed and opinions you formed

- Structure of the AEIC:
  - Background: how did the witness become involved, what was the scope of their engagement, what were they asked to investigate or assess
  - Site inspection/investigation: detailed account of what was inspected, when, how long, what testing or analysis was undertaken, what was observed
  - Findings: specific technical findings, supported by measurements, photographs, test results, samples analyzed
  - Analysis: how the witness analyzed the findings, what professional standards or methodologies were applied, what was the professional basis for conclusions
  - Opinion and conclusions: what is the witness's professional opinion, what is the basis, what are the implications
  - Remediation/resolution: what remediation or steps are necessary to address the issue

- Technical detail and explanation:
  - Include sufficient technical detail for a lay judge to understand (avoid jargon unless defined, explain technical concepts in accessible language)
  - Use diagrams, photographs, technical drawings to illustrate (append as exhibits)
  - Quantify findings: amounts, measurements, percentages, cost estimates
  - Reference industry standards, building codes, professional guidelines that are relevant

- Methodology and reliability:
  - Explain the methodology used (site inspection, sampling, testing, desktop analysis, calculation)
  - Justify why the methodology is appropriate and reliable (standard practice, professional conventions, basis for conclusions)
  - Acknowledge limitations: "This opinion is based on site observations only, without intrusive testing. A more detailed investigation would require [ACCESS/TESTING]"
  - Address alternative explanations: "There are several possible causes for this defect; however, based on the evidence, the most likely cause is [X]"

- Causation and sequencing:
  - If the expert is addressing causation (what caused the defect/loss), explain the causal chain (original failure → intermediate steps → ultimate harm)
  - Timeline: when did the defect likely occur, when should it have been detected, what was the progression
  - Exclude or include other potential causes: "While factor A could have contributed, the primary cause is factor B because [REASONING]"

- Quantification:
  - Cost of remediation: break down by component (materials, labor, contingency)
  - Quantum of loss: if the expert is addressing financial loss, how is it calculated (direct loss, consequential loss, mitigation costs)
  - Supporting calculations: show calculations in exhibits (do not hide methodology)

- Qualifications statement:
  - Confirm the expert is not being paid on a contingency basis (fee is not dependent on the outcome)
  - Confirm the expert understands their duty to the court (to provide impartial, objective evidence, not to advocate for a party)
  - Confirm the expert is aware of court rules on expert evidence

- Exhibits:
  - Photographs (with annotations if helpful)
  - Technical drawings or plans
  - Test results, certificates, lab reports
  - Industry standards or guidelines referenced
  - Calculation spreadsheets
  - Cost estimates with supporting documentation
  - Any prior reports or studies

- Cross-examination readiness:
  - Be prepared to defend the basis for opinions (why is this opinion reliable, what professional basis supports it)
  - Be prepared to acknowledge alternative opinions ("it's possible that X, but in my professional view Y is more likely because")
  - Be prepared to acknowledge limitations of the investigation ("I did not have access to [X]; if that information were available, it might affect my opinion")
  - Be prepared to explain technical details to a non-expert judge

Include guidance on the expert's duties to the court, the importance of objectivity and impartiality, and how expert evidence is tested in cross-examination. Address the distinction between facts and opinion, and the requirement that opinions be based on a reliable professional foundation.

Context: When a qualified expert (engineer, accountant, IT specialist, etc.) needs to provide technical evidence on a complex issue in litigation.
Difficulty: Advanced
Best AI tool: Claude
Follow-up: PROMPT 028 (Expert Report & Duty to Court) or PROMPT 029 (Cross-Examination Preparation)

**PROMPT 028 — Expert Report & Duty to the Court**

Prepare a comprehensive expert report from [EXPERT NAME], a [PROFESSION], addressing [DESCRIBE ISSUE]. The report will be used as evidence in litigation and must comply with court rules on expert evidence, including the duty to the court.

Please draft:
- Front matter:
  - Title page: "Expert Report of [NAME]"
  - Table of contents
  - Executive summary (1-2 page overview of findings and conclusions)

- Instruction to expert:
  - "This report has been prepared at the instruction of [INSTRUCTING PARTY'S SOLICITORS]"
  - State of the issues: "I have been asked to investigate and report on [DESCRIBE ISSUES]"
  - Date of instruction and scope of work

- Expert's qualifications:
  - Professional credentials (degrees, certifications, licenses)
  - Relevant experience (years in field, specialist knowledge, prior litigation experience)
  - Publications, awards, professional positions
  - Confirm expertise is relevant to the issues in the case

- Statement of truth/duty to court:
  - Standard form: "I confirm that I have made clear which facts and observations are within my own knowledge and which are not. I confirm that I am aware of my duties to the court and that I have complied with them. I confirm that this report is, to the best of my knowledge and belief, complete and accurate. I confirm that I am not aware of any matter which might render my report misleading or incomplete."
  - Independence statement: "I am not aware of any conflict of interest that might render my evidence biased or unreliable"

- Scope and instructions:
  - Define the issues I have been asked to address
  - Confirm the documents I have reviewed
  - Confirm the inspections/investigations I have undertaken
  - Identify any limitations on my investigations (access restrictions, time constraints, incomplete data)

- Methodology:
  - Describe how I approached the investigation
  - What standards or guidelines I applied (professional standards, industry conventions, technical specifications)
  - What testing, analysis, or measurements I undertook (and why)
  - What assumptions I made (and why they are reasonable)
  - Acknowledge any areas where my investigation was limited

- Factual findings:
  - Site/document inspection: what I observed, when, how detailed
  - Measurements and testing: specific measurements taken, tests conducted, results obtained
  - Data analysis: if applicable, analysis of financial data, statistical analysis, sampling
  - Photographs and diagrams: visual evidence supporting findings

- Analysis and opinion:
  - Application of professional knowledge to the facts
  - Causation analysis (if relevant): what caused the defect/issue/loss
  - Quantum analysis (if relevant): calculation of damages or costs
  - Alternative scenarios: consideration of alternative explanations or approaches
  - Basis for opinions: why am I confident in my conclusions, what professional standards support them

- Conclusions:
  - Summary of main findings
  - Answers to the specific questions posed by the instructing solicitor
  - Clear statement of expert opinion (with confidence levels where appropriate)

- Limitations and caveats:
  - Areas of uncertainty: "My opinion on [X] is based on limited data; a more detailed investigation would be required to confirm"
  - Matters outside expertise: "This issue falls outside my area of expertise and would require a [SPECIFIC PROFESSIONAL] to address"
  - Dependency on assumptions: "If the assumption [X] is incorrect, my opinion on [Y] would require revision"

- Appendices:
  - Curriculum vitae
  - Details of prior litigation experience and expert evidence provided
  - Photographs and diagrams
  - Test results and laboratory reports
  - Calculations and working
  - References to professional standards and guidelines
  - Any relevant publications or research

- Format and presentation:
  - Clear structure with numbered sections and paragraphs
  - Plain language, avoiding jargon (or defining jargon where necessary)
  - Consistent formatting and professional presentation
  - Page numbering, exhibit references, clear cross-references

- Court rules compliance:
  - Singapore: CPR rules on expert evidence, duty to court, form and content
  - Confirm adherence to court-approved expert's declaration
  - Confirm the report is not advocacy but objective evidence
  - Confirm the expert is not acting as an expert in breach of court orders (e.g., expert not being paid on contingency basis)

- Common pitfalls to avoid:
  - Advocacy tone: presenting the expert's opinion as fact to the court, rather than as the expert's best professional opinion
  - Overstatement: using absolute language ("certainly", "definitely", "impossible") where qualified language is more appropriate ("likely", "in my opinion", "probably")
  - Selective presentation: excluding information that contradicts the expert's opinion
  - Exceeding expertise: offering opinions on matters outside the expert's field
  - Failure to acknowledge uncertainty: presenting opinions as more certain than they actually are

- Expert's awareness:
  - An expert in litigation has a duty to the court that overrides any duty to the instructing party
  - An expert must present objective, impartial evidence
  - An expert's opinion must be based on a reliable professional foundation
  - An expert must acknowledge areas of uncertainty and alternative possibilities
  - An expert may be challenged in cross-examination on methodology, reliability, and impartiality

Include guidance on the expert's duties and the courts' expectations of expert evidence. Address the distinction between admissible expert opinion and inadmissible advocacy. Discuss how expert evidence is tested in cross-examination and the importance of maintaining credibility and objectivity.

Context: When preparing a formal expert report for use as evidence in litigation, complying with all court rules and ethical duties.
Difficulty: Advanced
Best AI tool: Claude
Follow-up: PROMPT 027 (AEIC with Expert Evidence) or PROMPT 029 (Cross-Examination Preparation)

## WITNESS STATEMENTS & PREPARATION

**PROMPT 029 — Cross-Examination Preparation Strategy & Questions**

Prepare a comprehensive cross-examination strategy and likely cross-examination questions for [WITNESS NAME], based on their AEIC/statement on [DESCRIBE ISSUE]. The witness will be cross-examined by [OPPOSING COUNSEL] regarding [DESCRIBE CLAIMS BEING CHALLENGED].

Please prepare:
- Overview of witness's evidence:
  - What is the witness testifying to (main claims)?
  - What is the value/importance of this evidence to your case?
  - What aspects are most vulnerable to challenge?

- Opposing counsel's likely strategy:
  - What outcome does the opposing counsel want (to discredit the witness, to show bias, to undermine specific evidence, to establish an alternative narrative)?
  - What weaknesses in the witness's evidence might opposing counsel exploit?
  - What contradictions with other evidence might be raised?
  - What attacks on credibility are likely (bias, financial interest, prior inconsistency, poor memory, lack of reliability)?

- Areas of vulnerability:
  - Credibility: Is the witness biased (has a financial interest in the outcome, has prior dealings with the party they're supporting)? Is there a history of dishonesty or prior inconsistency?
  - Reliability: Was the witness in a position to observe what they claim (were they present, do they have knowledge)? How reliable is the witness's memory (was the event recent or long ago)?
  - Accuracy: Are there inconsistencies in the witness's account (with their own prior statements, with other evidence, with documents)?
  - Completeness: Has the witness left out important information that would contradict their current narrative?
  - Interpretation: Has the witness claimed to know things they couldn't actually know (e.g., the defendant's mental state, the defendant's intent)?

- Preparation strategy:
  - Identify the key contentious points in the witness's evidence
  - For each point, consider: What is the opposing counsel likely to ask? How vulnerable is this evidence? What is the best way for the witness to address the question?
  - Prepare the witness to give short, direct answers to yes/no questions (don't volunteer information beyond what is asked)
  - Prepare the witness to acknowledge areas of uncertainty ("I don't recall", "I may be mistaken", "I was not present")
  - Prepare the witness to explain the basis for their evidence (how do they know this, what documents support this, who told them)
  - Prepare the witness for unfavorable premises ("You were paid by the plaintiff to provide this evidence, weren't you?" — the witness should acknowledge the payment while explaining that it doesn't affect the truthfulness of their evidence)

- Likely cross-examination topics:
  - Opportunity and knowledge: Was the witness actually present when they claim to have observed something? Did they have access to information they claim to have?
  - Memory and recollection: How long ago was the event? Is the witness relying on memory or on documents? Are there gaps or inconsistencies in their account?
  - Documents: Are there documents that contradict the witness's account? Has the witness reviewed documents that might affect their testimony?
  - Prior statements: Have the witness made prior statements that are inconsistent with their current AEIC?
  - Interest: What financial or personal interest does the witness have in the outcome?
  - Bias: Is the witness biased toward the party that engaged them (tendency to remember things favorably, to omit unfavorable information)?
  - Leading questions: opposing counsel will use leading questions to put a proposition to the witness and either get them to agree or to highlight inconsistency

- Anticipated cross-examination questions:
  - [For each major fact in dispute, draft 5-10 tough cross-examination questions]
  - Example: "Your AEIC states that the defendant said X on [DATE]. But isn't it true that [OPPOSING COUNSEL'S VERSION]?"
  - "You have a significant financial interest in this case succeeding, don't you? You stand to recover [AMOUNT]?"
  - "You didn't mention [IMPORTANT FACT] in your statement. Why was that omitted?"
  - "Your account is inconsistent with [DOCUMENTARY EVIDENCE]. How do you explain this?"
  - "You were not present when [KEY EVENT] occurred, so your knowledge is based entirely on what you were told?"
  - "You can't seriously expect this court to accept your version of events when it is directly contradicted by the contemporaneous documents?"

- Witness confidence assessment:
  - Identify areas where the witness is confident and can be firm in their answers
  - Identify areas where the witness is uncertain and should acknowledge uncertainty rather than guess
  - Prepare the witness to distinguish between what they know directly and what they were told (hearsay)
  - Prepare the witness for aggressive questioning (maintaining composure, not becoming defensive or argumentative)

- De-escalation and credibility maintenance:
  - Advice to witness: stay calm, don't argue with opposing counsel, acknowledge fair points, don't try to score points off opposing counsel
  - If the witness has made a mistake, acknowledge it early and move on (continuing to defend an indefensible position damages credibility)
  - If the witness doesn't know something, say so (jurors/judges respect honesty more than bluster)
  - Maintain a professional, balanced tone (not defensive, not evasive, not angry)

- Re-examination preparation:
  - After the witness is cross-examined, the examining counsel can re-examine to clarify or rehabilitate evidence
  - Note areas where the witness's credibility was challenged and may need rehabilitation
  - Identify issues that were unclear or misrepresented in cross-examination and might need clarification
  - Prepare questions that allow the witness to give their perspective on issues raised in cross-examination

- Documents and demonstratives:
  - If specific documents will be used in cross-examination, brief the witness on them beforehand
  - Have the witness review any documents likely to be put to them
  - Consider preparing visual aids or chronologies that support the witness's version

- Behavioral coaching:
  - Appearance and demeanor: witnesses should dress professionally, maintain eye contact with the judge (not counsel), speak clearly
  - Pace and tone: avoid speaking too quickly (which suggests nervousness), pause before answering difficult questions (which suggests thoughtfulness)
  - Body language: don't fidget, cross arms (defensive), or lean forward aggressively
  - Handling difficult moments: if the witness is caught out or contradicted, acknowledge it gracefully rather than doubling down

Include a sample cross-examination outline with likely questions and suggested answers. Discuss the importance of witness preparation (without coaching to dishonesty) and the distinction between legitimate preparation and inappropriate witness influencing.

Context: When preparing a witness for cross-examination by opposing counsel at trial or hearing.
Difficulty: Advanced
Best AI tool: Claude
Follow-up: PROMPT 026 (Draft AEIC) or PROMPT 031 (Chronology & Timeline)

**PROMPT 030 — Witness Statement (Non-Expert, Concise)**

Prepare a concise witness statement from [WITNESS NAME], addressing [DESCRIBE ISSUE: e.g., observed the defendant's conduct, participated in key meeting, witnessed communication, can verify loss]. The statement is for use as [SPECIFY USE: evidence in support of an interlocutory application, background information, supporting document for a discovery application].

Please draft:
- Opening:
  - "My name is [WITNESS NAME]. I live at [ADDRESS]. I am [OCCUPATION]. I am providing this statement in support of [DESCRIBE PURPOSE]."
  - "I have personal knowledge of the facts stated herein and they are true to the best of my knowledge and belief."

- Factual narrative:
  - Organized chronologically or by topic, depending on what is most logical
  - Clear, simple language
  - Specific details (dates, times, amounts, names, places)
  - Reference to documents where relevant ("as shown in the email of [DATE]")
  - Direct quotes where significant ("The defendant said to me, '[QUOTE]'")
  - Acknowledgment of uncertainty where appropriate ("I don't recall the exact date, but it was sometime in March")

- Key elements:
  - How the witness knows the facts they are stating (direct observation, participation, documentation, communication from another person)
  - What is the significance of these facts (how do they support the point being made)
  - Supporting documentation (references to exhibits)
  - Credibility markers (I was present, I maintain records, this happened recently so my memory is fresh)

- Length:
  - Statements should be concise but complete (typically 5-15 pages, depending on complexity)
  - Avoid unnecessary background or repetition
  - Focus on facts directly relevant to the issue

- Verification:
  - Final paragraph: "I make this statement knowing that I may be prosecuted for perjury if it is untrue."
  - Sign and date the statement
  - Have it witnessed (commissioner for oaths, notary, or attested by a lawyer)

- Use of statement:
  - Statements are typically used to support interlocutory applications (rather than trial evidence, which would be AEIC format)
  - Statements should be evidence, not argument (present facts; let the court draw conclusions)
  - After trial, statements may be converted to AEICs if the witness is called to give evidence

Include guidance on the difference between witness statements (for interim applications) and AEICs (for trial), and best practices for statement preparation.

Context: When you need concise, verified statements from witnesses to support an interlocutory application or to gather evidence early in proceedings.
Difficulty: Intermediate
Best AI tool: Claude
Follow-up: PROMPT 026 (Draft AEIC) or PROMPT 029 (Cross-Examination Preparation)

## EVIDENCE MANAGEMENT

**PROMPT 031 — Chronology & Timeline (Events, Communications, Transactions)**

Prepare a detailed chronology of events relating to [DESCRIBE MATTER: e.g., the plaintiff's dealing with the defendant, the defendant's course of conduct, development of the loss]. The chronology should cover the period from [START DATE] to [END DATE] and include [SPECIFY ELEMENTS: communications, transactions, meetings, decisions, observations].

Please prepare:
- Format:
  - Table format or narrative, depending on preference
  - Each entry should show: Date (DD/MM/YYYY), Time (if relevant), Event/Action, Source of information/Document reference
  - Alternative: narrative format organized chronologically, with dates clearly marked

- Table format example:
  - Column 1: Date (chronological order)
  - Column 2: Time (if significant)
  - Column 3: Event (who, what, where)
  - Column 4: Source/Document reference (email, meeting minutes, phone record, witness account)
  - Column 5: Notes (significance, impact)

- Content to include:
  - Contractual events: date of contract signing, when performance was due, when variations were agreed
  - Communications: key emails, letters, phone calls, meetings (date, who participated, what was discussed, outcome)
  - Transactions: payments made/received, deliveries, completions, transfers
  - Decisions or actions: decisions made by either party, actions taken (or not taken), changes in circumstances
  - Events observed by witness: observations relevant to the issue
  - Problem events: breaches, missed deadlines, performance failures, disputes

- Accuracy and verification:
  - Be accurate with dates (check documents, don't guess)
  - Use exact dates where available, acknowledge estimated dates where not ("approximately [DATE]")
  - Source each item: "Email from [SENDER] to [RECIPIENT]", "Meeting between [PARTIES]", "Bank statement showing [TRANSACTION]"
  - If relying on witness memory, acknowledge this: "According to witness X, [EVENT] occurred on approximately [DATE]"

- Scope and boundaries:
  - Define the time period covered
  - Define what events are included (all events or only events relevant to the dispute)
  - Include contextual events that help explain the significance of later events
  - Exclude purely background events that don't affect the issues

- Multiple strands:
  - If there are multiple parallel strands of events, consider separate chronologies or color-coding/numbering to separate them
  - Example: parallel chronologies of the plaintiff's performance, the defendant's performance, and the parties' communications

- Visual presentation:
  - Consider creating a visual timeline (if particularly complex)
  - Graph or chart showing progression of events, decision points, transitions
  - Highlighting critical events or decision points

- Cross-referencing:
  - Link the chronology to documents and exhibits
  - Use consistent document numbering (e.g., "DOC-00001: Email from X to Y")
  - Create an index linking chronology entries to supporting documents

- Uses of the chronology:
  - Tool for analyzing the case (helps identify patterns, contradictions, gaps)
  - Tool for preparing evidence (ensuring all key dates are covered in AEIC or statements)
  - Evidence presented to court (chronology helps judge understand the sequence of events)
  - Presentation at trial (chronology presented to judge, used in opening and closing submissions)

- Accuracy checks:
  - Review all dates against source documents
  - Check for internal consistency (events are in logical sequence, no impossible gaps)
  - Ensure no events are missing that would affect the narrative
  - Test against the other party's version (are there disputes about dates/events that should be noted)

Include a sample chronology covering a common dispute scenario (construction contract dispute, commercial contract breach, partnership dissolution) to illustrate best practices.

Context: When you need to organize complex events over time into a clear, document-supported timeline for analysis and presentation.
Difficulty: Intermediate
Best AI tool: Claude
Follow-up: PROMPT 026 (Draft AEIC) or PROMPT 032 (Evidence Matrix)

**PROMPT 032 — Evidence Matrix (Issue Analysis & Evidence Mapping)**

Prepare an evidence matrix mapping the relationship between key issues in dispute and the evidence supporting or contradicting each issue. The evidence matrix should identify [SPECIFY]: which party bears the burden of proof for each issue, what evidence supports each party's position, what evidence is problematic or contradicts a party's case.

Please prepare:
- Structure:
  - Rows: key issues, facts, or allegations in dispute (drawn from the pleadings)
  - Columns: Plaintiff's position/evidence, Defendant's position/evidence, Assessment of strength, Key documents, Key witnesses
  - Additional columns: legal burden of proof, evidentiary weight, credibility issues

- Completion for each issue:
  - Issue: clearly state the factual or legal issue in dispute
  - Plaintiff's position: what does the plaintiff allege? What is their version of events?
  - Plaintiff's evidence: what evidence supports the plaintiff's position? (documents, witness accounts, expert evidence, admissions)
  - Defendant's position: what does the defendant say? What is their version or explanation?
  - Defendant's evidence: what evidence supports the defendant's position?
  - Assessment: on the current evidence, which side's position is stronger? What further evidence would be helpful?
  - Key documents: which documents are most important to resolving this issue?
  - Key witnesses: whose evidence is critical to establishing the facts?
  - Evidentiary gaps: what evidence is missing or would be helpful?

- Sample issue matrix:
  - Issue: "Whether the defendant breached the contract by failing to deliver goods by 31 December 2023"
  - Plaintiff's position: "Defendant agreed to deliver by 31 December; goods were not delivered until 15 January 2024"
  - Plaintiff's evidence: Contract terms (Exhibit A), correspondence requesting delivery (Exhibit B), delivery confirmation dated 15 January 2024 (Exhibit C), witness evidence from X who followed up on the delivery
  - Defendant's position: "Delivery date was extended to 15 January by agreement; plaintiff agreed to the extension"
  - Defendant's evidence: Email of [DATE] from plaintiff agreeing to extension, witness evidence from Y who was present in meeting where extension was agreed
  - Assessment: Issue turns on whether oral extension was agreed. Email evidence supports defendant's position. Plaintiff will argue the extension was oral and not binding. Turns on credibility of witnesses.
  - Key documents: Email dated [DATE], any other communications about the deadline
  - Key witnesses: Y (defendant's witness re the alleged agreement), X (plaintiff's witness re the lack of extension)
  - Evidentiary gaps: No documentary evidence of the agreed extension date from plaintiff's perspective. Further evidence of what was communicated between the parties around [DATE] would be helpful.

- Burden of proof:
  - For each issue, identify who bears the burden
  - Civil litigation: balance of probabilities (whichever side's evidence is stronger)
  - Identify issues where one party bears a particularly heavy burden (e.g., fraud allegations require strong evidence; breach of implied term of good faith requires careful analysis)

- Evidentiary weight assessment:

**PROMPT 033 — Document Review for Discovery: Identifying Responsive & Privileged Documents**

Prepare a document review protocol for identifying responsive documents in discovery relating to [DESCRIBE SCOPE: the plaintiff's claim for breach of contract, the defendant's losses, communications regarding [TOPIC]].

Please prepare:
- Scope definition:
  - What time period is covered by the discovery request?
  - What categories of documents are requested?
  - Who are the relevant custodians (whose documents need to be reviewed)?
  - What locations (email servers, file servers, archives, external media)?

- Relevance assessment:
  - Responsive documents: documents that fall within the scope of the discovery request (date range, subject matter, custodians)
  - Relevance assessment: is the document relevant to a matter in issue in the case?
  - Materiality: is the document material (does it significantly affect the case) or merely tangentially relevant?
  - Approach: be pragmatic; if a document might be relevant, produce it (courts are more critical of documents improperly withheld than of documents produced that are marginally relevant)

- Privilege assessment:
  - Legal advice privilege: is the document a communication between client and lawyer seeking or giving legal advice?
  - Litigation privilege: was the document created for the dominant purpose of conducting or giving advice about litigation?
  - Without prejudice: is the document a settlement communication marked "without prejudice"?
  - Joint interest privilege: is the document a communication between parties with a common legal interest?
  - Waiver: has the party waived privilege (by disclosing the document to third parties or putting the contents in issue)?
  - If privileged: maintain a privilege log (listing privileged documents with reasons for the privilege claim)

- Confidential information:
  - Is the document confidential (trade secret, commercially sensitive information)?
  - Can the document be produced with the confidential information redacted?
  - Should a confidentiality order be sought to protect the document?
  - Approach: produce documents unless there is a strong reason not to (confidentiality orders can protect sensitive information)

- Third-party issues:
  - Does the document contain personal information about someone other than the parties (customer information, employee information)?
  - Can personal information be redacted?
  - Are there privacy concerns that justify withholding?
  - Approach: redact personal information where possible, otherwise produce subject to confidentiality order or seek specific exemption

- Document coding:
  - Responsive (Yes/No)
  - Privilege claimed (Yes/No, and what type)
  - Confidential (Yes/No)
  - Key document (Yes/No)
  - Further notes

- Quality assurance:
  - Spot-check reviewed documents (sample 5% and verify coding is correct)
  - Monitor for consistency (similar documents should receive similar coding)
  - Track issues that arise (difficult documents, close calls on responsiveness/privilege)
  - Escalate difficult decisions to reviewing counsel

- Timeline for review:
  - Small volumes (<10,000 docs): 2-3 weeks
  - Medium volumes (10,000-50,000 docs): 3-6 weeks
  - Large volumes (>50,000 docs): 6-12 weeks (or use technology-assisted review)

Include a detailed protocol for reviewing documents efficiently and consistently, and best practices for ensuring all responsive documents are identified and produced while protecting privileged and confidential information.

**PROMPT 034 — Hearsay Analysis & Admissibility Assessment**

Analyze the hearsay implications of evidence in [DESCRIBE MATTER], particularly regarding [SPECIFY EVIDENCE TYPE: witness statements referring to what others said, communications between parties, reports relying on other sources]. Assess what evidence can be relied on in court and what requires specific handling.

Please prepare:
- Definition of hearsay:
  - Hearsay: an out-of-court statement offered for the truth of its contents
  - In litigation: if a witness says "Person X told me that [FACT]", and the evidence is offered to prove that [FACT] is true, this is hearsay
  - Hearsay is generally not admissible unless an exception applies

- Exceptions to the hearsay rule (in Singapore):
  - Admission by a party: statements made by a party are admissible against them (e.g., defendant's admission in a letter that they breached the contract)
  - Business records: documents made in the course of business are generally admissible as an exception to hearsay
  - Public records: government records and records made in pursuance of public duty
  - Dying declarations: statements made by someone believing they are about to die
  - Statements in the presence of a party: if a party hears a statement and doesn't dispute it, their failure to dispute may be admissible as a tacit admission
  - Expert opinion: experts can rely on hearsay in forming their opinions (e.g., an accountant can rely on financial records)
  - Statements of present state of mind: statements about the speaker's current intentions, beliefs, or emotions

- Assessing evidence for hearsay problems:
  - Is the evidence being offered to prove the truth of what was said/written?
  - If yes, is it an admission by a party? (If so, admissible)
  - If not an admission, does an exception apply? (Business records, public records, etc.)
  - If no exception, is the evidence inadmissible hearsay?

- Handling hearsay evidence:
  - Avoid relying on hearsay in AEIC (the witness should testify to direct knowledge, not what others told them)
  - If the witness must refer to what others said, be clear: "X told me that [FACT]" and acknowledge this is hearsay
  - Call the original speaker as a witness if possible (avoids hearsay problem entirely)
  - If the original speaker is unavailable, explain the unavailability and consider whether an exception to hearsay applies
  - In expert evidence, hearsay can be relied on (expert can base opinion on data from others)

- Common hearsay traps:
  - Witness statement: "The defendant told me he breached the contract" — this is hearsay (witness is reporting what defendant said) but it may be an admission by the defendant if the defendant's admission is being offered
  - Email chain: "X wrote in the email that [FACT]" — if X is the sender and the email is offered to show X's statement, it's hearsay unless X is a party or the exception applies
  - Third-party report: "The consultant reported that [FINDING]" — this is hearsay; admissibility depends on whether the consultant's report is a business record exception

- Strategic considerations:
  - Plan early: identify what hearsay evidence you will rely on, whether exceptions apply, whether you need to call witnesses
  - Call key witnesses: if a piece of evidence is critical and is hearsay without an exception, calling the original source as a witness is preferable
  - Use business records: where available, business records are likely to be admissible as an exception to hearsay
  - Expert reliance: ensure experts are relying on reliable data (if an expert relies on hearsay that is unreliable or inadmissible, this affects the reliability of the expert's opinion)

- Documents and admissions:
  - A party's own documents (emails, letters, business records created by the party) are generally admissible against them as admissions
  - Ensure these documents are properly authenticated (show who created them, that they are genuine)

Include guidance on how to structure evidence to avoid hearsay problems, and how to handle necessary hearsay in the context of the evidence rules and litigation strategy.

Context: When reviewing evidence and need to assess whether it is admissible or hearsay, and how to handle hearsay issues strategically.
Difficulty: Intermediate
Best AI tool: Claude
Follow-up: PROMPT 026 (Draft AEIC) or PROMPT 028 (Expert Report)

**PROMPT 035 — Evidence Bundle Preparation (Organizing Exhibits for Court)**

Prepare an evidence bundle for [DESCRIBE MATTER], collecting and organizing all documents and exhibits that will be submitted to the court. The bundle should cover [SPECIFY SCOPE: evidence supporting the plaintiff's claim, defendant's defence, expert evidence] and comprise approximately [NUMBER] pages of documents.

Please prepare:
- Bundle structure:
  - Tabs and dividers: organize documents into logical sections (chronological, by issue, by document type)
  - Numbering: each page should be numbered (usually at the bottom right)
  - Exhibit references: exhibits should be referenced in the AEIC or statement (e.g., "Exhibit A", "Exhibit B")
  - Index: create an index listing all exhibits with page numbers and brief description
  - Table of contents: overview of the bundle structure

- Organization approach:
  - Chronological: if the matter is chronological in nature, organize documents in order of date
  - By issue: if multiple issues, separate sections by issue
  - By document type: all correspondence together, all financial documents together, etc.
  - Hybrid: combination of above, depending on the complexity

- Document preparation:
  - Authenticity: ensure all documents are authentic (originals or certified copies where possible)
  - Quality: ensure copies are clear and legible (poor quality copies are unhelpful)
  - Highlighting: highlight key passages or sentences (but sparingly — over-highlighting makes documents harder to read)
  - Annotations: add brief annotations to help the judge understand the significance (e.g., "Note: X admits liability in this email")
  - Translation: if documents are in a foreign language, provide certified translations
  - Redaction: redact confidential or sensitive information if appropriate (but sparingly and only if justified)

- Exhibit list:
  - Create a detailed exhibit list with: exhibit number/letter, date, brief description, page numbers in bundle
  - Ensure the exhibit list matches references in the AEIC/statements (if the AEIC refers to "Exhibit A", ensure Exhibit A is actually included)

- Volume and pagination:
  - Keep bundles to a manageable size (typically under 500 pages for a hearing, up to 1000+ pages for trial)
  - If the bundle is very large, consider splitting into separate bundles by issue or party
  - Ensure page numbering is consecutive and consistent throughout

- Agreed bundles:
  - In many cases, the parties can agree on an evidence bundle (saving time and cost)
  - An agreed bundle is typically prepared by the plaintiff's solicitors and circulated to the defendant's solicitors for agreement
  - Both parties sign off on the bundle, confirming the documents are authentic and in the agreed form

- Electronic bundles:
  - For modern litigation, bundles are often prepared in electronic format (PDF)
  - Electronic bundles allow for hyperlinks, bookmarks, and searchability (helpful for judges)
  - Ensure the electronic bundle is properly formatted and tested for functionality

- Presentation:
  - Consider the judge's ease of use (clear numbering, good organization, quick access to key documents)
  - Consider color-coding or tabs for easy navigation (though keep it professional)
  - Include page numbers and running headers/footers to help navigate large bundles

- Common errors to avoid:
  - Missing documents: ensure all exhibits referenced in AEIC are actually included in the bundle
  - Wrong documents: ensure exhibits are correctly labeled and correspond to their references
  - Poor quality: ensure copies are legible and clear
  - Inconsistent pagination: ensure page numbers are sequential and correct
  - No index: make it easy for the judge to find things in the bundle

Include a sample exhibit list and index for a typical civil dispute (contract dispute with correspondence, financial documents, and expert reports).

Context: When finalizing evidence for submission to the court and needing to organize all exhibits in a clear, accessible bundle format.
Difficulty: Intermediate
