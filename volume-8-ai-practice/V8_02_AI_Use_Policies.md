# AI Use Policies for Law Firms & Legal Departments

## Overview
This section provides templates and frameworks for creating firm-wide AI governance policies, training programs, and governance structures aligned with Singapore Law Society standards and international best practices.

---

## SECTION 1: Policy Development & Governance

**PROMPT 26 — Drafting a Firm-Wide AI Acceptable Use Policy**

Create a comprehensive template "Acceptable Use of Artificial Intelligence" policy that a law firm can adopt to govern how all staff use AI tools. The policy should include: (1) Policy scope and applicability: clearly stating that this policy applies to all lawyers, paralegals, support staff, and contractors using any AI tool in connection with firm work, and defining what constitutes "use" (entering confidential information, generating work product, conducting research, using AI in client deliverables), (2) Objectives: outlining why the firm has an AI policy (ensuring ethical use, protecting client confidentiality, maintaining quality, complying with Law Society rules, managing risks, protecting the firm's reputation), (3) Permitted use: describing what AI uses are permitted: (a) legal research and case law analysis (with verification requirements), (b) contract and document review (with human review before client delivery), (c) drafting assistance (AI generates draft, lawyer completes and verifies), (d) administrative tasks (scheduling, document organization), (e) professional development (learning about AI), and (f) client permission matters (when client consents), (4) Prohibited use: clearly prohibiting: (a) inputting confidential client information into public AI tools without client consent, (b) using AI to make final legal decisions without human lawyer review, (c) disclosing privileged attorney-client communications to third-party vendors, (d) using AI in matters where the lawyer lacks competence to supervise the AI, (e) relying on AI outputs for citations without verification, (f) using AI to draft court filings without disclosing AI use if required, (g) using AI for administrative tasks involving personal data unless data is properly protected, (h) using unauthorized AI tools (tools not approved by the firm), (5) Disclosure requirements: requiring staff to: (a) disclose AI use in client engagement letters (if AI will contribute significantly), (b) tell clients when AI was used in their matter, (c) obtain client consent before using AI (or rely on engagement letter consent if provided), (d) disclose to courts if required by rules of procedure, (6) Verification & quality assurance: mandating that: (a) all AI outputs must be reviewed by a lawyer before delivery to clients, (b) AI-generated legal cites must be verified against primary sources, (c) AI drafts must be checked for accuracy, completeness, and client suitability, (d) QA documentation must be completed and retained, (7) Data protection: requiring staff to: (a) never input unredacted personal data into AI tools unless the tool is approved and a DPA is in place, (b) minimize confidential information input (use examples, summaries, anonymization where possible), (c) check the AI tool's privacy policy before use, (d) assume any data entered into public AI may be used for vendor training unless contractually prohibited, (8) Approved tools: listing which AI tools the firm has approved for different purposes: (a) ChatGPT Enterprise (legal research, drafting), (b) Claude via Anthropic's enterprise API (legal analysis), (c) Microsoft Copilot (research), (d) specialized legal AI tools, and which tools are prohibited or require special approval, (9) Training & competence: requiring all staff to: (a) complete mandatory AI training before using any tool, (b) demonstrate competence in using the tool safely, (c) understand AI limitations and hallucination risks, (d) stay current with policy updates, (10) Consequences of non-compliance: explaining that violations can result in disciplinary action ranging from retraining to termination, and that the firm may be liable for staff non-compliance. Include implementation guidance, staff acknowledgment forms, and training materials.

Context: Use when establishing or updating firm AI policies; customize for your firm's practice areas and risk profile
Difficulty: Intermediate
Best AI tool: Claude / Copilot

**PROMPT 27 — Building a Comprehensive AI Risk Framework for Law Firms**

Design a risk management framework for law firms to identify, assess, prioritize, and mitigate AI-related risks across all business functions. The framework should include: (1) Risk categories: organizing risks into categories: (a) operational risks (tool failures, data loss, service interruptions), (b) security risks (unauthorized access, data breaches, cyberattacks), (c) compliance risks (Law Society violations, PDPA breaches, regulatory penalties), (d) liability risks (professional negligence, malpractice, claims), (e) reputational risks (client loss, market perception, media coverage), (f) strategic risks (competitive disadvantage, market disruption), (g) quality risks (AI errors, hallucinations, missed issues), (2) Risk identification process: establishing a process to identify risks: (a) surveying staff about AI concerns, (b) analyzing firm data (complaints, errors, near-misses), (c) reviewing industry incidents (cases where legal AI caused problems), (d) assessing each AI tool adopted (what could go wrong?), (e) consulting with experts (legal ethics advisors, cybersecurity consultants), (3) Risk assessment: for each identified risk, assessing: (a) likelihood (probability of the risk materializing: high/medium/low), (b) impact (severity if the risk occurs: high/medium/low), (c) overall risk score (likelihood x impact), (d) current controls (what existing controls help manage this risk?), (e) residual risk (how much risk remains after current controls?), (4) Risk prioritization: prioritizing risks by overall risk score, focusing first on high-risk items, (5) Risk mitigation strategies: for each material risk, developing mitigation strategies: (a) prevention (eliminate the risk), (b) detection (catch the risk early if it occurs), (c) response (respond quickly to minimize damage), (d) recovery (restore operations), (6) Specific AI risks to address: including: (a) AI hallucination causing malpractice → mitigated by verification protocols, QA review, training, (b) confidentiality breach when inputting client data into AI → mitigated by data redaction, vendor DPAs, client consent, (c) AI tool service interruption → mitigated by backup processes, multiple vendor tools, (d) AI bias affecting advice → mitigated by bias detection training, diverse review panels, (e) staff lacking AI competence → mitigated by training, competence assessments, supervision, (f) non-compliance with Law Society rules → mitigated by policies, regular audits, compliance monitoring, (7) Governance structure: assigning ownership and accountability: (a) AI governance committee (who are members?), (b) Risk owners (who owns each risk?), (c) Decision authority (who decides on risk tolerance?), (d) Escalation paths (if risk score becomes unacceptable, who is notified?), (8) Monitoring & reporting: establishing processes to: (a) monitor risks on an ongoing basis (quarterly reviews?), (b) track risk incidents and near-misses, (c) report to firm leadership/board regularly on risk status, (d) adjust mitigation strategies based on new information, (9) Integration with other systems: connecting AI risk management to: (a) professional indemnity insurance (ensuring coverage), (b) compliance audit programs (checking adherence), (c) CPD & training (developing competence), (d) IT security & infrastructure, (10) Documentation: maintaining a risk register (discussed in PROMPT 14) capturing all identified risks, assessments, mitigations, ownership, and status. Provide a risk assessment matrix template and governance charter.

Context: Use in firm governance; basis for developing other policies and procedures
Difficulty: Advanced

**PROMPT 28 — Creating AI Training Materials for Law Firm Staff**

Develop a comprehensive set of training materials (outlines, slides, scripts, quizzes) for educating all law firm staff about responsible AI use. The training should be modular, allowing different content for different roles (partners, senior lawyers, associates, paralegals, support staff). The training should cover: (1) Module 1—AI Fundamentals: What is AI? (Introduction to machine learning, large language models, how ChatGPT/Claude work), How does legal AI differ from other AI?, Why are lawyers using AI? (efficiency, research, drafting), What are AI limitations? (no internet access, hallucinations, training data cutoff, bias), (2) Module 2—Hallucinations & Accuracy: What is a hallucination? (AI inventing facts, wrong citations, false case law), Why do hallucinations happen? (AI statistical pattern matching, not true reasoning), Real examples of legal hallucinations (case studies), How to detect hallucinations (red flags), How to prevent hallucinations (verification protocols), (3) Module 3—Ethics & Professional Conduct: Law Society rules about AI use (Code of Professional Conduct), Disclosure obligations (when must you tell clients/courts about AI use?), Confidentiality risks (protecting client information), Verification responsibilities (you are responsible for AI accuracy, not the AI vendor), Competence obligations (you must be competent to use AI), (4) Module 4—Data Protection & Confidentiality: PDPA obligations when using AI (Personal Data Protection Act), Confidential information risks (what happens if you feed client data into an AI tool?), Privilege risks (does using AI waive attorney-client privilege?), Vendor assessment (how to evaluate if an AI tool is safe for confidential work), (5) Module 5—Practical AI Workflows: Safe AI research process (multi-step verification), AI for contract analysis (how to review AI suggestions), AI for drafting (generating a draft vs. completing the work), Quality assurance checklists (what to verify before delivering), (6) Module 6—Common Mistakes & Case Studies: Analysis of real incidents (where AI caused problems, what went wrong, how to prevent), Scenario-based learning (if this situation occurs, what should you do?), Problem-solving exercises, (7) Module 7—Role-Specific Content: For Partners: oversight, supervision, governance, tool selection, For Associates: appropriate use, disclosure, verification, For Paralegals: research, drafting support, documentation, For Support Staff: administrative use, data protection, (8) Module 8—Firm Policies & Procedures: Overview of firm's AI policy, Approved vs. prohibited tools, Data handling requirements, Escalation procedures, Where to get help if uncertain, (9) Assessment & Certification: quizzes for each module to verify learning, practical exercises applying concepts, sign-off/certification confirming staff understand and will comply with policies, (10) Refresher & Continuing Education: scheduling annual refresher training, updating training as policies evolve, providing alerts about new risks (e.g., a new AI hallucination incident occurs), encouraging continuing professional development in AI. Provide detailed outlines, PowerPoint slide templates, facilitator scripts, and assessment tools.

Context: Use to onboard staff to AI policies; mandatory for anyone using AI; refreshed annually
Difficulty: Intermediate
Best AI tool: Claude / Copilot

**PROMPT 29 — Establishing an AI Governance Committee: Terms of Reference**

Create a template "Terms of Reference" (charter) for an AI Governance Committee that a law firm can establish to oversee responsible AI adoption and use. The TOR should address: (1) Committee purpose: clearly stating the committee's role is to: (a) develop, implement, and maintain AI governance policies and procedures, (b) assess new AI tools before firm adoption, (c) monitor AI use and compliance with policies, (d) manage AI-related risks, (e) respond to AI incidents and complaints, (f) stay current with legal and regulatory developments related to AI, (g) drive responsible AI innovation, (2) Membership: specifying who serves on the committee: (a) mandatory: managing partner or partner delegate, general counsel or compliance officer, practice area leaders (litigation, corporate, advisory), IT/security officer, human resources representative, (b) optional: external advisors (legal ethics expert, technology consultant), (c) number of members (typically 5-9), term length (2 years?), (3) Roles within the committee: designating: (a) committee chair (responsible for scheduling meetings, setting agendas, driving execution), (b) secretary (documenting decisions, tracking action items), (c) risk owner (responsible for maintaining AI risk register), (d) tool evaluator (responsible for vendor assessments), (4) Responsibilities: detailing what the committee does: (a) develops and updates AI policies and procedures, (b) evaluates new AI tools before adoption (using vendor evaluation checklist from PROMPT 22), (c) makes decisions on tool approval/prohibition, (d) monitors firm-wide AI compliance, (e) receives incident reports and manages response, (f) conducts quarterly risk assessments, (g) provides feedback to staff on AI governance, (h) advises firm leadership on AI strategic decisions, (i) stays informed on Law Society guidance and regulatory changes, (j) coordinates CPD and training on AI, (5) Decision authority: clarifying what decisions the committee can make and what requires escalation: (a) committee can approve/prohibit tools, update policies, approve training, (b) managing partner approval needed for budget/resource allocation, Law Society referral, major policy changes, (c) firm leadership approval needed for strategic AI initiatives, significant vendor contracts, (6) Scope limitations: clarifying what the committee does NOT do: (a) the committee does not make individual legal work decisions (lawyers decide what tools to use for specific matters), (b) the committee does not hire/fire staff (HR does), (c) the committee does not investigate complaints (compliance team does, committee receives reports), (7) Meeting schedule: establishing: (a) frequency of meetings (monthly? quarterly?), (b) quorum requirements (how many members must attend for valid decisions?), (c) regular agenda items: compliance updates, incident reports, tool evaluation, risk assessment, policy updates, (d) ad-hoc meetings if urgent issues arise, (8) Reporting: determining: (a) who the committee reports to (managing partner? Board?), (b) what reporting is done (quarterly reports on AI governance status?), (c) escalation pathways for urgent issues, (9) Resources: identifying: (a) budget for tool evaluation, training, external advisors, (b) staff support (secretary, analyst to gather compliance data), (c) access to systems and information needed (vendor agreements, compliance data, incident logs), (10) Review and amendment: the TOR itself should be reviewed annually and amended as needed, (11) Confidentiality: noting that committee discussions may involve sensitive compliance and risk information, requiring confidentiality. Provide a template TOR and sample committee charter.

Context: Use to establish firm AI governance structure; essential for larger firms and those with more than minimal AI use
Difficulty: Intermediate

**PROMPT 30 — Data Classification System for AI Use in Legal Practice**

Create a data classification framework that helps lawyers and staff determine whether specific data can be safely used with AI tools. The framework should include: (1) Classification tiers: establishing 4-5 tiers of data sensitivity: (a) Tier 1—Public Data: information that is public and non-sensitive (general legal information, published cases, public statutes, general market information), can be freely used with any AI tool, (b) Tier 2—Internal Data: firm's own non-confidential information (standard forms, firm policies, organizational charts), generally safe to use with approved tools but check for any sensitive elements, (c) Tier 3—Confidential Client Data: confidential client information not covered by privilege (client contact information, transaction amounts, deal timelines), can be used with approved/secure AI tools with client consent and after redaction, (d) Tier 4—Highly Sensitive Client Data: highly sensitive information (health/medical information, financial account numbers, passport/ID numbers, DNA information, specific criminal charges), generally should not be input into AI without strong justification, redaction, anonymization, and explicit client consent, (e) Tier 5—Privileged Information: attorney-client privileged communications, work product, attorney mental impressions, should generally not be input into AI tools due to privilege waiver risk, except with enterprise tools and strong contractual protections, (2) Classification factors: for each piece of information, considering: (a) is it publicly available? (public data = lower sensitivity), (b) does the client consider it confidential? (client's own confidential information = higher sensitivity), (c) is it sensitive personal data? (health, finance, ID = higher sensitivity), (d) is it privileged? (attorney-client communications = highest sensitivity), (e) what would be the harm if disclosed? (identity theft risk, business harm, legal liability = higher sensitivity), (f) does the person whose information it is have privacy interests? (their personal data = higher sensitivity), (3) Decision tree: establishing a clear decision tree: (a) Is the information already public? → Yes: Tier 1, proceed with any tool, No: continue, (b) Is it your firm's non-confidential information? → Yes: Tier 2, can use with approved tools, No: continue, (c) Is it confidential but non-privileged client/third-party information? → Yes: Tier 3/4 depending on sensitivity, (d) Is it privileged or attorney work product? → Yes: Tier 5, avoid AI or use only with highest-security tools, (4) Use guidance for each tier: (a) Tier 1: use freely with any AI tool, (b) Tier 2: use with approved/secure tools, document use, (c) Tier 3: redact sensitive identifiers (names, accounts, specific amounts), anonymize, get client consent, use approved/secure tools only, (d) Tier 4: consult with privacy/compliance officer before use, requires explicit client written consent, minimal input, maximum anonymization, most secure tools only, (e) Tier 5: generally avoid, only use if strong business justification, enterprise tool with highest security/confidentiality commitments, explicit client consent, (5) Redaction guidelines: for Tier 3/4 information: (a) remove client names (use "Client" or "Party A"), (b) remove specific amounts (use "significant amount" or "X figure"), (c) remove dates if not essential (use "recent" or "in 2024"), (d) remove account numbers, IDs, passwords, (e) remove health/medical details if not essential to analysis, (f) preserve enough context for AI to provide useful analysis while minimizing identifiable information, (6) Documentation: for all Tier 3/4 uses, documenting: (a) what information was classified as Tier 3/4?, (b) why was AI use necessary?, (c) what redaction/anonymization was done?, (d) what was the client consent (if required)?, (e) who reviewed for compliance?, (7) Examples by practice area: (a) Corporate M&A: deal structures, preliminary valuations (Tier 2-3), client identities, financial information (Tier 4), negotiation strategy (Tier 5), (b) Family law: client relationship details, children information, financial assets (Tier 4), (c) Criminal defense: client charges, victim information, privileged attorney strategy (Tier 5), (d) Employment law: employee names and details (Tier 3-4), health information (Tier 4), settlement terms (Tier 3), (8) Tool matching: matching tools to data tiers: (a) Tier 1-2: any tool (including free ChatGPT), (b) Tier 3: approved tools with reasonable security (ChatGPT Enterprise, Claude API, Copilot), (c) Tier 4: secure/enterprise tools with DPAs and highest confidentiality (private Claude instance, enterprise Copilot, custom solutions), (d) Tier 5: avoid AI or external enterprise tools with maximum protections, (9) Regular review: data classification framework should be reviewed annually and updated as: (a) new AI tools are adopted (may change acceptable tiers), (b) legal regulations change (PDPA updates), (c) incidents occur (learning from breaches), (d) business practices change (new practice areas, new matter types), (10) Training: all staff must be trained on data classification: (a) understanding tiers, (b) classifying common information types, (c) implementing redaction/anonymization, (d) selecting appropriate tools for each tier. Provide a classification flowchart, data tier guide, redaction checklists, and staff training slides.

Context: Use to guide daily decisions about AI tool use; ensure all staff understand before using any AI
Difficulty: Intermediate
Best AI tool: Claude / Copilot

**PROMPT 31 — AI Incident Response Procedures & Escalation Protocol**

Create a detailed incident response procedure that specifies what staff must do if an AI-related incident occurs (data breach, hallucination discovered, vendor tool failure, security incident). The procedure should include: (1) What constitutes an incident: defining incidents: (a) hallucination discovery (AI error affecting work product), (b) data breach or security incident, (c) unauthorized AI use (someone using a prohibited tool), (d) policy violation (confidential information improperly input into AI), (e) tool failure/unavailability affecting client work, (f) vendor misconduct (vendor using your data for their own purposes), (g) client complaint related to AI use, (h) Law Society inquiry about AI practices, (2) Immediate response (first 24 hours): (a) upon discovering an incident, immediately reporting to: designated incident response officer, managing partner (if serious), IT security (if breach), (b) documenting the incident: what happened, when, who discovered it, what data/work is affected, (c) containing the incident: stopping any ongoing breach (revoke access, shut down unauthorized tool), preserving evidence, avoiding further damage, (d) initial assessment: is this urgent/crisis-level or routine?, does it affect client matters? Does it require external notification? (3) Incident severity assessment: establishing a severity scale: (a) Critical: client confidential data breached, vendor tool breaches/mishandles data, AI produced grossly incorrect legal work affecting multiple matters → immediate response, executive involvement, client notification, external counsel consultation, (b) High: confidential client information input into unapproved AI tool (but not yet breached), AI hallucination affecting significant client work → response within 24 hours, senior lawyer involvement, (c) Medium: minor AI error caught before delivery, policy violation (AI use not initially disclosed but correctable) → response within 48-72 hours, supervisor involvement, (d) Low: minor violations, questions about policy → routine handling, departmental level response, (4) Investigation phase: after initial response, conducting investigation: (a) what caused the incident? (technology failure, human error, vendor fault?), (b) was policy violated or just an accident?, (c) how did the incident occur? (detailed root cause analysis), (d) how many people/matters affected?, (e) who knew about the problem and when? (for accountability), (f) was the incident preventable?, (5) Stakeholder notification: determining who must be notified: (a) Affected clients: (i) Critical severity → notify immediately, (ii) High severity → notify within 24 hours, (iii) Provide transparency, take responsibility, explain impact, offer remediation, (b) Professional indemnity insurer: notify for any incident that might lead to a claim (insurer may designate defense counsel), (c) Vendor/AI provider: if their tool/service caused the incident, demand explanation, invoke remedy clauses, (d) Law Society: if the incident constitutes professional misconduct, may need to self-report (depends on severity and impact), (e) Regulators: if data breach involving personal data, may need to report to PDPC under PDPA, (f) Internal stakeholders: managing partner, governance committee, compliance officer, (6) Documentation: maintaining comprehensive incident record: (a) incident report form (completed by person who discovered it), (b) investigation notes (detailed findings from root cause analysis), (c) remediation plan (steps taken to fix and prevent recurrence), (d) stakeholder notifications (copies of all notices sent), (e) evidence preservation (logs, emails, screenshots), (f) timeline (when each step was taken), (7) Remediation & response: for each incident type: (a) Hallucination: correct the work product, notify client, redo the work if necessary, investigate why hallucination wasn't caught in QA, (b) Data breach: secure systems, assess harm, notify affected parties, implement security improvements, consider vendor change, (c) Unauthorized use: discipline staff member, retrain on policies, audit for similar violations, (d) Policy violation: corrective action (warning, retraining), escalation if repeated, (e) Tool failure: switch to backup tool/process, notify clients if work delayed, fix/replace tool, (8) Follow-up and lessons learned: after resolving incident: (a) conduct post-incident review/debrief with relevant staff, (b) identify process improvements (what could prevent this?), (c) implement improvements: policy updates, training, tool changes, supervision enhancements, (d) communicate learnings to staff (what happened, how we're fixing it), (e) document lessons for future reference, (9) External escalation: for serious incidents, consulting external advisors: (a) law firm counsel (independent outside counsel to advise on response), (b) cybersecurity experts (for data breaches), (c) PR consultants (for reputational management), (10) Escalation matrix: defining triggers for escalation: (a) any Critical incident → immediately to managing partner/executive committee, (b) any incident affecting multiple clients → governance committee, (c) any potential Law Society issue → compliance officer/external counsel, (d) any data breach → insurer + cybersecurity experts. Provide incident report forms, escalation flowcharts, and notification templates.

Context: Use proactively to establish procedures before incidents occur; implement immediately after first incident
Difficulty: Intermediate

**PROMPT 32 — AI Audit Trail Requirements & Monitoring Systems**

Develop a framework for establishing AI audit trail and monitoring systems that law firms can implement to track AI usage, demonstrate compliance, and detect anomalies. The framework should include: (1) What to log: for each AI tool used, logging: (a) user identifier (who used it?), (b) timestamp (when was it used?), (c) tool name and version, (d) what was input (summary of prompt/request, not storing confidential content), (e) what was output (summary of response), (f) whether output was used in client work (yes/no), (g) whether output was verified (yes/no), (h) metadata (document name if applicable, matter ID if applicable), (2) Where logs are stored: (a) tool-native logs: many AI tools (especially enterprise versions) provide usage logs, (b) firm systems: if integrating AI into firm IT, maintaining logs in firm systems, (c) manual tracking: if using public tools without built-in logging, using spreadsheet/database to track use, (d) all logs should be: kept confidential, encrypted, retained for compliance period (typically 6 years), regularly backed up, (3) Logging by tool type: (a) ChatGPT Enterprise: ChatGPT provides usage logs and analytics within the enterprise dashboard; firm monitors these, (b) Claude via API: Anthropic provides usage reports; firm integrates logging into application, (c) Copilot: Microsoft provides tenant-level activity logs; firm integrates these into audit systems, (d) Custom/in-house solutions: built-in logging and monitoring required, (e) Public/free tools: require manual tracking if any compliance tracking needed, (4) Analytics and reporting: analyzing logs to: (a) quantify AI usage (how much AI is firm using? Which tools? Which practice areas?), (b) identify patterns: do certain users repeatedly use AI? Are certain matter types always AI-assisted?, (c) detect anomalies: is usage pattern unusual? (e.g., someone suddenly using ChatGPT 50x per day → potential data dumping), (d) assess compliance: are verification procedures being followed? Are required disclosures happening?, (e) measure efficiency gains: is AI actually saving time as expected?, (5) Monitoring for risks: using logs to detect: (a) unauthorized tool use: someone using prohibited AI tools, (b) excessive data input: evidence someone is feeding large amounts of confidential data into AI, (c) missing verification: AI used but no verification documented, (d) missing disclosure: AI used but no client/court notification, (e) suspicious patterns: unusual access (outside business hours, high volume, unusual users), (6) Compliance audit procedures: periodically (quarterly/annually) conducting AI compliance audits: (a) sample AI-assisted work products, (b) verify verification was done, (c) check for appropriate disclosure, (d) assess data handling, (e) document audit findings, (f) investigate any gaps found, (7) Staff notifications about monitoring: ensuring staff understand: (a) AI usage is logged, (b) logs are monitored for compliance, (c) policy violations may be detected through logs, (d) logs are confidential and used only for governance purposes (not for performance management), (8) Data retention and privacy: (a) logs are retained for 6 years (statute of limitations for malpractice), (b) logs containing confidential information are stored securely, (c) logs are not accessible to non-essential staff, (d) when logs are deleted (after 6-year period), deletion is documented, (9) Integration with incident response: logs serve as evidence for incident investigation: (a) if a hallucination is discovered, logs show who used the tool and when, (b) if a data breach occurs, logs show what data might have been exposed, (c) if policy violation alleged, logs show actual usage, (10) Regulatory expectations: understanding that Law Society and regulators may expect: (a) logs demonstrating AI governance, (b) evidence of verification procedures, (c) documentation of compliance with disclosure obligations, (d) logs demonstrating responsible use. Include log format templates, sample audit procedures, and monitoring dashboards.

Context: Use to design monitoring systems; implement with AI governance framework

**PROMPT 33 — Client Consent Forms for AI Use in Legal Work**

Create template client consent forms and disclosure statements that law firms can include in engagement letters, retainer agreements, and advisory communications to obtain client consent and disclosure regarding AI use. The templates should address: (1) Simple disclosure statement (for standard matters where AI may be used): (a) "Your Legal Matter and Artificial Intelligence: [Firm Name] may use artificial intelligence (AI) tools in preparing work for your matter. These tools may be used for legal research, contract analysis, document drafting, and administrative tasks. We maintain strict confidentiality and verify all AI-generated work before delivery. Using AI helps us manage your costs efficiently while maintaining the highest quality. If you do not consent to AI use, please notify us immediately." (b) include checkbox for consent, (c) opt-out provision: "Client opts out of AI use: Yes / No", (2) Detailed consent form (for matters involving extensive AI use): (a) explanation of what AI is and how it's used in legal services, (b) specific statement of AI tools that may be used, (c) explanation of AI limitations (including hallucination risk), (d) statement that the firm will verify all AI output and does not rely on AI as final work product without human review, (e) statement that confidentiality protections apply even to AI-assisted work, (f) statement of firm's competence and training in AI use, (g) client's right to request that specific work be done without AI assistance, (h) acknowledgment that client has read and understands the consent, (i) signature line and date, (3) Specific-matter consent: for high-sensitivity matters or substantial AI use: (a) describing the specific matter type and context, (b) identifying which specific AI tools will be used and for what purposes, (c) explaining the benefits of AI use for this matter, (d) addressing potential risks specific to this matter, (e) obtaining specific, informed consent from client (not blanket consent), (4) Data protection statement: (a) "Your confidential information will be protected even when used with AI tools. We will redact or anonymize sensitive information before input into AI systems, use only approved secure tools, have agreements in place with AI vendors ensuring data protection and confidentiality, and maintain audit logs demonstrating responsible use. Your information will not be used by AI vendors to train their general systems without your explicit separate consent.", (5) Billing statement: (a) "AI-assisted work may be billed at our standard rates or reduced rates depending on the nature of the work and time saved. We will advise you of the billing arrangement for AI-assisted work in our engagement letter. Using AI enables us to provide cost-effective legal services while maintaining quality.", (6) Client questions and controls: (a) "If you have questions about AI use in your matter, please ask. If you are uncomfortable with AI-assisted work on any issue, we will be happy to use traditional methods. You may request that any AI-generated work product be reviewed or re-done without AI assistance.", (7) For litigation matters: (a) "We will disclose our use of AI tools to opposing counsel and the court as required by court rules and professional conduct obligations. This disclosure will not affect your legal rights or our advocacy for your interests.", (8) For transactional matters: (a) "AI may be used to review documents, analyze transaction structures, and draft contract terms. All AI-generated provisions will be reviewed for accuracy and appropriateness to your transaction. You will have the opportunity to review and approve all final contract language.", (9) For advisory/counseling matters: (a) "AI-generated analysis will be reviewed and verified against primary legal authorities before being provided as legal advice. All advice remains the advice of our lawyers, not the AI system, and you may rely on it as such.", (10) Review and signature: (a) statement that client has read, understands, and consents to the AI use disclosure, (b) date and client signature/digital acknowledgment, (c) option for client to request a physical or electronic copy of the disclosure for their records. Provide multiple template versions (simple, detailed, by practice area) and guidance on when to use each.

Context: Use in all engagement letters and retainer agreements where AI may be used; customize for your practice areas
