## ARBITRATION FRAMEWORK & PROCEDURES

**PROMPT 043 — SIAC Arbitration: Notice of Arbitration & Claimant's Statement**

Prepare a Notice of Arbitration and detailed Statement of Claim for SIAC (Singapore International Arbitration Centre) arbitration against [RESPONDENT], seeking relief for [DESCRIBE CLAIM: e.g., breach of contract, breach of warranty, unlawful termination, quantum meruit].

Please draft:
- Notice of Arbitration (preliminary notice):
  - Formal notice commencing the arbitration
  - Identify the parties (Claimant and Respondent with full legal names and addresses)
  - Identify the arbitration agreement (reference to the contract clause, date of contract, parties to the contract)
  - Identify the contract or transaction giving rise to the dispute
  - Brief description of the dispute
  - Names and addresses of the parties' representatives/solicitors
  - Confirmation of SIAC as the arbitral institution
  - Confirmation of any seat of arbitration (usually Singapore)
  - Number of arbitrators (1, 3, or as agreed)
  - Proposed seat of arbitration (seat law will govern the arbitration procedure)

- SIAC Rules compliance:
  - Follow SIAC Arbitration Rules (5th Edition) format and requirements
  - Include requisite information in the Notice
  - Serve on the Respondent in accordance with the Rules
  - Keep registrars of court informed of the arbitration (if relevant)

- Statement of Claim (main pleading):
  - Introduction: identify the parties, the arbitration agreement, relief sought
  - Background: describe the relationship between the parties, the contract, context
  - Facts: detailed chronological narrative of the facts giving rise to the claim
  - Breach or wrongdoing: identify the specific breach or wrongdoing (what obligation was breached, when, how)
  - Causation: explain how the breach caused loss to the claimant
  - Loss and damage: detail the quantum of the loss (with calculation and supporting evidence)
  - Legal basis: identify the legal principles supporting the claim
  - Relief: specify what relief is sought (damages, interest, costs, specific performance, declaratory relief)

- Key components:
  - Clear identification of the contractual obligation allegedly breached
  - Specific facts showing breach (date of breach, nature of breach, evidence)
  - Quantification of loss with clear methodology
  - Reference to the contract and relevant clauses
  - Compliance with SIAC procedural rules (notice requirements, timelines, format)

- Drafting approach:
  - Organize by issue (if multiple claims, separate sections for each)
  - Cite the contract and relevant clauses for each obligation
  - Provide evidence for each factual allegation (references to exhibits)
  - Be specific about quantum (avoid vague claims; provide detailed calculations)
  - Include prayer for relief (exactly what is being claimed)

- Exhibits:
  - Attach key documents (the contract, correspondence, invoices, payment records, expert evidence, etc.)
  - Index all exhibits clearly
  - Ensure exhibits are legible and authentic

- Service:
  - Serve the Statement of Claim on the Respondent in accordance with SIAC Rules
  - Typically 30 days from Notice of Arbitration
  - Maintain proof of service

Include a detailed sample Notice of Arbitration and Statement of Claim for a typical SIAC arbitration (e.g., contract for the sale of goods, employment contract, commercial services agreement) covering multiple issues and substantial damages claims.

Context: When commencing SIAC arbitration and preparing the initial pleadings to present your case.

**PROMPT 044 — SIAC/ICC Arbitration: Respondent's Defence & Counterclaim**

Prepare a comprehensive Defence (and Counterclaim if applicable) in SIAC or ICC arbitration in response to the Claimant's Statement of Claim.

Please draft:
- Defence (primary response):
  - Introduction: identify the respondent and reference the Claimant's Statement
  - Overview: provide a brief overview of the Defence and key positions
  - Detailed response to each allegation in the Statement of Claim:
    - For each factual allegation, respond with: (a) admit, deny, or do not admit; (b) explanation or alternative version if denying
    - For each legal argument, respond with alternative legal interpretation or counter-argument
    - Identify areas where Respondent agrees with the Claimant (to narrow the issues)
  - Factual narrative: present the Respondent's version of the relevant facts
  - Legal defences: identify the legal principles supporting the Defence
  - Absence of loss/causation: challenge whether the Claimant has suffered loss, or challenge the causal chain

- Paragraph-by-paragraph response:
  - Match the numbering in the Claimant's Statement
  - For each numbered paragraph in the Claimant's Statement, provide a response
  - Example: "Para 15: The Respondent admits that a contract was entered into on [DATE]. The Respondent denies that [OBLIGATION] was imposed by the contract, or alternatively denies that the Respondent breached this obligation."

- Effective defences:
  - Contractual interpretation: the contract does not impose the obligation the Claimant alleges
  - Performance: the Respondent performed all obligations or was excused from performance
  - Claimant's breach: the Claimant breached their obligations first, excusing the Respondent
  - Mitigation: the Claimant failed to mitigate their loss
  - Causation: the alleged breach did not cause the claimed loss
  - Quantum: the claimed damages are not properly calculated or are excessive
  - Limitation: the claim is time-barred (statute of limitations)
  - Illegality: the contract is illegal or unenforceable
  - Waiver/estoppel: the Claimant waived or is estopped from enforcing the obligation
  - Force majeure: the breach was excused by force majeure or supervening event

- Counterclaim (if applicable):
  - Introduce the Counterclaim separately
  - State the basis for the Counterclaim (breach by the Claimant, other wrongdoing)
  - Follow the same structure as the Claimant's Statement (facts, breach, loss, quantum)
  - Identify the relief sought (damages, interest, costs, set-off)
  - Ensure the Counterclaim is within the scope of the arbitration agreement (not separate disputes)

- Detailed response methodology:
  - Organize by issue (e.g., Issue 1: Whether Respondent breached the contract...)
  - For each issue, provide: (1) the legal test; (2) the Claimant's position and evidence; (3) the Respondent's position and evidence; (4) why the Respondent's position is correct
  - Cite the contract, relevant provisions, case law
  - Reference exhibits supporting the Defence

- Tone and presentation:
  - Professional and measured (avoid accusations or inflammatory language)
  - Factual and specific (not vague denials)
  - Logical and clear (easy for the tribunal to follow)
  - Honest about areas of agreement (narrows issues for the tribunal)

- Procedural compliance:
  - Follow the applicable Arbitration Rules (SIAC, ICC, etc.)
  - Meet the filing deadline (typically 30-45 days from service of Statement of Claim)
  - Serve copies on the Claimant and tribunal members
  - Maintain proof of service

- Exhibits:
  - Attach all documents supporting the Defence
  - Index exhibits clearly
  - Reference exhibits in the Defence narrative

Include a detailed sample Defence and Counterclaim addressing multiple issues, with careful paragraph-by-paragraph responses to the Statement of Claim.

Context: When responding to a Statement of Claim in institutional arbitration and needing to present a comprehensive Defence and any Counterclaim.
Difficulty: Advanced
Best AI tool: Claude
Follow-up: PROMPT 043 (SIAC Claimant's Statement) or PROMPT 048 (Arbitration Hearing Strategy)

**PROMPT 045 — Ad Hoc Arbitration: Setting Up the Arbitral Tribunal**

Prepare documentation for establishing an ad hoc arbitration under the UNCITRAL Arbitration Rules, including the Notice of Arbitration, confirmation of the arbitration agreement, and procedural matters.

Please draft:
- Introduction to ad hoc arbitration:
  - Ad hoc arbitration operates without an institutional framework (no SIAC, ICC, etc.)
  - The parties themselves manage administration, rule-making, tribunal appointment
  - Typically used for complex cases, parties with prior arbitration experience, or when parties want to avoid institutional fees

- Notice of Arbitration:
  - Formal notice commencing the arbitration
  - Identify the parties and their representatives
  - Identify the arbitration agreement (contract clause creating the arbitration agreement)
  - Identify the dispute (what is the subject matter of the arbitration)
  - Identify the seat of arbitration (country/location where arbitration will be seated; law of that country applies)
  - Language of arbitration (typically English)
  - Number of arbitrators (1 or 3, or as agreed)
  - Proposed appointment procedures (how will each arbitrator be appointed)
  - Relief sought (summary of claims)

- Confirmation of Arbitration Agreement:
  - Formal document signed by both parties confirming existence and validity of the arbitration agreement
  - Confirms that the dispute is within the scope of the arbitration agreement
  - Confirms the seat, language, number of arbitrators, rules (e.g., UNCITRAL)
  - Confirms parties' intent to proceed to arbitration

- Procedural matters:
  - Appointment of arbitrators: discuss the procedure for appointing the tribunal
    - For 3-arbitrator tribunal: each party appoints one arbitrator; the two appointed arbitrators appoint the president/chair
    - For 1-arbitrator tribunal: parties agree on the arbitrator, or if no agreement, appoint from a panel
  - Arbitrator qualifications: proposed qualifications (neutrality, expertise, availability)
  - Seat of arbitration: discuss and agree on the seat (typically the location where awards will be enforced, or a neutral location)
  - Procedural rules: confirm UNCITRAL Arbitration Rules will apply
  - Language: confirm the language of the arbitration (usually English)

- Administrative arrangements:
  - Secretarial support: discuss whether a co-arbitrator or external secretary will support the tribunal
  - Costs of arbitration: discuss cost-sharing (typically each party bears its own costs; tribunal costs are split 50/50)
  - Timeline: propose a schedule for appointment, hearings, and award
  - Place of hearing: agree on where physical hearings will take place (or virtual if preferred)

- Document preparation:
  - Prepare detailed arbitration agreement confirmation
  - Prepare procedural order 1 (first procedural order, to be issued by the tribunal once appointed)
  - Discuss and agree on key procedural points before the tribunal is fully appointed

- Advantages and challenges:
  - Advantages: flexibility, lower institutional costs, parties control the process
  - Challenges: more complex administration, parties must manage procedural matters themselves, tribunal appointment can be contentious

Include detailed documentation for establishing an ad hoc arbitration, including templates for the Notice of Arbitration and confirmation of arbitration agreement.

Context: When commencing an ad hoc arbitration and need to establish the arbitral tribunal and procedural framework without institutional support.
Difficulty: Advanced
Best AI tool: Claude
Follow-up: PROMPT 043-044 (SIAC/ICC procedures) or PROMPT 048 (Arbitration Hearing Strategy)

**PROMPT 046 — Mediation Brief: Positioning for Settlement Negotiations**

Prepare a comprehensive mediation brief for submission to the mediator before mediation proceedings commence, designed to position your client favorably and encourage settlement.

Please draft:
- Introduction:
  - Identify the parties and subject matter of the dispute
  - Confirm the parties' commitment to mediation and settlement
  - Overview of your client's position and reasons for settlement

- Your client's perspective:
  - Summary of your client's version of the facts (favorable to your client)
  - Explain your client's understanding of the dispute and the underlying issues
  - Identify any root causes or systemic issues that could be addressed in settlement
  - Explain your client's interests (not just the legal claim, but the underlying interests: cost avoidance, relationship repair, certainty, etc.)

- Legal position:
  - Summary of your client's legal case (without detailed argument, just overview)
  - Key strengths (why your client is likely to win at arbitration/trial)
  - Realistic assessment of any weaknesses (be candid; mediators value realism)
  - Relevant contract provisions or law

- Quantum and damages:
  - Summary of the claimed loss (with calculation breakdown)
  - Support for the quantum (expert evidence, market analysis, documented loss)
  - Realistic assessment of achievable recovery (avoid over-inflating the claim)
  - Discussion of any alternatives to cash settlement

- Other party's position:
  - Fair and balanced summary of the other party's legal position
  - Acknowledge the merits of any defensible arguments raised by the other party
  - Identify areas where the other party's position is weak
  - Identify common ground or areas of potential agreement

- Risk assessment:
  - Risks of proceeding to litigation/arbitration (uncertainty, delay, costs, reputational damage, etc.)
  - Cost-benefit analysis (costs of litigation/arbitration vs. settlement)
  - Timeline considerations (litigation/arbitration will take X years; settlement now provides certainty)
  - Enforcement considerations (if you win at trial/arbitration, how confident are you that you can enforce the award)

- Settlement options:
  - Proposed settlement amount or range
  - Alternative settlement structures (payment schedule, non-monetary relief, future cooperation)
  - What your client values (money, relationship repair, injunctive relief, apology, etc.)
  - What your client is willing to accept as a compromise

- Your client's motivation for settlement:
  - Explain why your client wants to settle (cost avoidance, business reasons, relationship preservation, etc.)
  - Demonstrate genuine interest in finding a middle ground
  - Explain the benefits of settlement to both parties

- Timeline and pressure points:
  - Identify any external factors creating urgency (upcoming hearing, new facts emerging, business developments, etc.)
  - Discuss the timeline for moving toward settlement

- Confidentiality:
  - Confirm that the mediation brief is confidential and without prejudice
  - Confirm that statements in the brief are not admissions and cannot be used against your client
  - Confirm that the mediator will not disclose settlement offers or positions to the other party without permission

- Professional tone:
  - Be respectful of the other party and their counsel
  - Avoid inflammatory language or personal attacks
  - Focus on interests and issues, not personality conflicts
  - Demonstrate good faith

Include a detailed sample mediation brief for a typical dispute (e.g., breach of contract with damages claim of $500k, or partnership dispute with multiple issues) showing effective positioning and realistic settlement discussion.

Context: When preparing for mediation and needing to present your client's case and settlement position to the mediator.

**PROMPT 047 — Position Paper for Arbitration (Detailed Legal Submissions)**

Prepare a detailed position paper (memorial) for submission in arbitration proceedings, comprehensively addressing the issues in dispute and legal arguments.

Please draft:
- Structure:
  - Executive summary (1-2 pages): overview of the issues, your client's position, relief sought
  - Factual background
  - Legal analysis (by issue)
  - Conclusion
  - Appendices (exhibits, authorities)

- Factual background section:
  - Narrative of the facts relevant to the disputes
  - Identify undisputed facts (streamlines the tribunal's task)
  - Identify areas of factual dispute (and your client's version)
  - Reference supporting documents (exhibit numbers and page numbers)
  - Explain the significance of the facts to the legal issues

- Legal analysis (by issue):
  - For each disputed issue:
    - State the legal test or principle (cite authorities)
    - Apply the law to the facts
    - Explain why the law supports your client's position
    - Address counter-arguments raised by the other party
    - Reach a conclusion on the issue

- Substantive issues to address:
  - Contractual interpretation (what does the contract require)
  - Performance (did your client perform, or was the other party in breach)
  - Quantum (calculation of damages if your client is owed money)
  - Defenses (any defenses to liability or quantum)
  - Applicable law (which country's law applies)
  - Jurisdiction/arbitration (confirmation that the tribunal has jurisdiction)

- Detailed structure example:
  - Issue 1: Interpretation of Contract Clause X
    - Legal test for contractual interpretation (cite relevant authorities)
    - The parties' competing interpretations
    - Analysis of the clause language, context, factual matrix
    - Why your interpretation is correct
    - Consequences of each interpretation
  - Issue 2: Whether Respondent Breached [Obligation]
    - What was the respondent required to do
    - What did the respondent actually do
    - Why this constitutes a breach (or alternative, why there was no breach)
  - Issue 3: Quantum of Damages
    - Legal principles on quantum
    - Calculation methodology
    - Detailed breakdown of the claim
    - Why the claimed amount is reasonable and justified

- Addressing the other party's case:
  - Systematically address each argument raised by the other party
  - Explain why their legal analysis is flawed, or why their facts are incorrect
  - Cite counter-authorities to refute their case
  - Don't ignore their strong points; address them and explain why they don't change the outcome

- Evidence and exhibits:
  - Reference specific evidence supporting each point
  - Cite bundle pages and exhibit numbers
  - Explain why the evidence is reliable and compelling
  - Address any inconsistencies or gaps in the evidence

- Procedural issues:
  - Address any procedural disputes (admissibility of evidence, witness sequencing, etc.)
  - Confirm compliance with procedural orders

- Authority and case law:
  - Cite relevant authorities (statutes, case law, arbitral awards)
  - Explain how each authority supports your position
  - Address and distinguish any authorities cited by the other party

- Prayer for relief:
  - Clearly state what relief is sought
  - Quantum of any monetary award, interest, costs
  - Any non-monetary relief (declaratory relief, injunctive relief, etc.)
  - Request for costs on a full indemnity basis if appropriate

- Length and presentation:
  - Position papers can be lengthy (30-50 pages for complex cases)
  - Organize clearly with numbered sections and headings
  - Use clear language accessible to an international tribunal
  - Use footnotes sparingly (extensive footnoting reduces readability)

- Confidentiality and privilege:
  - Ensure the position paper does not contain privileged information
  - Do not disclose settlement discussions or without prejudice communications
  - Protect confidential business information if necessary (seek protective order)

Include a detailed sample position paper addressing multiple complex issues (interpretation, performance, damages calculation) in a typical arbitration dispute.

Context: When preparing detailed written legal submissions for arbitration proceedings.
Difficulty: Advanced
Best AI tool: Claude
Follow-up: PROMPT 043-044 (Statement of Claim/Defence) or PROMPT 048 (Arbitration Hearing Strategy)

**PROMPT 048 — Arbitration Hearing Strategy & Examination Plan**

Prepare a comprehensive hearing strategy and witness examination plan for an arbitration hearing in [DESCRIBE CASE], scheduled for [DATES] before [TRIBUNAL COMPOSITION].

Please draft:
- Pre-hearing strategy:
  - Identify the key issues in dispute (what needs to be decided to achieve your client's objectives)
  - Identify the key evidence (which witnesses and documents are most critical)
  - Identify the tribunal's concerns (what questions is the tribunal likely to ask)
  - Develop a persuasive narrative (how will the evidence be presented to tell a compelling story)
  - Anticipate the other party's case (what arguments will they make, how can we respond)

- Hearing structure and timeline:
  - Opening submissions (5-10 minutes per party)
  - Witness examination (claimant's witnesses, then respondent's witnesses)
  - Expert evidence (if applicable)
  - Closing submissions (10-15 minutes per party)
  - Allocate time for each component
  - Plan which party goes first (if you are the claimant, you may have the advantage)

- Opening submissions strategy:
  - What is the theme or key message (briefly state the essence of the case)
  - What is the narrative (explain how the facts and law support your position)
  - What are the key points the tribunal must accept (focus on undisputed or well-supported facts)
  - What questions do you want to raise in the tribunal's mind

- Witness examination plan:
  - For each witness, prepare:
    - Proposed examination (chief examination): how will you elicit your evidence, in what order
    - Anticipated cross-examination: what will the other party ask, what admissions should you try to avoid
    - Documentary support: which documents will you rely on with this witness
    - Timing: how long will the examination take (allocate appropriately)

  - Examination strategy:
    - Build credibility: start with background and context before getting to the disputed facts
    - Use documents: introduce key documents through the witness
    - Establish knowledge: confirm the witness's basis for knowledge and reliability
    - Pin down facts: get agreement on undisputed facts to build momentum
    - Reach conclusions: lead the witness to agree with your overall narrative

  - Cross-examination preparation:
    - Anticipate questions the other party will ask
    - Brief the witness on how to handle aggressive questioning (don't argue, don't be evasive, acknowledge fair points)
    - Prepare short, direct answers (not volunteering information beyond what is asked)
    - Prepare for documentary challenges (understand what each key document shows)

- Expert evidence:
  - If you have expert witnesses, plan their examination carefully
  - Understand the expert's methodology and conclusions
  - Prepare the expert to defend their opinion against cross-examination
  - If there are competing experts, plan how you will highlight the superiority of your expert's analysis

- Documentary evidence:
  - Prepare a document strategy: which documents are most important, how will you introduce them
  - Organize documents in a logical sequence (chronological, by issue)
  - Ensure all key documents are clearly identified and available during the hearing
  - Prepare for the other party to challenge the authenticity or interpretation of documents

- Procedural issues:
  - Confirm any procedural orders have been complied with
  - Raise any procedural concerns (inadequate disclosure by the other party, admission of evidence, etc.)
  - Plan how you will handle procedural disputes (respect the tribunal, raise issues firmly but professionally)

- Tribunal engagement:
  - Prepare questions the tribunal might ask
  - Plan how to respond to tribunal questions (concise, directly responsive, helpful)
  - Use tribunal questions as opportunities to reinforce key points
  - Be responsive to the tribunal's concerns (if the tribunal seems focused on a particular issue, be prepared to address it thoroughly)

- Closing submissions:
  - Plan key messages: what will you emphasize in closing
  - Structure: how will you organize the closing (by issue, chronologically, by strength of argument)
  - Reference evidence: cite specific evidence supporting each point
  - Address the law: apply the law to the facts as established by the evidence
  - Address the other party's case: explain why you should prevail over their arguments

- Visual aids and presentations:
  - Consider using visual aids (chronology, diagrams, charts, photographs)
  - Prepare any PowerPoint presentations or demonstratives
  - Ensure visual aids are clear, professional, and helpful (not cluttered or confusing)
  - Test all technology before the hearing

- Team roles:
  - Identify roles for counsel and solicitors during the hearing
  - Who will conduct opening, examination, cross-examination, closing
  - Who will manage documents and exhibits
  - Who will manage procedural matters and tribunal administration

- Risk management:
  - Identify areas of weakness in your case and have strategies to address them
  - Prepare contingencies (if a key witness is unavailable, what is the fallback plan)
  - Be ready to make concessions on points you cannot win (preserves credibility)
  - Maintain composure if the tribunal seems skeptical (don't become defensive)

Include a detailed hearing strategy and examination plan for a typical arbitration (e.g., international sales contract dispute with 3-4 key witnesses on each side, expert evidence on quantum, 5-day hearing scheduled).

Context: When preparing for an arbitration hearing and need to develop a comprehensive strategy for witness examination and persuasive presentation.
Difficulty: Advanced

**PROMPT 049 — Mediation Strategy: Interests vs. Positions, BATNA & WATNA**

Prepare a comprehensive mediation strategy for [DESCRIBE CASE], analyzing your client's interests, best alternative to negotiated agreement (BATNA), worst alternative to negotiated agreement (WATNA), and settlement range.

Please draft:
- Understanding interests vs. positions:
  - Position: what the party says they want (e.g., "I demand $1 million damages")
  - Interest: the underlying need or concern (e.g., "I need to cover my business losses and ensure I'm not liable for future claims")
  - Interests are often deeper and broader than positions, and can be common ground

- Your client's interests:
  - Financial interests: how much money does your client need to satisfy their losses and concerns
  - Non-financial interests: what else matters (relationship, reputation, continuing business, avoiding precedent, etc.)
  - Priority ranking: which interests are most important, which could be compromised

- Analyzing the other party's likely interests:
  - What is likely motivating the other party
  - What are their financial constraints (can they afford to pay a large amount)
  - What are their reputational concerns
  - What are their business imperatives (do they need certainty, want to avoid publicity, etc.)
  - Where might there be common ground

- BATNA (Best Alternative to Negotiated Agreement):
  - What is your client's best alternative if mediation fails and the case proceeds to litigation/arbitration
  - Best case scenario: if your client wins at trial/arbitration, what is the best outcome
  - Realistic scenario: what will your client likely recover, accounting for the risks and uncertainties of litigation
  - Legal costs and time: how much will it cost to litigate, how long will it take
  - Risks and uncertainties: what are the risks that your client might lose, or recover less than claimed

- WATNA (Worst Alternative to Negotiated Agreement):
  - What is the worst case scenario if your client proceeds to litigation/arbitration
  - Possibility of losing entirely (or recovering nothing)
  - Possibility of adverse findings (defendant receives judgment against claimant, or substantial counterclaim succeeds)
  - Costs and time: will your client have to bear their own costs if unsuccessful
  - Reputational damage: could the litigation cause reputational harm
  - Business disruption: will ongoing litigation interfere with business operations

- Settlement range:
  - Aspiration: what is your client's ideal settlement (usually higher than BATNA)
  - Realistic range: what settlement range would satisfy your client (typically between 40-80% of the claim)
  - Walk-away point: below what amount would your client prefer to proceed to litigation rather than settle
  - Rationale: explain why this range is appropriate (based on BATNA, WATNA, case strength, etc.)

- Other party's likely BATNA and WATNA:
  - Estimate what the other party sees as their BATNA (defending the case in litigation)
  - Estimate their best case (winning at trial, owing nothing)
  - Estimate their worst case (losing and owing the full claim plus interest and costs)
  - Use this analysis to estimate what settlement range might be attractive to them

- Settlement scenarios:
  - Scenario 1: Settlement at [AMOUNT] — explain why both parties might find this attractive
  - Scenario 2: Settlement with alternative terms (payment schedule, non-monetary relief, ongoing relationship)
  - Scenario 3: Settlement with conditions (e.g., non-disclosure, admission of liability or denial of liability)
  - Explain the advantages and disadvantages of each scenario

- Negotiation strategy:
  - Initial offer: what offer should your client make (should be anchored to a reasonable position, not ridiculously high or low)
  - Room to move: how much is your client willing to move from the initial position as negotiations progress
  - Concessions: what concessions is your client willing to make (in exchange for reciprocal concessions from the other party)
  - Credibility: what is your client's credibility in negotiations (can they afford to walk away, do they have leverage, etc.)

- Managing the mediation:
  - Relationship with the mediator: work with the mediator to understand the other party's true interests and constraints
  - Candor with the mediator: be honest about your client's BATNA and realistic settlement range (helps the mediator facilitate resolution)
  - Without prejudice communications: understand that offers made in mediation cannot be used as admissions if the case proceeds to litigation
  - Momentum: look for early agreement on smaller issues to build momentum toward overall settlement

- Risk communication:
  - Help your client understand the risks and uncertainties of litigation (even strong cases can be lost)
  - Help your client understand that litigation is expensive, time-consuming, and distracting
  - Help your client see settlement as a positive resolution, not as "giving in"

- Cultural and relational factors:
  - If there is an ongoing business relationship, consider how settlement could preserve or repair the relationship
  - If there are cultural or relational sensitivities, factor these into the negotiation strategy
  - Sometimes non-financial terms (apology, explanation, acknowledgment) are valuable to parties

Include a detailed sample mediation strategy for a typical dispute (e.g., breach of contract with $500k claim) showing realistic BATNA/WATNA analysis and appropriate settlement range.

Context: When preparing for mediation and need to develop a clear understanding of your client's interests and realistic settlement parameters.
Difficulty: Intermediate

**PROMPT 050 — New York Convention Award Enforcement (Multi-Jurisdictional)**

Prepare an application to enforce an international arbitration award under the New York Convention in [JURISDICTION], seeking to compel the Respondent to comply with the award or to execute against their assets.

Please draft:
- Overview of the New York Convention:
  - The New York Convention on the Recognition and Enforcement of Foreign Arbitral Awards (1958) applies in [SPECIFY NUMBER] countries
  - The Convention provides for enforcement of international arbitration awards with minimal scrutiny
  - Grounds for refusing enforcement are limited (narrow grounds of challenge)
  - Enforcement is typically obtained by court application in the jurisdiction where the respondent's assets are located

- Application structure:
  - Title: "Application to Enforce Arbitration Award under the New York Convention, 1958"
  - Identify the Award (issuing tribunal, date, claimant, respondent, amount, key terms)
  - Identify the Respondent's assets in the jurisdiction (property, bank accounts, business interests)
  - Request for enforcement order (judgment in favor of the Claimant, enforcement powers against Respondent's assets)

- Affidavit evidence:
  - Confirm the validity of the arbitration agreement (the award arose from a valid arbitration)
  - Confirm the award is final and binding (not subject to appeal or further arbitration proceedings)
  - Confirm the award has not been complied with voluntarily (the Respondent has not paid)
  - Confirm the seat of arbitration and applicable law
  - Provide a copy of the award (certified copy, with translation if necessary)
  - Identify the Respondent's assets in the jurisdiction

- Legal framework:
  - New York Convention Article IV: enforcement application must be accompanied by the award and the arbitration agreement
  - New York Convention Article V: limited grounds for refusing recognition and enforcement
    - The arbitration agreement was invalid
    - The Respondent did not receive proper notice of the arbitration or had no opportunity to present their case
    - The award deals with issues outside the scope of the arbitration agreement
    - The tribunal exceeded its powers (decided issues beyond what was submitted)
    - The composition of the tribunal was improper or the tribunal did not follow the agreed procedure
    - The award has not yet become binding, or has been set aside in the country of origin
  - Address why none of these grounds apply (or why they should not succeed)

- Procedural requirements:
  - Serve application on the Respondent with sufficient notice
  - Provide the original award or certified copy
  - Provide the original arbitration agreement or certified copy
  - Provide certified translation if the award is in a foreign language
  - File supporting affidavits and evidence

- Responding to potential challenges:
  - The Respondent may argue invalidity of the arbitration agreement (typically difficult to establish under the Convention)
  - The Respondent may argue breach of natural justice (lack of notice, inability to present case) — prepare evidence that proper notice was given
  - The Respondent may argue the award exceeds the tribunal's jurisdiction — prepare evidence of the tribunal's authority
