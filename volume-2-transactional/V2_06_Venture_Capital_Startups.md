## Investment Documents - Term Sheets & SAFEs

**PROMPT 050 — Venture Capital Term Sheet Preparation (Series A)**

Prepare a comprehensive Series A venture capital term sheet for investment in a Singapore startup, including: (1) Investment Overview: (a) investor name and investment amount (e.g., $2M), (b) post-money valuation ($10M typical for early-stage startup), (c) percentage ownership acquired (e.g., 20%), (d) security type (preferred stock vs common stock), (e) closing timeline and conditions precedent; (2) Security Terms: (a) Series A Preferred Stock issued to investor, (b) preferences attaching to preferred stock: (i) liquidation preference (preferred stockholders paid before common in liquidation, typically non-participating preferred), (ii) conversion rights (preferred converts to common at 1:1 at IPO or liquidation), (iii) dividend rights (if company declares dividend, preferred receives per-share amount before common); (c) number of shares: investor receives shares calculated as Investment Amount / Valuation Per Share, (d) price per share: inverse of valuation/fully-diluted share count; (3) Liquidation Preference: (a) definition: in exit event (sale, acquisition, IPO), preferred stock liquidated first, (b) non-participating preferred: investor receives greater of (i) liquidation preference multiple (typically 1x investment), or (ii) common stock conversion value, (c) participating preferred: investor gets liquidation preference PLUS participation in remaining proceeds (less common, founder-unfavorable); (4) Conversion Rights: (a) automatic conversion to common stock at IPO (no longer preferred class), (b) investor conversion election at sale/exit (preferred can choose common conversion if more valuable), (c) conversion adjustment for anti-dilution events; (5) Anti-Dilution Protection: (a) weighted average anti-dilution (most common): if future funding at lower price, preferred share price adjusted downward using weighted formula, (b) full ratchet anti-dilution (rare, founder-unfavorable): preferred share price reset to lowest price round, (c) no adjustment for stock splits, stock dividends, rights offerings; (6) Protective Provisions (Investor Veto Rights): (a) investor consent required for: (i) authorization of preferred stock class, (ii) liquidation, dissolution, or sale of company, (iii) merger or consolidation, (iv) change of business/strategy, (v) borrowing debt above threshold, (vi) incurring annual operating expenses above budget, (vii) hiring/firing CEO or CFO, (viii) related party transactions above threshold, (ix) acquisition of another company, (x) amendment of constitution affecting preferred rights, (xi) sale of assets above threshold; (b) voting: single investor with veto power if holds >25% (on these matters); (7) Board Representation: (a) investor nominates one board seat (if investment > $1M typical), (b) founder retains CEO or board chair seat, (c) third director appointed mutually or through independent director selection process, (d) board size typically 3 (2 designees, 1 independent) or 5 (2 investor, 2 founder, 1 independent); (8) Information & Inspection Rights: (a) quarterly unaudited financial statements within 45 days of quarter end, (b) annual audited financial statements within 120 days of year end, (c) annual budget within 30 days of fiscal year end, (d) monthly management accounts, (e) right to inspect books and facilities (reasonable notice), (f) right to attend all board meetings (observer status if no board seat); (9) Participation Rights (Pro-Rata Rights): (a) investor has right to participate in future funding rounds, (b) pro-rata = maintain same ownership % in subsequent rounds, (c) example: if investor owns 20% in Series A, can purchase 20% of Series B (if chooses), (d) typically limited to 2-3 subsequent rounds; (10) Drag-Along Rights: (a) if holders of majority preferred stock approve sale/exit, minority shareholders must also participate, (b) forces all shareholders to sell on same terms, (c) prevents minority blocking exit; (11) Tag-Along Rights: (a) if founder/majority shareholder sells shares to third party, minority shareholders have right to co-sell on same terms, (b) prevents founder from selling at premium while minority left behind; (12) Redemption Rights: (a) rare in true venture investing, (b) if included: investor can force company to repurchase shares at valuation if no exit after X years, (c) typically seen in mezzanine/later stage rounds; (13) Vesting Schedules: (a) founder shares typically vest over 4 years with 1-year cliff (if founder leaves within year, forfeits all shares), (b) investor stock typically NOT vested (investor commitment is cash, not services), (c) option pool: typically 10-20% of post-money cap table reserved for employee options; (14) Board Observation: (a) investor who doesn't have board seat receives observer status, (b) right to attend board meetings but not vote, (c) access to financial information; (15) Pro-Rata Participation: (a) investor right to maintain ownership % in future rounds, (b) exercised by purchasing allocated shares in next round, (c) if investor doesn't exercise, ownership % diluted; (16) Expenses: (a) company pays investor's reasonable legal expenses (typically $25-50K cap), (b) company pays investor's accounting/audit fees; (17) Key Closing Conditions: (a) completion of legal due diligence satisfactory to investor, (b) delivery of representations, warranties, certificates, (c) no material adverse change in company, (d) execution of definitive agreements (SAFE, stockholders agreement, investor rights agreement). Include detailed explanations of preferred vs common stock economics, cap table models showing dilution effects, and negotiation guidance on contentious terms (liquidation preferences, pro-rata rights, board seats).

**PROMPT 051 — Simple Agreement for Future Equity (SAFE) Documentation**

Prepare comprehensive guidance on Simple Agreements for Future Equity (SAFE) as alternative to preferred stock for early-stage startup fundraising, including: (1) SAFE Purpose: (a) simple instrument for early-stage investing (pre-valuation stage), (b) investor provides capital now, equity issued later (Series A or earlier), (c) avoids need to set company valuation immediately, (d) delays governance complexity (preferred stock rights) until later funding; (2) SAFE Structure: (a) not debt (no interest, no maturity), (b) not equity (no voting rights, no dividends immediately), (c) simple contract for future equity upon triggering event, (d) convertible: SAFE converts to equity when company raises Series A (or triggers event); (3) Conversion Events (When SAFE Converts to Equity): (a) Equity Financing Event: company raises institutional funding round (Series A, Series B), conversion automatically triggered, (b) sale/exit of company: SAFE converts to common stock prior to sale, (c) IPO: SAFE converts to common stock, (d) dissolution: company is dissolved/liquidated (SAFE treated as debt, investor recovers capital if funds available); (4) Valuation Cap: (a) optional protection for SAFE holder, (b) if Series A at valuation > cap, SAFE converts at cap (favorable to SAFE holder), (c) example: SAFE with $2M cap, Series A at $5M valuation, SAFE converts at $2M rate (discount), (d) protects investor from excessive dilution if company raised multiple SAFEs; (5) Discount Rate: (a) alternative to valuation cap, (b) Series A price discounted by % (typically 20-30%), (c) example: Series A at $1/share, SAFE with 20% discount converts at $0.80/share, (d) rewards early investment; (6) MFN (Most Favored Nation) Clause: (a) if subsequent SAFE issued with better terms (lower cap, higher discount), earlier SAFE automatically receives better terms, (b) prevents later SAFEs from receiving better economics; (7) Pro-Rata Rights: (a) SAFE holder has right to participate in future funding rounds at pro-rata ownership, (b) less common in SAFEs vs preferred stock; (8) Investor Governance: (a) no board seat typically with SAFE (governance deferred), (b) no voting rights as SAFE holder (no preferred stock rights), (c) no protective provisions or veto rights, (d) governance deferred until preferred stock issued in Series A; (9) SAFE Economics Example: (a) founder raises $500K via 3 SAFEs ($200K, $200K, $100K) with different terms, (b) each SAFE has valuation cap (e.g., $2M, $2.5M, $3M respectively), (c) Series A at $5M valuation with $2M commitment, (d) SAFE conversions: (i) SAFE 1: converts at $2M cap (250K shares at $5M/250K = $0.20/share effective cap rate), (ii) SAFE 2: converts at $2.5M cap, (iii) SAFE 3: converts at $3M cap, (e) founder's dilution: founder retains 70% ownership, SAFEs convert to 20% aggregate, option pool 10%; (10) SAFE vs Convertible Notes: (a) convertible notes: include interest accrual, maturity date, debt features, (b) SAFE: no interest, no maturity, true equity instrument, (c) SAFE advantages: simpler, lower cost, faster close, (d) SAFE disadvantages: no investor protections if never converted (worse downside); (11) Preferred Stock vs SAFE: (a) preferred stock: full governance rights (protective provisions, board seat, information rights), (b) SAFE: deferred governance, no control/veto, (c) SAFE appropriate for friends-and-family seed rounds, (d) preferred stock appropriate for institutional VC rounds; (12) Key SAFE Terms to Negotiate: (a) valuation cap (lower = better for investor), (b) discount rate (higher = better for investor), (c) pro-rata rights (whether included), (d) MFN rights (whether included), (e) liquidation scenario: what happens if company sells before Series A (typically investor receives capital back); (13) Closing Mechanics: (a) SAFE signature (relatively simple, 2-3 page document), (b) SAFE executed by founder(s) and investor(s), (c) no complex closing conditions vs preferred stock, (d) funds wired typically same day/next day; (14) Tax Implications: (a) SAFE holder may have exercise risk (Section 409A taxation), (b) if company never raises Series A and SAFE doesn't convert, tax treatment unclear, (c) SAFE issuing company: no tax deduction for SAFE (unlike debt interest); (15) Practical SAFE Deployment: (a) typical use: pre-seed and seed rounds, (b) typical SAFE cap: $1M-$3M, (c) typical discount: 20-30%, (d) multiple SAFEs may be issued before Series A (rolling closes), (e) SAFE typically held until Series A (when converts to preferred stock), (f) if no Series A within 3-5 years, SAFE treatment uncertain (company may need to repay). Include SAFE template document, cap table modeling for SAFE conversions, and SAFE vs convertible note comparison.

Context: When fundraising pre-Series A and seeking simple early-stage investment documentation
Difficulty: Intermediate

**PROMPT 052 — Convertible Notes & Debt Investment Instruments**

Prepare comprehensive guidance on convertible notes and other debt-based investment instruments for startup fundraising, including: (1) Convertible Note Structure: (a) promissory note from company to investor, (b) investor lends capital at 0-6% interest, (c) note matures after X years (typically 2-3 years), (d) upon maturity: either repaid or converted to equity at Series A, (e) conversion terms set upon Series A closing; (2) Interest Rate: (a) typically low (0-6% per annum), (b) reflects early-stage risk (investor providing capital, uncertain exit), (c) interest accrues and added to principal upon conversion, (d) early convertible notes had higher rates; (3) Maturity Date: (a) typical maturity 2-3 years post-closing, (b) at maturity, company must either: (i) repay principal + accrued interest, (ii) convert to equity at agreed valuation, (c) extension possible if mutual consent; (4) Valuation Cap: (a) cap on conversion price if Series A at higher valuation, (b) investor converts at lower of (i) Series A price, or (ii) cap rate, (c) caps typically $2-5M for early-stage startups, (d) rewards early investment; (5) Discount Rate: (a) alternative or in addition to cap, (b) if Series A at price P, convertible converts at P × (1 - discount %), (c) typical discount: 20-30%, (d) can use both cap and discount (more favorable conversion uses lesser price); (6) Conversion Mechanics: (a) automatic conversion upon Series A closing, (b) investor's choice to convert at Series A or repay at maturity, (c) if maturity reached with no Series A, note due and repaid by company, (d) compound interest accrues if note not converted (adds to repayment amount); (7. Events Triggering Conversion: (a) Series A or later equity funding, (b) exit/acquisition of company (conversion to common stock at valuation prior to exit), (c) IPO, (d) optional: investor election to convert at specific time; (8) Default & Acceleration: (a) if company misses interest payment, note in default, (b) investor can demand repayment (rare in venture context), (c) investor conversion acceleration (force conversion to equity), (d) event of default: material adverse change, bankruptcy, change of control; (9) Convertible Note vs SAFE: (a) convertible note: includes interest, maturity, debt features, (b) SAFE: no interest, no maturity, pure equity instrument, (c) convertible note more investor-favorable (interest + maturity puts), (d) SAFE simpler/faster, preferred for friends-and-family, (e) venture math: SAFE now more popular than convertible notes; (10) Convertible Note vs Preferred Stock: (a) preferred stock: governance rights, liquidation preferences, protective provisions, (b) convertible note: no governance until conversion, simpler, (c) convertible note lower cost to document, (d) preferred stock more favorable for investor control; (11) Tax Implications: (a) investor: ordinary income on interest received, (b) company: interest tax-deductible, (c) conversion: tax-free exchange of debt for equity (Section 351-like treatment), (d) Section 409A: timing of valuation determination can trigger tax issues; (12) Warrant Features: (a) some convertible notes include detachable warrants, (b) warrant: right to purchase shares at fixed price (separate from conversion), (c) warrants add value for investor, dilutive to founder, (d) warrant exercise price typically below conversion price; (13) MFN & Pro-Rata Clauses: (a) MFN: if subsequent convertible note issued with better terms (lower cap, higher discount), earlier note gets better terms, (b) pro-rata: investor maintains ownership % in Series A; (14) Parity Clauses: (a) conversion price of convertible note maintains parity with other notes, (b) all notes convert at same price/cap in Series A; (15) Registration Rights: (a) if investor converts to common stock, they typically receive registration rights (right to require company to register for public sale if IPO), (b) S-3 rights (lighter registration), demand rights (investor can force registration); (16) Practical Deployment: (a) typical use: seed and pre-Series A rounds, (b) typical note amount: $100K-$500K, (c) aggregate convertible notes can total $2M+ before Series A, (d) notes typically mature 2-3 years, (e) if Series A doesn't occur by maturity, company in difficult position (owes money); (17) Challenges with Convertible Notes: (a) if company fails to raise Series A by maturity, notes come due (company funding crisis), (b) multiple notes with different terms create cap table complexity, (c) maturity dates create deadline pressure (company must raise Series A before notes mature), (d) interest accrual increases dilution (principal + interest converts to more shares). Include convertible note template, cap table modeling for multiple notes, and comparison of note terms (cap, discount, interest, maturity) and conversion outcomes.

## Cap Table & Equity Compensation

**PROMPT 053 — Cap Table Management & Fully-Diluted Analysis**

Prepare comprehensive guidance on capitalization table (cap table) management and dilution analysis for startup equity funding, including: (1) Cap Table Components: (a) common stock: founder shares, employee option shares, (b) preferred stock: investor preferred shares (Series A, B, C), (c) options: unvested employee stock options in option pool, (d) warrants: rights to purchase shares (from convertible notes, debt), (e) convertible notes: debt instruments convertible to equity upon trigger event; (2) Ownership Percentage Calculation: (a) ownership % = shares owned / total shares issued and outstanding, (b) basic example: founder owns 1M shares, investor owns 250K shares, total = 1.25M, founder % = 1M/1.25M = 80%, (c) fully-diluted: also includes in-the-money options, in-the-money warrants, convertible notes; (3) Fully-Diluted Share Count: (a) includes not just outstanding, but also in-the-money options/warrants, (b) calculation: outstanding shares + unvested options (assuming exercised) + in-the-money options (assumed exercised) + convertible instruments (assumed converted), (c) important for Series A valuation (valuation based on fully-diluted count, not just current shares), (d) example: (i) common shares outstanding: 1M, (ii) options outstanding (in-the-money): 200K (option pool), (iii) SAFEs convertible: 200K, (iv) fully-diluted = 1.4M shares; (4) Pre-Money vs Post-Money Valuation: (a) pre-money valuation: company value before investor investment, (b) post-money valuation: company value after investor investment, (c) formula: post-money = pre-money + investment amount, (d) investor ownership % = investment amount / post-money valuation, (e) example: company values at $4M pre-money, investor puts in $1M, post-money = $5M, investor owns 20%; (5) Valuation Per Share (Series A Example): (a) price per share = post-money valuation / fully-diluted share count, (b) example: post-money $5M, fully-diluted 2M shares, price per share = $2.50, (c) investor buying at $2.50/share for 400K shares = $1M investment, (d) founder's shares diluted (founder ownership reduced by Series A issuance); (6) Dilution Effects: (a) after Series A, founder ownership % decreases (more shares issued), (b) founder's absolute share count unchanged (still 1M shares), but as % of total, decreased, (c) example: (i) pre-Series A: founder 80% of 1.25M = 1M shares, (ii) post-Series A: founder 1M shares of 2.4M total = 41.7%, (d) dilution allows growth funding, investor upside, alignment; (7) Option Pool Mechanics: (a) typical option pool: 10-20% of post-money cap table, (b) option pool carved from founder shares (founder absorption of dilution), (c) option pool allows hiring without further dilution, (d) example: Series A post-money 2.4M shares, option pool = 480K shares (20%), founder diluted to 52% (1M of 2.4M), with remaining 10% distributed to future employees; (8) Option Pool Expansion: (a) if pool depleted by employee hiring, board may authorize expansion, (b) expansion = additional option issuance, dilutive to founders, (c) typical expansion: add another 10% for Series B preparation; (9) Vesting Schedules for Options: (a) standard: 4-year vesting with 1-year cliff, (b) cliff: if employee leaves before 1 year, forfeits all shares (0 vesting), (c) after cliff, vesting monthly (1/48th per month), (d) acceleration: option acceleration upon change of control or termination (founder favorable); (10) Stock Appreciation Rights (SARs): (a) alternative to options: employee receives cash equal to stock appreciation, (b) no exercise required, cash settled at exit, (c) founder preferred over options (non-dilutive), but cash cost at exit; (11) Secondary Market & Partial Exits: (a) secondary market: employees, founders selling shares to investors before exit, (b) allows partial liquidity for early investors without IPO, (c) company may have right of first refusal on secondaries (prevent ownership changes), (d) secondary deals impact cap table; (12) Cap Table Waterfall (Exit Scenario): (a) exit at $100M acquisition, (b) waterfall calculation: (i) Series C liquidation preference gets paid first, (ii) then Series B, (iii) then Series A, (iv) remainder to common stock (founders, employees), (c) example: if Series C has 1x preference on $30M investment, gets $30M, remainder $70M split among other classes; (13) Common Stock vs Preferred Stock Economics: (a) common: pro-rata participation in proceeds (simple), no special rights, (b) preferred: liquidation preferences, protective provisions, board representation; (14) Anti-Dilution Provisions: (a) weighted average: if Series B at lower price, Series A price adjusts (dilution shared), (b) full ratchet: Series A price resets to Series B price (worst case), (c) broad-based: all common stock included in adjustment, (d) narrow-based: only other preferred stock included; (15) Future Dilution Scenarios: (a) Series B at higher valuation: founder ownership increases in %, (b) Series B at lower valuation: founder ownership may decrease (valuation resets), (c) Series C: further dilution unless founder maintains pro-rata; (16) Cap Table Tools & Spreadsheets: (a) typical cap table in Excel with columns: shareholder, shares, %, strike price, vesting, (b) automated cap table models (Carta, Pulley, etc.) more common now, (c) cap table audits pre-Series A (investor diligence). Include detailed cap table examples, fully-diluted analysis templates, and dilution scenario modeling for multiple funding rounds.

**PROMPT 054 — Stock Option Agreements & Vesting Schedules**

Prepare comprehensive guidance on drafting stock option agreements and vesting schedules for startup employee equity compensation, including: (1) Option Plan Authority: (a) board must adopt stock option plan (ESOP or similar), (b) plan establishes framework: shares reserved, exercise price determination, vesting schedule, (c) plan document forms authorization for all future option grants, (d) plan typically has 10-year duration, (e) board can amend/extend plan; (2. Stock Option Grant Components: (a) grant document: issued to employee specifying terms, (b) grant specifies: number of shares, exercise price, vesting schedule, grant date, (c) exercise price: typically fair market value (FMV) on grant date, (d) FMV determination: board determination, independent appraisal, formula-based; (3) Option Exercise Price: (a) typically set at FMV on grant date (allows preferential pricing), (b) exercise price fixed at grant (doesn't change with future valuations), (c) below FMV pricing: tax consequences (Section 409A risk), (d) AFV for late-stage startups approaching IPO: investment banker valuation; (4) Vesting Schedule - Standard: (a) 4-year vesting with 1-year cliff: (i) no vesting for first 12 months, (ii) if employee leaves within 12 months, forfeits entire grant, (iii) after cliff, vesting monthly (1/48th per month for remaining 36 months), (b) example: 1M option grant on Jan 1, Year 1, (i) on Dec 31 Year 1: 250K vested (1M/4 years × 1 year), (ii) on Jun 30 Year 2: 375K vested (250K from cliff + 125K from 6 months × 1/48), (iii) on Dec 31 Year 5: 1M vested (all options); (5) Cliff Vesting: (a) cliff = waiting period before any vesting occurs, (b) purpose: protect company from immediate resignations by early employees, (c) typical cliff: 1 year, (d) all-or-nothing at cliff date (either employee stays through cliff and vests 25%, or leaves and forfeits all); (6) Straight Vesting (Alternative): (a) no cliff, vesting from day 1, (b) example: 48-month vesting, vesting 1/48th per month immediately, (c) less common, used for contractors/advisors where cliff inappropriate; (7) Acceleration Provisions: (a) partial acceleration: vesting accelerates upon qualifying event, (b) example: option acceleration if founder leaves (partial vest of remaining unvested), (c) full acceleration: upon acquisition, all options fully vest, (d) single-trigger acceleration: vests upon acquisition regardless of employment termination, (e) double-trigger acceleration: vests upon acquisition + employee termination (more common); (8) Special Acceleration Scenarios: (a) founder termination for cause (fraud, breach): no acceleration, options forfeited, (b) founder death: options fully accelerate (estate inherits vested equity), (c) disability: partial acceleration (remaining vesting at accelerated rate), (d) change of control: often full acceleration (founder retains all equity); (9) Exercise Mechanics: (a) employee exercises options by paying strike price × number of shares, (b) exercise notice given to company, (c) company issues shares or stock certificates upon exercise, (d) exercise typically occurs at departure (employee can exercise all vested options), (e) 90-day exercise window post-employment (if not exercised, options expire); (10) Post-Termination Exercise Window: (a) after termination, employee has limited time to exercise vested options (typically 90 days), (b) rationale: prevent shares held by departed employees, (c) exception: long exercise window for founders (can extend to 5-10 years post-termination), (d) extended window: only for founder vested options, not unvested; (11) Incentive Stock Options (ISOs) vs Non-Qualified Options (NSOs): (a) ISO: if qualifies under Section 422 tax code, allows preferential tax treatment (capital gains vs ordinary income), (b) NSO: standard option, taxed as ordinary income upon exercise, (c) ISO requirements: exercise price = FMV on grant date, no discounts, (d) ISO limits: aggregate value of options that vest in year cannot exceed $100K per employee; (12) Equity Incentives for Consultants/Advisors: (a) consultants receive options similar to employees, (b) typical vesting: 2-year vesting with 6-month cliff (shorter than employees), (c) advisor pools: separate from employee option pool, (d) advisory shares: sometimes granted directly as restricted stock (not options); (13) Section 409A Compliance: (a) tax code protecting options from adverse taxation, (b) requirements: (i) fair market value determination on grant date, (ii) no discounted pricing, (iii) no material modification of terms post-grant, (c) penalties: if non-compliant, accelerated taxation + 20% tax penalty; (14) Taxation Upon Exercise & Sale: (a) upon exercise of NSO: employee pays ordinary income tax on difference between strike price and FMV, (b) upon sale of shares: capital gains tax on appreciation from exercise price to sale price, (c) upon exercise of ISO: no tax on exercise (if qualifies), capital gains only on sale, (d) if ISO doesn't qualify: treated as NSO (higher tax); (15) Option Grant Timing & Board Approvals: (a) option grants typically approved by board quarterly, (b) board certifies FMV for grant price (important for ISO compliance), (c) grant date = date board approves (not signature date), (d) delay between approval and employee notification typical; (16) Restricted Stock vs Options: (a) restricted stock: immediate issuance of shares, with vesting conditions (forfeiture if vesting conditions not met), (b) options: right to purchase shares at exercise price, (c) restricted stock: employee owns shares from grant date (can vote, receive dividends if declared), (d) options: employee owns nothing until exercise, (e) restricted stock advantages: immediate ownership, dividends (if declared), disadvantages: immediate tax on grant (if vesting), (f) restricted stock units (RSUs): right to receive shares on vesting (common in larger companies). Include stock option plan template, option grant agreement template, and vesting schedule calculation examples.

## Founder & Investor Agreements

**PROMPT 055 — Shareholders Agreement (Preferred Stock Investor)**

Prepare comprehensive guidance on shareholders agreements governing relationships between founder and institutional investor in venture-backed startup, including: (1) Parties: (a) founders (original shareholders), (b) Series A investor (preferred stock holder), (c) sometimes includes future Series B/C investors; (2) Governance & Control: (a) board composition (founders, investors, independent directors), (b) board observer rights (investors without seats have observer status), (c) management authority (CEO controls day-to-day, major decisions require board approval), (d) information/reporting rights (monthly financials, quarterly board materials); (3) Protective Provisions: (a) investor veto rights on major decisions: (i) merger/sale of company, (ii) new equity issuance (except option grants under plan), (iii) debt issuance above threshold, (iv) annual budget adoption, (v) acquisition/disposal of material assets, (vi) related party transactions, (vii) change of business, (viii) dividend declarations; (b) voting: if investor holds majority preferred stock, consent not required from founders, but if minority investor, consent required from investor percentage stake; (4) Anti-Dilution: (a) weighted average provision (if Series B at lower price, Series A adjusted), (b) applies to issuances below Series A price, (c) excludes splits, stock dividends, employee option grants; (5) Redemption Rights: (a) uncommon in true venture, but may appear in mezzanine financing, (b) investor right to require company repurchase shares at specific value if no exit after X years, (c) places pressure on founders for exit timeline; (6) Pro-Rata Rights: (a) investor right to participate in future funding rounds, (b) maintains ownership % in Series B, Series C (if wants), (c) investor required to commit capital to participate; (7) Drag-Along & Tag-Along Rights: (a) drag-along: if majority shareholders approve sale, minority must sell on same terms, (b) tag-along: if founder sells to third party, minority investors can co-sell, (c) purpose: ensure all shareholders can exit; (8) Transfer Restrictions: (a) founder shares subject to lock-up (cannot sell for 6-12 months post-IPO), (b) preferred stock transfer requires investor consent (prevent hostile investors), (c) right of first refusal on share sales (company can repurchase before third party), (d) co-sale rights (investor sells with founder); (9) Vesting Schedules: (a) founder shares typically subject to vesting (4-year with 1-year cliff), (b) if founder leaves early, unvested shares forfeited or repurchased at FMV, (c) investor shares not vested (investor commitment is capital, not services); (10) Management Incentives & Bonus Plans: (a) founder may have right to bonus if performance targets met, (b) bonus pools may be subject to investor approval, (c) approval of bonus ensures alignment with investor returns; (11) Voting Agreements: (a) separate from shareholders agreement, specifies voting obligations, (b) founder agrees to vote for: (i) board seats designated by investor, (ii) approval of major transactions, (c) founder forfeits independent board voting in certain matters; (12) Co-Sale Rights: (a) if founder sells shares in secondary transaction, investor has right to co-sell (participate in transaction), (b) ensures investor liquidity if founder liquidity event occurs, (c) typically pro-rata to ownership; (13) Confidentiality & Non-Disparagement: (a) shareholders agree to maintain confidentiality of company information, (b) non-disparagement: shareholders cannot publicly criticize company/management, (c) enforced through specific performance (injunction) or damages; (14) Dispute Resolution: (a) governing law (typically Singapore or US if SAFE/investor in US), (b) arbitration vs litigation, (c) mediation before arbitration/litigation, (d) loser pays attorney fees (discourages frivolous claims); (4) Mandatory Insurances: (a) work injury compensation insurance: mandatory if > 1 employee, covers workplace accidents, (b) employers' liability insurance: covers employer liability for employee injuries, (c) key person insurance: optional but recommended for founders (if key founder dies, insurance funds working capital); (6) Industry-Specific Licenses & Permits: (a) financial services: if conducting insurance, fund management, credit intermediation, requires MAS approval, (b) food & beverage: if selling food, requires license from NEA (National Environment Agency), health permits, (c) telecommunications: if operating telecom services, requires IDA (Infocomm Media Development Authority) license, (d) real estate: if dealing in properties, requires CBRE (Council for Estate Agents) registration, (e) professional services: lawyers, accountants, doctors require professional licenses from respective bodies; (7) Environmental Compliance: (a) if manufacturing: environmental impact assessment may be required, (b) waste disposal: hazardous waste must be properly disposed, (c) pollution control: air/water/noise pollution compliance, (d) environmental impact assessment: required for certain projects (NEA approval); (8. Intellectual Property Protection: (a) trademark registration: register company name/logo with IPOS (Intellectual Property Office Singapore) to prevent infringement, (b) patent registration: if applicable, register patents for technology/processes, (c) copyright: automatic protection, but registration recommended, (d) trade secret protection: maintain confidentiality of proprietary information; (9) Competition & Anti-Trust: (a) Competition Act compliance: ensure no anti-competitive agreements (price-fixing, market sharing), (b) CCCS (Competition Commission Singapore) can fine violators, (c) mergers >SGD 100M may require CCCS approval; (11) Product Liability & Consumer Protection: (a) consumer protection: ensure products/services comply with Consumer Protection Act, (b) product safety: if selling goods, must comply with product safety standards, (c) warranties: offer warranties if products sold; (12) Contract Management: (a) customer terms & conditions: establish clear T&Cs for products/services, (b) supplier agreements: negotiate favorable terms with suppliers, (c) employee agreements: non-compete, confidentiality, IP assignment (addressed above); (13) Cybersecurity & Data Breach: (a) if handling customer data, invest in cybersecurity protections, (b) incident response plan: if data breach occurs, notify affected individuals/PDPC within certain timeline, (c) cyber insurance: optional but recommended; **PROMPT 058 — Anti-Money Laundering (AML) & Know-Your-Customer (KYC) Compliance for Fintechs**

Prepare comprehensive guidance on AML/KYC compliance for fintech startups and payment service providers operating in Singapore, including: (1) Regulatory Framework: (a) Money Laundering and Terrorist Financing (Suppression) Act (MLTA), (b) MAS Notice 635 (anti-money laundering/counter-financing of terrorism), (c) MAS Guidelines on AML/CFT Compliance, (d) Singapore is on FATF Grey List oversight; (2) AML/KYC Obligations: (a) customer due diligence (CDD): identify and verify customer identity before opening account, (b) enhanced due diligence (EDD): for high-risk customers (politically exposed persons, jurisdictions with weak AML regimes), (c) ongoing monitoring: monitor transactions for suspicious patterns, (d) record-keeping: maintain CDD records for 5 years post-transaction; (3. Customer Identification: (a) collect customer information: full legal name, date of birth, residential address, identification documents (passport, NRIC), (b) verify identity: check documents against database (government registry), (c) biometric verification: increasingly required (facial recognition, fingerprint), (d) beneficial ownership: if customer is company, identify ultimate beneficial owner (>25% shareholder); (4) Beneficial Ownership Identification: (a) if customer is company, identify shareholders owning >25%, (b) if chain of companies, trace beneficial ownership to natural persons, (c) requirements: names, addresses, ownership percentages, (d) regulators increasingly focus on beneficial ownership transparency; (5) Risk-Based Approach: (a) AML compliance tailored to risk profile, (b) low-risk customers: simplified KYC (minimal documentation), (c) high-risk customers: enhanced due diligence (extensive verification), (d) risk factors: (i) jurisdictional risk (source country of funds), (ii) customer type (politically exposed person, high-net-worth), (iii) business type (cash-intensive, high-value transactions), (iv) customer behavior (large wire transfers, frequent transactions); (6) Politically Exposed Persons (PEPs): (a) enhanced due diligence for PEPs (government officials, senior management of state-owned enterprises), (b) conduct source of funds investigation (ensure funds not from corruption), (c) ongoing monitoring: any unusual activity requires investigation, (d) families of PEPs also subject to enhanced due diligence; (7) Transaction Monitoring: (a) ongoing monitoring of transaction patterns, (b) suspicious activity indicators: (i) large sudden deposits/withdrawals, (ii) rapid fund movements (layering), (iii) transactions inconsistent with customer profile, (iv) round-amount transfers (unusual pattern), (v) frequent transfers to jurisdictions with weak AML; (c) filing Suspicious Transaction Report (STR) to Financial Intelligence Unit (FIU) Singapore if suspicious activity detected, (d) threshold: typically report for transactions > SGD 250K or patterns showing suspicion; (8) Customer Due Diligence (CDD) Procedures: (a) obtain customer information (application form with personal details), (b) document collection: copies of identification, address verification documents, (c) system verification: run against MAS watchlists, UN/US sanctions lists, (d) approval: compliance officer approves opening after verification, (e) timeline: CDD before customer transacts (not after); (9) Sanctions Compliance: (a) export controls: cannot transact with sanctioned individuals/countries (US OFAC sanctions, UN sanctions), (b) screening: check customer against OFAC list, UN list, other sanctioned entity lists, (c) technology: use automated screening tools (flag potential matches), (d) resolution: if match found, apply risk-based approach (false positive vs real match); (10) Record-Keeping: (a) maintain CDD records for 5 years post-transaction, (b) records: copies of identification, verification evidence, CDD assessment, (c) retention: even if customer closes account, maintain records for 5 years, (d) destruction: after 5 years, securely destroy records (prevent misuse); (12) Compliance Program: (a) appoint AML/KYC compliance officer, (b) establish written AML policies and procedures, (c) provide staff training (annual AML/KYC training mandatory), (d) conduct internal audits (verify compliance), (e) documentation: maintain compliance files; (13) Penalties for Violations: (a) civil penalties: fines up to SGD 1M + 5% of transaction value, (b) criminal penalties: imprisonment up to 7 years, (c) business license revocation, (d) reputational damage; (14) Technology Tools for AML/KYC: (a) customer identification verification platforms (ID matching, biometric verification), (b) sanctions screening tools (automated watchlist matching), (c) transaction monitoring software (flags suspicious patterns), (d) data analytics: machine learning models to detect anomalies; (15) Third-Party Service Providers: (a) outsourcing to third-party KYC providers common (e.g., specialist AML firms), (b) responsibility: company remains liable even if outsourced, (c) diligence: ensure third-party meets regulatory standards, (d) agreements: written contracts with SLAs for KYC turnaround; (16) Practical Implementation: (a) onboarding process: CDD completed before account opening, (b) application form: comprehensive, captures required information, (c) document collection: secure upload of identification, (d) verification timeline: target 24-48 hours (balancing compliance vs customer experience), (e) threshold monitoring: automated alerts for large transactions.
