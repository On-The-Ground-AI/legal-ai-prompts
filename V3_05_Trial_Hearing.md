## OPENING & CLOSING

**PROMPT 036 — Opening Statement (Narrative, Non-Argumentative)**

Prepare a draft opening statement for [SPECIFY PARTY: plaintiff or defendant] in [DESCRIBE CASE], to be delivered at the commencement of trial before the [SPECIFY JUDGE/JURY: judge, jury, panel of arbitrators].

Please draft:
- Purpose and structure:
  - An opening statement is a narrative account of the facts, not an argument about the law
  - It is designed to help the judge/jury understand the case from that party's perspective
  - It should be clear, engaging, and accessible (not legal jargon)
  - It should follow the likely evidentiary flow (how the evidence will be presented at trial)

- Opening section (1-2 minutes):
  - Greeting and context: "Your Honour, I represent [PARTY NAME]. This case concerns [BRIEF SUMMARY]."
  - The story: introduce the parties, the relationship, and the key facts in a narrative way
  - Theme: establish a theme or overarching narrative that ties the case together (e.g., "This is a case about a broken promise", "This is a case about negligent advice that cost my client their business")

- Chronological narrative (main body, 10-15 minutes):
  - Follow the evidence chronologically
  - For each major event or period, explain: what happened, who was involved, what was said (key communications), what was understood at the time
  - Link to the parties' positions: how does this evidence support your client's case?
  - Highlight key documents and communications (you will refer the judge to them during the trial)
  - Explain causation: how did the events lead to the loss/harm your client suffered

- Key facts to emphasize:
  - Facts that are undisputed (will save time if acknowledged early)
  - Facts that strongly support your client's case
  - Facts that respond to the other side's likely defense or counter-claim
  - Admissions or clear evidence that supports your client

- Anticipated challenges and counter-arguments:
  - Acknowledge areas where the other side will dispute the facts (preview how you will respond)
  - Explain why the other side's version is inconsistent with the documents or evidence
  - Explain weaknesses in the other side's case (but avoid being argumentative; present facts that the judge will see)

- Loss and damage (if applicable):
  - Explain what harm the plaintiff suffered
  - Explain how the loss quantified (without detailed argument, just explanation of the claim)
  - Preview the evidence (witnesses, documents, expert evidence) that will establish the loss

- Conclusion (2-3 minutes):
  - Restate the theme
  - Explain what the evidence will show when the trial concludes
  - Request the relief sought (judgment for [AMOUNT], declaratory relief, injunctive relief, etc.)
  - Thank the judge/jury

- Tone and presentation:
  - Speak as if you are explaining the case to an intelligent person, not a lawyer
  - Use simple, clear language (avoid jargon)
  - Tell the story in a logical, engaging way (not just a list of facts)
  - Use visual aids if helpful (chronology, bundle references, diagrams)
  - Speak with confidence and conviction, but not arrogance
  - Make appropriate eye contact with the judge/jury

- What not to do:
  - Do not argue the law (that comes in closing submissions)
  - Do not make speeches or emotive appeals (stick to facts)
  - Do not refer to evidence that you don't expect to introduce at trial
  - Do not make promises about evidence that you may not be able to prove
  - Do not attack the other party or their evidence (focus on your positive case)
  - Do not go over time (courts typically allow 20-30 minutes for opening; practice timing)

- Visual aids:
  - Consider using a chronology (helpful for the judge to track events)
  - Consider a simplified evidence matrix (shows which evidence supports which facts)
  - Consider photos, diagrams, or videos (if relevant and helpful)
  - Ensure visual aids are professional and clear

- References to the bundle:
  - As you narrate events, reference the specific bundle pages/exhibits that evidence the facts
  - This helps the judge follow along with the actual documents
  - Example: "As shown in Exhibit A (page 45), the contract clearly states [TERM]"

- Witness examination order:
  - Briefly preview the order in which witnesses will be called
  - Explain why this order makes sense (helps the judge anticipate the evidentiary flow)

- Anticipated judicial questions:
  - Anticipate what questions the judge might ask during opening
  - Be prepared to answer questions but stay focused on your narrative
  - If asked about contested facts, acknowledge the dispute and preview your evidence

Include a sample opening statement for a typical dispute (e.g., breach of contract, personal injury, professional negligence) to illustrate best practices. Discuss the importance of opening statements in setting the narrative and helping the judge understand the case.

Context: When preparing to deliver an opening statement at trial or hearing and need to structure a compelling, clear narrative.

**PROMPT 037 — Closing Submissions (Argument, Law & Facts)**

Prepare comprehensive closing submissions for [SPECIFY PARTY: plaintiff or defendant] in [DESCRIBE CASE], to be delivered at the conclusion of trial. The submissions should address all major issues in dispute and tie together the evidence presented during the trial.

Please draft:
- Structure:
  - Introduction: thank the judge, introduce myself and the party I represent, brief overview of what the submissions will cover
  - Law: state the applicable law, principles, and tests
  - Facts: apply the law to the facts as established by the evidence at trial
  - Conclusion: synthesis, request for relief

- Legal framework:
  - Identify the cause of action (contract law, tort law, statutory claim, equitable claim)
  - State the key legal principles (tests, elements, factors to be considered)
  - Cite relevant authorities (case law, statutes)
  - Explain how the law applies to this case

- Factual analysis (main body of submissions):
  - For each key issue, explain:
    - The issue: what must be established? (what are the elements?)
    - The evidence: what evidence was presented at trial that addresses this issue?
    - Application: how does the evidence satisfy the legal test?
    - Credibility: was the evidence reliable, consistent, supported by documents?
  - Address areas of dispute:
    - The defendant/plaintiff's position was [X]. This is contradicted by [EVIDENCE]. I submit [Y] is the correct version because [REASONS].
  - Address weaknesses in your own case:
    - Acknowledge areas where the evidence is weak or contested
    - Explain why, despite any weakness, the legal test is still met
    - Don't ignore weaknesses; address them head-on (judges respect this more than evasion)

- Key themes:
  - Identify overarching themes or narratives that tie the evidence together
  - Refer back to these themes throughout the submissions
  - Example: "Throughout this case, the pattern is clear: the defendant made promises they never intended to keep. The evidence will show [EXAMPLES]"

- Document analysis:
  - Refer to specific documents from the bundle
  - Explain what each key document shows or proves
  - Address the authenticity and reliability of documents
  - Explain how documents support your client's case

- Witness credibility:
  - For key witnesses, assess their credibility (did they come across as truthful and reliable, were there inconsistencies, did they have bias or interest)
  - Explain which witnesses were credible and reliable
  - Explain which witnesses were not credible (inconsistencies, bias, motivation to lie, poor memory)
  - Explain how the witness evidence affects the outcome (which witnesses' evidence was accepted and why)

- Expert evidence:
  - Address any expert evidence presented
  - Assess the expert's qualifications and methodology
  - Apply the expert evidence to the issues
  - If there are competing experts, explain why one expert's opinion is more reliable than the other's

- Burden and standard of proof:
  - Confirm the standard of proof (balance of probabilities in civil cases)
  - Explain that the judge must assess which party's version is more likely to be true
  - Explain that credible evidence and documents will outweigh speculation or assertion

- Addressing the other party's case:
  - Anticipate the other party's main arguments
  - Explain why those arguments are not supported by the evidence
  - Explain what evidence contradicts the other party's case
  - Avoid simply attacking the other party (focus on showing why the law and evidence support your client)

- Causation (if relevant):
  - Explain how the defendant's breach/conduct caused the plaintiff's loss
  - Explain the chain of causation with reference to the evidence
  - Address any intervening events or alternative causes the defendant might rely on

- Quantification (if relevant):
  - Explain how the damages are calculated
  - Address the expert evidence on damages (if any)
  - Explain why the claimed quantum is reasonable and supported by evidence

- Relief sought:
  - Be clear about what relief is being sought (judgment for [AMOUNT], declaratory relief, injunction, etc.)
  - Explain why this relief is appropriate (it flows from the breaches/wrongs established)
  - Request any ancillary relief (interest, costs, etc.)

- Conclusion (5-10 minutes):
  - Synthesize the key points
  - Summarize why the law and evidence support your client's case
  - Summarize why the other side's case should be rejected
  - Explicitly state the relief sought
  - Thank the judge and acknowledge the gravity of the decision

- Tone and presentation:
  - Be respectful to the judge and the other party
  - Use measured language; avoid inflammatory accusations
  - Refer to the evidence and law; don't make appeals to emotion
  - Speak with confidence, based on the strength of your case
  - Maintain clarity and logical flow

- Common pitfalls:
  - Repetition: avoid repeating the same points multiple times (judges lose patience)
  - Vagueness: be specific about what evidence proves what (cite the bundle page)
  - Avoiding weakness: address weaknesses in your case head-on rather than ignoring them
  - Over-arguing: don't over-state the strength of your evidence; let it speak for itself
  - Personal attacks: avoid attacking the other party's counsel or character (focus on the evidence)

- Handling difficult questions:
  - If the judge asks difficult questions, answer them directly and honestly
  - Use difficult questions as an opportunity to reinforce key points
  - Don't be defensive; acknowledge if the judge has identified a genuine weakness

Include a detailed sample closing submission for a typical case (e.g., breach of contract claim with damages issues) covering multiple disputed issues, credibility assessments, and application of law to facts.

Context: When preparing final closing submissions after all evidence has been presented at trial.
Difficulty: Advanced

Follow-up: PROMPT 036 (Opening Statement) or PROMPT 040 (Skeleton Argument)

**PROMPT 038 — Skeleton Argument (Concise Written Submissions for Hearing)**

Prepare a skeleton argument (concise written submissions) for [DESCRIBE MATTER] to be filed and relied upon in [SPECIFY PROCEEDING: interlocutory hearing, directions hearing, trial, appeal]. The skeleton should be [SPECIFY LENGTH: typically 5-10 pages for an interlocutory matter, 15-20 pages for trial, 20-30 pages for appeal].

Please draft:
- Format:
  - Numbered paragraphs (1, 2, 3... makes referencing easy)
  - Clear headings for each section
  - Short, punchy paragraphs (avoid dense blocks of text)
  - Simple, clear language (judges will read this quickly)
  - Page numbers at the bottom

- Opening section (Paras 1-3):
  - Para 1: Identify the party represented and briefly state the issue(s)
  - Para 2: State what relief is sought (be specific and concrete)
  - Para 3: Brief narrative of the key facts (1-2 paragraphs)

- Law section (Paras 4-X):
  - For each legal issue, state:
    - The applicable legal principle or test (cite the relevant case law or statute)
    - How the principle applies to the facts
    - Conclusion on this point
  - Use headings to organize by issue
  - Reference authorities (cite cases, statutes, rules)
  - Be concise: explain the law in straightforward terms

- Application section (Paras X+1 to end-2):
  - Apply the law to the facts
  - For each element of the legal test, explain how the facts satisfy (or do not satisfy) that element
  - Cite specific evidence (bundle pages, witness evidence, documents)
  - Address the other party's arguments: explain why they are not correct/not supported by the law or evidence

- Prayer/conclusion (final para):
  - Summarize the main point
  - State explicitly what relief is sought
  - Example: "For the reasons above, I submit the court should [GRANT JUDGMENT / STRIKE OUT THE CLAIM / ORDER X / etc.]"

- Key features of an effective skeleton argument:
  - Brevity: say what you need to say and stop (don't over-elaborate)
  - Clarity: write in a way that's easy to follow (clear headings, logical flow)
  - Specificity: refer to specific evidence and law (not vague generalities)
  - Honesty: acknowledge weaknesses in your case (courts respect this)
  - Accessibility: use simple language, define technical terms

- What not to do:
  - Don't over-cite authorities (one or two key cases per point is usually sufficient)
  - Don't repeat arguments: say it once, clearly, and move on
  - Don't include detailed evidence: refer to evidence, don't reproduce it
  - Don't make arguments in footnotes (main submissions should be in the body)
  - Don't use emotional language or appeals (stick to law and evidence)

- Addressing the other side:
  - Anticipate the other party's main arguments and address them
  - Explain why their arguments are wrong (with reference to law and evidence)
  - Use "However" or "By contrast" to highlight differences with the other side's position
  - Don't simply assert that the other side is wrong; explain why with reference to the law

- Cross-references:
  - Reference specific bundle pages for evidence: "as shown in the email of 15 June 2023 (Bundle p. 127)"
  - Reference specific case law by page number if possible: "per Lord X at p. YZ"
  - Help the reader find the evidence/authorities quickly

- Length discipline:
  - For a 5-page skeleton: 25-50 words per paragraph (tight and focused)
  - For a 10-page skeleton: 50-100 words per paragraph (more room for detail)
  - For a 20-page skeleton: 75-150 words per paragraph (narrative and detailed application)

- Tone:
  - Professional and respectful
  - Confident, but not arrogant
  - Honest and candid
  - Avoid inflammatory language

Include detailed sample skeleton arguments for different contexts: (a) an interlocutory application (e.g., striking out); (b) trial submissions on a key issue; (c) appeal submissions. Discuss the utility of skeleton arguments in helping judges understand the case quickly and fairly.

Context: When preparing written submissions for a hearing or trial that must be concise and clear, with reference to law and evidence.
Difficulty: Advanced
Best AI tool: Claude
Follow-up: PROMPT 037 (Closing Submissions) or PROMPT 036 (Opening Statement)

## TRIAL PROCEDURES & DOCUMENTS

**PROMPT 039 — Cross-Examination Checklist & Question Bank**

Prepare a comprehensive cross-examination checklist and question bank for cross-examining [WITNESS NAME / EXPERT NAME] in [DESCRIBE MATTER]. The witness will testify about [SPECIFY TOPICS], and the cross-examination should target [SPECIFY GOALS: discrediting the witness, establishing alternative facts, testing the reliability of expert opinion, securing admissions].

Please prepare:
- Pre-cross-examination strategy:
  - What is the goal of cross-examining this witness? (discredit, obtain admissions, establish alternative narrative, test expert reliability)
  - What are the key points you need to establish? (list 5-10 critical facts or admissions)
  - What are the vulnerabilities in the witness's evidence? (gaps, inconsistencies, bias, poor memory, lack of direct knowledge)
  - What documents will you rely on to challenge the witness? (identify 3-5 key documents to put to the witness)
  - What tone should you adopt? (depends on the witness's credibility and how they are likely to respond; aggressive vs. measured)

- Witness assessment:
  - Credibility level (assess whether the witness is credible or vulnerable to challenge)
  - Confidence level (is the witness confident in their evidence or uncertain?)
  - Hostility level (is the witness hostile or cooperative?)
  - Knowledge level (does the witness have direct knowledge or is much of their evidence hearsay?)
  - Bias level (is the witness biased toward the party that called them?)

- Cross-examination principles:
  - Ask leading questions (put a proposition to the witness, don't ask open-ended questions)
  - Ask one thing at a time (not compound questions)
  - Ask questions you know the answer to (avoid asking questions you don't know the answer to)
  - Listen to the answer (be flexible if the answer is unexpected)
  - Don't argue with the witness (challenge with documents/questions, not by arguing)
  - Control the witness (leading questions should guide the witness to agreeing with your propositions)

- Question bank (organized by topic):

  Topic 1: Credibility/Bias
  - "You were paid by [PARTY] to provide this evidence, correct?"
  - "Your company has a business relationship with [PARTY], doesn't it?"
  - "You stand to benefit financially if [PARTY] wins this case, correct?"
  - "You have already given evidence in other cases on behalf of [PARTY], haven't you?"

  Topic 2: Direct Knowledge
  - "You were not present when [KEY EVENT] occurred, were you?"
  - "Your knowledge of [FACT] is based entirely on what [PERSON] told you, correct?"
  - "You did not personally observe [EVENT], did you?"
  - "You are relying on memory of an event that happened [X YEARS/MONTHS AGO]?"

  Topic 3: Document Contradictions
  - "The email dated [DATE] shows that [FACT], doesn't it?" (produce document)
  - "In your AEIC you said [X], but the contemporaneous record shows [Y], correct?"
  - "This contract [EXHIBIT] clearly states [TERM], does it not?"
  - "Your earlier statement was [X], but the document shows [Y], how do you explain this?"

  Topic 4: Inconsistency/Contradiction
  - "In your first statement you said [X], but in your AEIC you said [Y]. Which is correct?"
  - "You previously told [PERSON] that [X], but now you're saying [Y]. Which version is true?"
  - "These two parts of your evidence are contradictory, are they not?"

  Topic 5: Alternative Narrative
  - "Isn't it possible that [ALTERNATIVE SCENARIO] occurred?"
  - "The evidence is also consistent with [YOUR VERSION], isn't it?"
  - "You cannot rule out [ALTERNATIVE EXPLANATION], can you?"

  Topic 6: Expert Reliability (if cross-examining expert)
  - "Your methodology was [METHOD]. Is that the standard approach used by other experts in your field?"
  - "You didn't have access to [KEY DATA/INFORMATION], did you?"
  - "If [ASSUMPTION] is incorrect, would that affect your conclusion?"
  - "There are other qualified experts who disagree with your conclusion, aren't there?"
  - "Your opinion is based partly on [HEARSAY/ASSUMPTION], is that correct?"

  Topic 7: Securing Admissions
  - "So you agree that [FACT A] and [FACT B] are both true?"
  - "Following from that, the only explanation is [YOUR CONCLUSION], isn't it?"
  - "You cannot say that [YOUR POSITION] is wrong, can you?"

- Document selection:
  - Identify 3-5 key documents that contradict or support your case
  - Prepare to put these documents to the witness in a logical order
  - Have documents bookmarked or easily accessible
  - Plan how you will use each document (what admission or concession do you want)

- Tactical approach:
  - Start with areas where the witness is likely to agree (establish rapport and build momentum)
  - Progress to more difficult areas (once the witness is in the habit of agreeing)
  - Use documents strategically (not all at once, but in a logical sequence)
  - Concede areas where you are clearly wrong (preserves credibility)
  - Don't over-reach (stop while you're winning)

- Difficult witness responses:
  - "I don't recall" — have documents to refresh memory
  - "I don't know" — establish whether they should know, move on if not
  - "I disagree" — explain why with a document or logical deduction
  - Evasive answers — repeat the question, use documents to tie down the answer
  - Hostile witness — stay measured, don't react emotionally, use documents to control

- Time management:
  - Estimate how long the cross-examination should take (aim for 30-60 minutes for most fact witnesses, 60-90 minutes for experts)
  - Prioritize the most important questions
  - Don't over-cross-examine (many short questions are better than a few long ones)
  - Watch the judge's reaction (if losing the judge, move on)

- Ending the cross-examination:
  - Finish strong: make your last question a powerful one that supports your case
  - Don't ask trailing questions that allow the witness to rehabilitate their evidence
  - Once you've got the admission you want, stop

Include detailed sample cross-examination question banks for different types of witnesses (fact witness, expert engineer, financial expert, etc.) to illustrate best practices.

Context: When preparing to cross-examine a witness at trial and need to structure a challenging but effective examination.
Difficulty: Advanced

**PROMPT 040 — Costs Submissions (Post-Trial Cost Determination)**

Prepare detailed costs submissions for [SPECIFY PARTY: party that won / party that was successful on some issues] following [DESCRIBE OUTCOME: judgment for plaintiff on all counts, judgment for defendant on main claim but plaintiff successful on counterclaim, mixed result].

Please draft:
- Costs principles (general):
  - General rule: costs follow the event (the unsuccessful party pays the successful party's costs)
  - Discretionary variation: court can depart from the general rule based on: (1) partial success (different outcomes on different issues); (2) conduct (unreasonable behavior, delay, failure to settle); (3) complexity (did the losing party raise complex issues requiring significant costs?); (4) proportionality

- Successful party submissions:
  - Summarize the outcome: "My client succeeded on all issues" or "My client succeeded on the main claim but failed on the defendant's counterclaim"
  - Explain why costs should follow the event: party was successful on the substantive merits
  - Address any areas of partial failure: if unsuccessful on some counts, explain why costs should not be apportioned
  - Address the other party's conduct: if the other party behaved unreasonably or made unrealistic settlement demands, argue for enhanced costs (indemnity basis, uplift)
  - Quantum: provide estimate or actual costs incurred (solicitor time, counsel fees, expert fees, court fees, service costs)
  - Request: "Costs to be paid by the defendant to the plaintiff on a party-and-party basis, assessed if not agreed" OR "Costs on an indemnity basis"

- Unsuccessful party submissions:
  - Acknowledge the outcome: "My client was unsuccessful on this issue"
  - Explain any mitigating factors: (1) the law was unclear; (2) the other party's case was sophisticated and required serious consideration; (3) the issue was complex; (4) the costs were proportionate to the importance
  - Identify areas where your client was successful: if there were any issues where your client prevailed, highlight them and argue for no order as to costs on those issues
  - Highlight good conduct: if your client conducted the case reasonably or made reasonable settlement offers, highlight this as a reason for a lower costs order
  - Request: "No order as to costs on this issue" OR "Costs in the cause" OR "The costs of this application be shared equally"

- Apportionment of costs:
  - If there are multiple issues with different outcomes, costs may be apportioned by issue
  - Example: "The plaintiff succeeded on issues 1-3 and the defendant succeeded on issue 4. Costs should be apportioned 75% to the plaintiff (3/4 success rate) and 25% to the defendant (1/4 success rate). However, the primary issue was issue 1, which the plaintiff succeeded on; the defendant's success on issue 4 was minor. Therefore, I submit the plaintiff should recover the bulk of costs."

- Conduct issues:
  - Settlement offers: "The plaintiff made a settlement offer of $[X] on [DATE], which was reasonable. The defendant rejected it without justification and proceeded to trial, ultimately recovering less than the offer. Costs should be awarded on an indemnity basis as a consequence of unreasonable rejection of a settlement offer."
  - Delay or breach of orders: "The defendant breached the court's order to disclose documents on [DATE]. This caused significant delay and additional costs. Costs should include the costs of dealing with this breach."
  - Failure to mitigate: "The defendant's failure to take reasonable steps to [MITIGATE/RESOLVE] forced the plaintiff to incur additional costs. Costs on an indemnity basis are appropriate."

- Quantum assessment:
  - Provide a breakdown of costs: solicitor time (hourly rate × hours), counsel fees (brief fee + trial fee), expert fees, court fees, service/disbursement costs
  - For estimated costs: provide a conservative estimate based on industry standards
  - For assessed costs: provide detailed time records and cost breakdowns
  - Proportionality check: are the costs claimed proportionate to the complexity and importance of the case?
  - Comparison: compare to similar cases (if available)

- Specific cost categories:
  - Pre-trial: advice, correspondence, disclosure, interim applications
  - Trial preparation: preparation time, witness management, bundle preparation, trial brief
  - Trial: attendance at trial, cross-examination, submissions, time spent waiting
  - Post-trial: costs submissions, assessment, implementation of judgment
  - Ancillary: court fees, service costs, expert fees, travel, etc.

- Basis of costs:
  - Party-and-party costs: standard costs awarded in litigation; usually covers 60-70% of actual costs incurred
  - Indemnity costs: higher basis, awarded where there has been unreasonable conduct; covers a higher proportion of actual costs (80-90%)
  - Fixed costs: court awards a fixed sum rather than assessed costs (faster, more certain)

- Cost of specific applications:
  - If the trial included interlocutory applications (summary judgment, striking out, etc.), address costs of those applications
  - Generally, costs of applications follow the event (unsuccessful party pays for the application)
  - If an application was partially successful, costs may be reserved or apportioned

- Settlement negotiations and costs:
  - If the case should have been settled earlier and wasn't, this may affect costs
  - If a party made a reasonable settlement offer that was rejected, this is a factor in costs
  - "My client offered settlement at [AMOUNT/TERMS] on [DATE]. The defendant rejected this offer unreasonably and continued to trial, ultimately obtaining [LESS/WORSE OUTCOME]. This demonstrates the unreasonableness of the defendant's rejection and justifies enhanced costs."

- Final prayer:
  - "For the above reasons, I submit the court should order that [DEFENDANT] pay the costs of the [TRIAL / APPLICATION] incurred by [PLAINTIFF] on a [PARTY-AND-PARTY / INDEMNITY] basis, to be assessed if not agreed by [DATE]."

Include detailed sample costs submissions for different scenarios: (a) complete success by one party; (b) mixed success; (c) partial success where costs apportionment is required.

Context: When preparing written or oral submissions regarding costs following trial or a major application.
Difficulty: Intermediate
Best AI tool: Claude
Follow-up: PROMPT 037 (Closing Submissions) or PROMPT 041 (Bundle Preparation)

**PROMPT 041 — Bundle Preparation & Organization (Final Trial Bundle)**

Prepare the final evidence bundle for trial in [DESCRIBE CASE], organizing all documents, exhibits, and supporting materials in a format accessible to the court and counsel.

Please prepare:
- Bundle structure and organization:
  - Overall organization approach (chronological, by issue, by party, by document type)
  - Division into volumes (if the bundle is very large, divide into manageable volumes: Volume 1 = [DATES/ISSUES], Volume 2 = [DATES/ISSUES])
  - Tab dividers for major sections and sub-sections
  - Consistent, sequential page numbering throughout (usually at bottom right)
  - Running header with volume number and section to help locate items

- Index and table of contents:
  - Create a comprehensive index of all documents in the bundle
  - Format: Document number, Date, Description (1 line), Page numbers
  - Group by section to match the bundle organization
  - Ensure all exhibits referenced in AEICs/statements are included and properly indexed

- Document categories (typical structure):
  - Section A: Contracts & key agreements (main contract, variations, side letters)
  - Section B: Correspondence between parties (chronologically, grouped by topic if helpful)
  - Section C: Internal communications (emails within each party's organization)
  - Section D: Third-party communications (with customers, suppliers, advisors)
  - Section E: Financial documents (invoices, payment records, accounting records)
  - Section F: Expert reports
  - Section G: Photographs, diagrams, plans
  - Section H: Other evidence

- Page numbering:
  - Each page in the bundle should have a unique page number
  - Page number should appear at the bottom right of each page (consistent location)
  - Ensure page numbers are sequential with no gaps or duplicates
  - For multi-page documents (emails with attachments, reports with appendices), number every page

- Exhibits and references:
  - Each exhibit should be clearly labeled: "Exhibit A", "Exhibit B", "Exhibit C", etc. (or "Exhibit P1", "Exhibit P2" for plaintiff, "Exhibit D1" for defendant)
  - Ensure the exhibits match the references in the AEIC/statements
  - If an AEIC references "Exhibit A" but that exhibit is not in the bundle, there is a problem; correct before trial
  - Consider "super-exhibits" (collections of related documents grouped together with a single exhibit letter)

- Document authenticity:
  - Where possible, use original documents (not copies)
  - If originals are not available, use certified copies
  - Ensure copies are clear and legible (if a copy is illegible, it is unhelpful)
  - If a document is in a foreign language, include a certified translation

- Highlighting and annotations:
  - Highlight key passages (but sparingly — over-highlighting reduces readability)
  - Use different colors for different parties (plaintiff's highlights in one color, defendant's in another, if multiple bundles)
  - Annotations should be minimal and professional (avoid sarcastic or inflammatory comments)
  - Consider margin notes pointing to significance ("Note: D admits breach in this sentence")

- Redaction:
  - Redact confidential or sensitive information if appropriate and necessary (but this should be rare)
  - Clearly mark redactions as such (usually by shading or black marker)
  - Document the reason for each redaction (on the privilege log or confidentiality schedule)
  - Avoid over-redacting; only redact what is truly sensitive

- Special formats:
  - Photographs: ensure they are clear, properly numbered, and referenced in a schedule
  - Diagrams/plans: ensure they are legible, oriented correctly, and annotated if helpful
  - Videos/audio: provide transcripts and timestamps in the written bundle; provide the video/audio separately on USB if needed
  - Electronic documents: if emails or electronic records are being produced, provide them in a searchable PDF format with index/bookmarks

- Agreed bundle:
  - In many cases, the parties agree on a bundle (saving costs and ensuring consistency)
  - The bundle is typically prepared by the plaintiff's solicitors and circulated to the defendant's solicitors
  - Both parties sign off, confirming the documents are authentic and complete
  - An agreed bundle is then filed with the court

- Court filing:
  - Prepare the bundle according to court rules (layout, margin requirements, page numbering, etc.)
  - Ensure multiple copies are printed if required (for the judge, defendant's counsel, court file)
  - Bind the bundle professionally (comb binding, ring binding, or wire binding; not loose papers)
  - Include a transmittal letter indicating the number of bundles, pages, and organization

- Version control:
  - If the bundle is updated or corrected, ensure a new version is clearly marked
  - Document version number and date of changes
  - Circulate the revised version to all parties
  - Don't use outdated bundles at trial

- Electronic bundle:
  - Increasingly, bundles are provided in electronic format (PDF)
  - Ensure the PDF is properly formatted and navigable (bookmarks, hyperlinks to index)
  - Test the electronic bundle to ensure hyperlinks work and documents are searchable
  - Provide the bundle on USB or cloud link, with clear instructions for access
  - Ensure the electronic bundle is compatible with the court's system

- Bundle preparation checklist:
  - All AEICs are reviewed and all exhibits referenced are located
  - All exhibits are clearly labeled and indexed
  - Page numbering is sequential and correct
  - Table of contents is accurate and complete
  - Key documents are highlighted (appropriately, not excessively)
  - The bundle is professionally bound and presented
  - Multiple copies are prepared (as required)
  - A copy is delivered to the other party for agreement
  - The bundle is filed with the court by the required deadline

- Common mistakes to avoid:
  - Missing exhibits: ensure every document referenced in pleadings/AEICs is actually in the bundle
  - Incorrect page numbering: go through and verify page numbers are sequential
  - Illegible copies: ensure all documents can be read clearly
  - Poor organization: use clear headings and indexing so items can be found quickly
  - Inconsistent formatting: maintain consistent margins, headers, fonts
  - Unsigned or unverified documents: ensure key documents are signed originals or certified copies
  - Too much highlighting: highlighting should be used sparingly to highlight key passages, not entire paragraphs

**PROMPT 042 — Trial Chronology & Timeline (for Presentation)**

Prepare a presentation chronology for use at trial in [DESCRIBE CASE], setting out the sequence of key events in a format easy for the judge to follow and reference during the trial.

Please prepare:
- Format options:
  - Table format: dates in first column, events in second column, reference to bundle page numbers in third column
  - Timeline format: visual representation showing events along a timeline with key dates and events marked
  - Narrative chronology: events told in story form, with dates clearly marked

- Content:
  - Key events that are relevant to the case
  - Significant communications or decisions
  - Contract performance or breaches
  - Transactions or payments
  - Meetings, inspections, or observations
  - Changes in circumstances that affected the parties

- Dates and timing:
  - Use consistent date format (DD/MM/YYYY or MM/DD/YYYY, depending on jurisdiction)
  - Include times if significant (e.g., phone calls, urgent communications)
  - Note approximate dates if exact dates are unknown ("approximately [DATE]")
  - Align with the agreed bundle page numbers for easy reference

- Level of detail:
  - Include sufficient detail to tell the story but not so much as to overwhelm
  - Use plain language (not legal jargon)
  - Link events together (show causal relationships or dependencies)
  - Highlight critical turning points or decision points

- Color coding / highlighting:
  - If using visual timeline, consider color-coding by party or issue (e.g., red for plaintiff's actions, blue for defendant's actions)
  - Use visual cues to emphasize key events (stars, bold text, highlighting)
  - Maintain professional appearance (not too busy or distracting)

- Reference to documents:
  - Reference specific bundle pages for each event
  - Example: "15 June 2023 — Plaintiff sends email requesting payment (Bundle p. 127)"
  - This allows the judge to quickly look up the evidence for each event

- Multiple versions:
  - Prepare different chronologies for different purposes: (a) trial presentation chronology; (b) working chronology for counsel; (c) simplified chronology for handout
  - Trial presentation chronology should be clear and accessible to a non-expert
  - Working chronology can be more detailed

- Disputed events:
  - If the parties dispute certain dates or events, note this
  - Example: "15 June 2023 — Alleged breach (Plaintiff says deadline was 15 June; Defendant says deadline was extended to 20 June — see Exhibit [X])"
  - Include both versions if there is genuine dispute

- Visual presentation:
  - If using a timeline diagram, ensure it is professionally designed and legible
  - Use clear, consistent formatting
  - Ensure the timeline can be easily presented to the judge (print size, projection quality)
  - Consider a vertical timeline (events flowing from top to bottom) or horizontal timeline (left to right) — vertical is often clearer for complex cases

- Length and scope:
  - Decide whether to include every event or only the key 20-30 events
  - Including too many events clutters the timeline; being too selective may miss important context
  - Generally, aim for 20-50 events depending on the complexity of the case

- Trial use:
  - Have multiple copies printed for the judge, all counsel, and the court
  - Reference the chronology during opening statement ("As shown in the trial chronology...")
  - Use the chronology during witness examination to keep the narrative organized
  - Refer to the chronology in closing submissions to remind the judge of the sequence of events

- Digital version:
  - Prepare a digital version (PowerPoint, PDF) if the trial is using digital presentation
  - Ensure the timeline is easy to navigate and reference
  - Test the technology to ensure it works smoothly at trial
